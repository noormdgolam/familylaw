Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in Florida

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Child custody disputes in Florida are governed by the Florida State Statutes, specifically Chapter 61, which outlines the procedures and standards for determining the best interests of the child. As a family law attorney in Florida, it is essential to understand the intricacies of Florida's child custody laws to effectively represent clients and advocate for their rights.

Florida's Child Custody Laws

Florida law recognizes two primary types of child custody arrangements: sole custody and joint custody. Sole custody grants one parent the exclusive right to make decisions regarding the child's welfare, while joint custody allows both parents to share decision-making authority. Florida Statutes Section 61.13(2)(a)1. and 2. outline the factors that courts consider when deciding on a custody arrangement:

Sole Custody:

  • One parent is deemed unfit or incapable of providing adequate care for the child.
  • The child's physical, emotional, or mental well-being is at risk with one parent.
  • The parents live in separate households, and co-parenting is not feasible.

Joint Custody:

  • The parents are able to cooperate and communicate effectively regarding the child's care.
  • The child's best interests are served by joint decision-making and shared responsibilities.
  • The parents are willing and able to work together to provide a stable environment.

Factors Determining the Best Interests of the Child

Florida courts consider the following factors when determining the best interests of the child, as outlined in Florida Statutes Section 61.13(3):

Factor Description
Moral fitness of the parents The court considers the moral character and behavior of each parent, including any history of abuse, neglect, or substance abuse.
Parents' ability to provide a stable environment The court evaluates the parents' ability to provide a stable, loving, and nurturing environment for the child.
Parents' ability to meet the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs The court considers the parents' ability to provide adequate food, shelter, clothing, education, and medical care for the child.
Parents' ability to cooperate and communicate effectively The court evaluates the parents' ability to work together, communicate effectively, and make joint decisions regarding the child's care.
Child's preference (if old enough to express a preference) The court considers the child's wishes, if they are old enough to express a preference and have a mature understanding of the situation.
History of domestic violence or abuse The court considers any history of domestic violence or abuse between the parents, as well as any impact on the child.
Parents' ability to provide a stable residence and lifestyle The court evaluates the parents' ability to provide a stable residence, a regular routine, and a stable lifestyle for the child.
Parents' ability to provide a loving and nurturing relationship The court considers the parents' ability to provide a loving, nurturing, and supportive relationship with the child.

Legal Proceedings for Child Custody in Florida

Child custody disputes in Florida typically follow the following legal proceedings:

Initial Petition:

  • A parent files a petition for child custody with the court, requesting a specific type of custody arrangement.
  • The other parent is served with notice of the petition and given an opportunity to respond.

Temporary Custody Hearing:

  • A temporary custody hearing is held to determine a temporary custody arrangement until a final decision is made.
  • The court considers the best interests of the child and may award temporary custody to one parent.

Discovery and Depositions:

  • Both parties engage in discovery, exchanging information and evidence related to the case.
  • Depositions may be taken of both parties and witnesses to gather additional information.

Final Custody Hearing:

  • A final custody hearing is held to determine a permanent custody arrangement.
  • The court considers the factors outlined in Florida Statutes Section 61.13 and makes a decision based on the best interests of the child.

FAQs about Child Custody in Florida

Here are some frequently asked questions about child custody in Florida:

Q: What is the best type of custody arrangement for my child?

A: The best type of custody arrangement for your child depends on their individual needs and circumstances. Joint custody may be beneficial for children who benefit from a close relationship with both parents, while sole custody may be more suitable for children who require more stability and consistency.

Q: How do I prove that I am the best parent for my child?

A: To prove that you are the best parent for your child, you must demonstrate that you can provide a stable, loving, and nurturing environment. This may involve providing evidence of your ability to provide for your child's physical, emotional, and educational needs, as well as your ability to cooperate and communicate effectively with the other parent.

Q: What happens if the other parent is abusive or neglectful?

A: If the other parent is abusive or neglectful, you may be able to seek a protective order or temporary custody arrangement to keep your child safe. You should consult with an attorney to determine the best course of action in your specific situation.

Q: How long does a child custody case typically take?

A: The length of a child custody case in Florida can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case, the number of hearings, and the level of attorney involvement. Typically, a child custody case can take several months to a year or more to resolve.
