Introduction to Child Custody in New Mexico

In New Mexico, child custody is governed by the New Mexico Statutes Annotated (NMSA) 1978, § 40-7-1 et seq. The state's primary concern is the best interests of the child, and the court's decision is based on this principle. There are two types of child custody in New Mexico: physical custody and legal custody. Physical custody refers to the physical care and supervision of the child, while legal custody refers to the decision-making authority regarding the child's education, health, and welfare.

Types of Child Custody in New Mexico

There are several types of child custody arrangements in New Mexico, including:

  • Sole physical custody: One parent has primary physical custody of the child, and the other parent may have visitation rights.
  • Joint physical custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, and the child spends significant time with each parent.
  • Sole legal custody: One parent has decision-making authority regarding the child's education, health, and welfare.
  • Joint legal custody: Both parents share decision-making authority regarding the child's education, health, and welfare.

Child Custody Proceedings in New Mexico

Child custody proceedings in New Mexico typically involve the following steps:

  1. Filing a petition: One parent files a petition with the court, requesting child custody and outlining the proposed custody arrangement.
  2. Service of process: The other parent is served with the petition and has a certain amount of time to respond.
  3. Temporary orders: The court may issue temporary orders regarding child custody and visitation while the case is pending.
  4. Discovery: Both parents exchange information and evidence regarding the child's needs and the proposed custody arrangement.
  5. Mediation: The parents may be required to participate in mediation to try to reach a mutually acceptable custody arrangement.
  6. Trial: If the parents cannot reach an agreement, the case will proceed to trial, and the court will make a decision based on the evidence presented.

Factors Considered in Child Custody Decisions

The court considers several factors when making a child custody decision in New Mexico, including:

  • The child's wishes, if the child is old enough to express a preference.
  • The child's relationship with each parent and other significant individuals in the child's life.
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • The mental and physical health of each parent and the child.
  • Any history of domestic violence or substance abuse.
  • The parents' ability to cooperate and communicate with each other.

Expected Attorney Costs for Child Custody Cases in New Mexico

The cost of hiring a child custody attorney in New Mexico can vary depending on several factors, including the complexity of the case, the attorney's experience, and the location. On average, the cost of a child custody attorney in New Mexico can range from $200 to $500 per hour. The total cost of the case can range from $2,000 to $10,000 or more, depending on the length and complexity of the proceedings.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Child Custody Cases in New Mexico

To navigate the child custody process in New Mexico, follow these steps:

  1. Consult with an experienced child custody attorney to discuss your options and develop a strategy.
  2. Gather evidence and documentation to support your proposed custody arrangement, including records of your involvement in the child's life and any relevant witnesses.
  3. File a petition with the court, outlining your proposed custody arrangement and requesting a hearing.
  4. Participate in mediation and negotiation with the other parent to try to reach a mutually acceptable custody arrangement.
  5. Prepare for trial, if necessary, by gathering evidence and witnesses to support your case.
  6. Attend all court hearings and follow the court's orders regarding child custody and visitation.
