Tennessee Child Custody Laws: A Comprehensive Guide for Parents

Introduction

In Tennessee, child custody laws are governed by the Tennessee Code Annotated, specifically Title 36, Chapter 6, Part 4. The state's primary concern is the best interests of the child, and court decisions are made with this principle in mind. As a Family Law attorney, it's essential to understand the complexities of Tennessee child custody laws to ensure that your clients receive the best possible outcome.

Types of Custody

There are two primary types of custody in Tennessee: sole custody and joint custody.

  • Sole Custody: One parent has physical and legal custody of the child, making all major decisions regarding their upbringing.
  • Joint Custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child, making joint decisions regarding their upbringing.

Additionally, there are two types of joint custody: joint physical custody and joint legal custody.

  • Joint Physical Custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, with each parent having significant periods of time with the child.
  • Joint Legal Custody: Both parents share decision-making authority regarding the child's upbringing, with one parent often having primary physical custody.

Custody Determinations

In Tennessee, custody determinations are made based on the best interests of the child. The court considers the following factors:

  • Age and physical, emotional, and educational needs of the child
  • Parents' ability to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs
  • Parents' ability to cooperate with each other regarding the child's upbringing
  • Willfulness or fault of either parent with regard to the child's needs
  • Length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment
  • Parents' ability to maintain a stable, loving, and supportive home environment
  • Parents' ability to provide the child with a stable and secure home environment
  • Whether either parent has failed to support the child financially or emotionally
  • Whether either parent has failed to provide the child with a stable and secure home environment
  • Parents' ability to communicate with each other regarding the child's needs
  • Willfulness or fault of either parent regarding the child's needs

The court may also consider the following factors, if relevant:

  • Domestic violence or child abuse allegations
  • Substance abuse or neglect
  • Parental incarceration or criminal history
  • Parental education, employment, and financial stability

Custody Proceedings

Custody proceedings in Tennessee typically involve the following steps:

  1. Filing a Petition: One parent files a petition for custody with the court, stating their desired custody arrangement.
  2. Service of Process: The petition is served on the other parent, who has the opportunity to respond.
  3. Discovery: Both parents engage in discovery, exchanging information and evidence related to the custody dispute.
  4. Mediation: The court may order mediation to help the parents reach a mutually acceptable custody arrangement.
  5. Trial: If the parents cannot reach an agreement, the case goes to trial, where the court makes a custody determination based on the best interests of the child.
  6. Contempt Proceedings: If one parent fails to comply with a custody order, the other parent may file a contempt petition to enforce the order.

Attorney Costs

Attorney costs can vary widely in Tennessee child custody cases. The following are estimated costs:

  • Flat Fee: $500 - $2,000
  • Hourly Rate: $200 - $500 per hour
  • Contingency Fee: 30% - 40% of the child support award

These costs can add up quickly, especially in complex custody cases. It's essential to discuss costs with your attorney and understand their billing practices.

Step-by-Step Guidance

Here's a step-by-step guide to help you navigate Tennessee child custody laws:

  1. Gather Information: Collect relevant documents, such as birth certificates, school records, and medical records.
  2. Consult an Attorney: Hire a Family Law attorney experienced in Tennessee child custody law.
  3. File a Petition: File a petition for custody with the court, stating your desired custody arrangement.
  4. Engage in Discovery: Exchange information and evidence related to the custody dispute.
  5. Attend Mediation: Participate in mediation to help reach a mutually acceptable custody arrangement.
  6. Attend Trial: If necessary, attend trial and present your case to the court.
  7. Enforce the Order: If the court makes a custody determination, ensure compliance with the order.

FAQs

Here are some frequently asked questions about Tennessee child custody laws:

  • Q: What is the primary concern of Tennessee child custody laws?

    A: The best interests of the child.

  • Q: What types of custody are there in Tennessee?

    A: Sole custody and joint custody (joint physical and joint legal custody).

  • Q: What factors does the court consider when making a custody determination?

    A: Age and physical, emotional, and educational needs of the child, parents' ability to provide for the child's needs, ability to cooperate with each other, willfulness or fault of either parent, length of time the child has lived in a stable environment, and more.

  • Q: What is the typical process for child custody proceedings in Tennessee?

    A: Filing a petition, service of process, discovery, mediation, trial, and contempt proceedings.

  • Q: How much do attorney costs vary in Tennessee child custody cases?

    A: Estimated costs range from $500 - $2,000 (flat fee) to $200 - $500 per hour (hourly rate) to 30% - 40% of the child support award (contingency fee).
