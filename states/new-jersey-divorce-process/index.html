A Comprehensive Guide to the Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in New Jersey

Divorce can be a complex and emotionally challenging process, especially in a state like New Jersey that has specific requirements and procedures. In this guide, we will walk you through the divorce filing process and requirements in New Jersey, providing you with a clear understanding of the steps involved and the necessary documentation.

Understanding the Grounds for Divorce in New Jersey

In New Jersey, divorces are typically filed under the grounds of "irreconcilable differences." This means that one spouse must file a complaint stating that the marriage is irretrievably broken and that there are no reasonable grounds for reconciliation.

It's worth noting that New Jersey does not allow fault grounds for divorce. This means that you cannot file for divorce based on the fault of your spouse, such as adultery or abandonment. However, you can file for divorce based on the irreconcilability of your marriage.

Residency Requirements for Filing Divorce in New Jersey

In order to file for divorce in New Jersey, one spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least six months prior to filing. This means that you must have lived in New Jersey for six months before you can file for divorce.

This residency requirement is in place to ensure that the court has jurisdiction over the case and that the parties have had sufficient opportunity to become familiar with the laws and procedures of the state.

The Divorce Filing Process in New Jersey

The divorce filing process in New Jersey typically begins with the filing of a complaint by one spouse. This complaint must include the following information:

  • The name and address of the parties involved
  • A statement of the grounds for divorce
  • A statement of the relief sought, including the division of property and the custody of any minor children
  • A request for a hearing to resolve any issues that cannot be agreed upon

The complaint must be served to the other spouse, either personally or by certified mail, and they must have 30 days to respond to the complaint. If they fail to respond, the court may enter a default judgment in favor of the party who filed the complaint.

Waiting Period for Divorce in New Jersey

Once the complaint has been filed and served, there is a 30-day waiting period before the court can enter a final judgment of divorce. This waiting period is in place to give the parties an opportunity to reconcile and to allow them to finalize their divorce proceedings.

During this waiting period, the parties may attempt to negotiate a settlement agreement, which can be presented to the court for approval. If a settlement agreement is not reached, the court will schedule a hearing to resolve any issues that cannot be agreed upon.

FAQs about Divorce in New Jersey

Here are some common questions about divorce in New Jersey:

Q: How long does it take to get a divorce in New Jersey?

A: The length of time it takes to get a divorce in New Jersey depends on several factors, including the complexity of the case and the availability of the court. However, the minimum waiting period is 30 days after the complaint has been filed and served.

Q: Do I need a lawyer to get a divorce in New Jersey?

A: While it is not necessary to have a lawyer to get a divorce in New Jersey, having one can be beneficial in navigating the process and protecting your rights. A lawyer can help you understand the laws and procedures of the state and can represent you in court if needed.

Q: Can I file for divorce if my spouse is in the military?

A: Yes, you can file for divorce if your spouse is in the military, but there may be additional requirements and procedures to follow. You should consult with a lawyer to understand the specific requirements and procedures that apply to your situation.

Q: Can I get a divorce if my spouse is not a resident of New Jersey?

A: No, you cannot get a divorce in New Jersey if your spouse is not a resident of the state. However, you may be able to file for divorce in the state where your spouse resides, if they meet the residency requirements of that state.

Q: What is the cost of getting a divorce in New Jersey?

A: The cost of getting a divorce in New Jersey can vary depending on several factors, including the complexity of the case and the lawyer's fees. However, the court filing fee for a divorce is currently $300.
