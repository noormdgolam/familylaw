Family Law Statute of Limitations in Oklahoma: A Comprehensive Guide

Filing a family law case in Oklahoma requires a thorough understanding of the statute of limitations (SOL). The SOL is the time frame within which a party must initiate a lawsuit. Failure to meet the SOL can result in dismissal of the case, and the party may be barred from pursuing their claims. In Oklahoma, there are specific SOLs for various family law actions. This guide provides an overview of the SOLs relevant to family law in Oklahoma, as well as the general principles that govern these time limits.

General Principles of Statute of Limitations in Oklahoma

The SOL in Oklahoma is governed by the Oklahoma Statutes, specifically Title 12, which deals with Civil Procedure. The SOL is a defense that can be raised by a defendant in a lawsuit, and it can be waived if not timely raised. The SOL applies to all civil actions, including family law cases.

Personal Injury, Property Damage, and Breach of Contract SOLs

The SOLs for personal injury, property damage, and breach of contract are as follows:

  • Personal Injury: 4 years (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 12, Section 95)
  • Property Damage: 5 years (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 12, Section 95)
  • Breach of Contract: 10 years (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 12, Section 95)

Family Law Actions and Statute of Limitations in Oklahoma

While the SOLs mentioned above apply to general civil actions, family law cases have their own specific SOLs. The following table provides an overview of the SOLs relevant to family law in Oklahoma:

Family Law Action Statute of Limitations
Divorce 2 years (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 43, Section 121)
Custody and Visitation No specific SOL (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 43, Section 110)
Child Support No specific SOL (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 43, Section 118)
Adoption 1 year (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 10, Section 60)
Marriage Dissolution (Separation Agreement) 2 years (Oklahoma Statutes, Title 43, Section 121)

Calculating the Statute of Limitations in Oklahoma

To calculate the SOL, the date of the incident or act giving rise to the claim must be determined. The SOL begins to run from this date. For example, if a party was injured in a car accident on January 1, 2020, the SOL for personal injury would be 4 years, meaning the party would have until January 1, 2024, to file their lawsuit.

Waiving the Statute of Limitations in Oklahoma

The SOL can be waived in certain circumstances. If the defendant fails to timely raise the SOL as a defense, the party may be deemed to have waived their right to assert it. Additionally, a party may waive the SOL by taking some action that acknowledges the existence of a claim, such as making a payment or admitting liability.

Conclusion

Filing a family law case in Oklahoma requires a thorough understanding of the SOL. The SOLs for personal injury, property damage, and breach of contract are 4, 5, and 10 years, respectively. The SOLs for family law actions vary, but generally range from 1 to 2 years. Calculating the SOL involves determining the date of the incident or act giving rise to the claim and applying the relevant SOL. Waiving the SOL is possible under certain circumstances, such as failure to timely raise it as a defense or taking action that acknowledges the existence of a claim.
