Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Kansas: A Comprehensive Guide

In the state of Kansas, child custody laws are guided by the principle that the "best interests of the child" should be the primary consideration in any custody decision. This guide will delve into the specifics of Kansas child custody laws, including the presumption of joint custody and the age at which a child can choose their own custodian.

Understanding the Primary Custody Factor in Kansas

The primary custody factor in Kansas is the "best interests of the child." This means that any decision regarding custody, visitation, or other parental rights should be made with the goal of promoting the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being. This principle is enshrined in Kansas Statute Annotated (KSA) 60-1610, which states that the court's primary concern in custody decisions is the "best interests of the child."

Factors Considered in Determining the Best Interests of the Child

While the "best interests of the child" is the primary consideration, the court will also consider a range of other factors when making a custody decision. These factors include:

  • The wishes of the child, if old enough to express them
  • The wishes of the parents
  • The child's physical and emotional needs
  • The child's educational and extracurricular needs
  • The parents' ability to cooperate and communicate
  • The parents' physical and emotional fitness
  • The child's relationship with each parent

The Joint Custody Presumption in Kansas

In Kansas, there is a presumption in favor of joint custody. Joint custody is defined as a situation in which both parents share legal and physical custody of the child, either equally or in some other proportion. This presumption is enshrined in KSA 23-3202, which states that joint custody is presumed to be in the best interests of the child, unless the court finds that joint custody would be detrimental to the child.

Types of Joint Custody in Kansas

There are several types of joint custody arrangements in Kansas, including:

  • Joint physical custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, with the child spending equal or near-equal time with each parent.
  • Joint legal custody: Both parents share legal custody of the child, with decision-making authority divided between the parents.
  • Split custody: One or more children are awarded to each parent, with each parent having sole custody of one or more children.
  • Shared physical custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, but the child spends more time with one parent than the other.

Age at Which a Child Can Choose Custodian in Kansas

There is no set age in Kansas at which a child can choose their own custodian. In Kansas, the court will consider the child's wishes, but ultimately the decision regarding custody will be based on the best interests of the child. This means that the child's wishes may be considered, but they are not automatically entitled to choose their own custodian.

FAQs About Child Custody Laws in Kansas

Q: What is the primary custody factor in Kansas?

A: The primary custody factor in Kansas is the "best interests of the child."

Q: Is there a presumption of joint custody in Kansas?

A: Yes, there is a presumption of joint custody in Kansas. Joint custody is presumed to be in the best interests of the child, unless the court finds that joint custody would be detrimental to the child.

Q: Can a child choose their own custodian in Kansas?

A: No, there is no set age in Kansas at which a child can choose their own custodian. The court will consider the child's wishes, but ultimately the decision regarding custody will be based on the best interests of the child.

Q: How can I modify a custody order in Kansas?

A: A parent or other party may petition to modify a custody order if there has been a change in circumstances. The petition must be filed in the court that issued the original custody order, and the party seeking modification must demonstrate that there has been a significant change in circumstances since the original custody order was issued.

Q: What is a custody evaluation, and how is it used in Kansas?

A: A custody evaluation is a psychological evaluation that is conducted to determine the best interests of a child in a custody dispute. In Kansas, the court may order a custody evaluation to assist in making a custody decision. The evaluation will typically involve interviews with the parents, the child, and other relevant parties, as well as an assessment of the parties' fitness and ability to care for the child.
