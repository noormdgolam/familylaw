New Jersey Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

As a highly experienced Family Law attorney and legal consultant, I have dedicated my career to guiding individuals through the complex and often emotionally charged process of divorce and child custody proceedings in New Jersey.

Overview of New Jersey Family Law

New Jersey is a no-fault divorce state, meaning that a couple can seek a divorce without having to prove fault or misconduct on the part of one spouse.

The New Jersey Statute, N.J.S.A. 2A:34-2, permits a divorce to be granted based on a finding that the marriage is "irreconcilably broken" or that the parties have "irreconcilable differences."

Grounds for Divorce in New Jersey

  • Irreconcilable differences
  • Adultery
  • Desertion
  • Extreme mental or physical cruelty
  • Bigamy
  • Deviant sexual behavior
  • Imprisonment of the defendant for 18 months or more
  • Separation for 18 months or more

Divorce Proceedings in New Jersey

The divorce process in New Jersey typically begins with the filing of a complaint by one spouse seeking a divorce, known as the "plaintiff."

The complaint must include the grounds for divorce and must be served on the other spouse, known as the "defendant." The defendant then has 35 days to respond to the complaint.

Steps in a Divorce Proceeding in New Jersey

  1. Filing of the complaint
  2. Service of the complaint on the defendant
  3. Response by the defendant
  4. Motion for temporary relief (e.g. custody, support, etc.)
  5. Discovery (e.g. depositions, interrogatories, etc.)
  6. Motion for final relief (e.g. divorce, custody, support, etc.)
  7. Final hearing

Child Custody in New Jersey

Child custody is one of the most critical issues in a divorce proceeding in New Jersey, as it involves the well-being and care of minor children.

The New Jersey Statute, N.J.S.A. 2A:34-23, provides that the court shall award custody of a minor child to the parent who is most likely to act in the best interests of the child.

Types of Child Custody in New Jersey

  • Physical custody: The parent who has physical care and control of the child
  • Legal custody: The parent who has decision-making authority over the child's education, healthcare, and other important decisions
  • Shared custody: Both parents have physical and legal custody of the child, with joint responsibility for decision-making
  • Sole custody: One parent has physical and legal custody of the child, with the other parent having limited rights and responsibilities

Step-by-Step Guidance for a New Jersey Divorce Proceeding

Here is a step-by-step guide to help you navigate the New Jersey divorce process:

Step 1: Gather Information and Documents

Gather all relevant documents and information related to your marriage, including:

  • Copies of marriage certificates and divorce complaints
  • Copies of financial records, including bank statements and tax returns
  • Copies of property records, including deeds and title reports
  • Copies of child care records, including birth certificates and school records

Step 2: Hire a Lawyer

Hire a qualified New Jersey family law attorney to represent you in your divorce proceeding.

Make sure to ask about their experience in handling divorce cases and their fees.

Step 3: File a Complaint

File a complaint with the court seeking a divorce and listing your grounds for divorce.

You will need to serve the complaint on your spouse and wait for their response.

Step 4: Respond to the Complaint

If you are the defendant, you will need to respond to the complaint within 35 days.

You can either file an answer or a counterclaim.

Step 5: Motions for Temporary Relief

Either party can file a motion for temporary relief, including custody, support, and other issues.

The court will hear the motion and make a ruling.

Step 6: Discovery

Both parties will engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and documents related to the case.

This may include depositions, interrogatories, and requests for production of documents.

Step 7: Motions for Final Relief

Either party can file a motion for final relief, including a final divorce decree and a custody order.

The court will hear the motion and make a ruling.

Step 8: Final Hearing

The court will hold a final hearing to determine the final outcome of the case.

The judge will hear testimony and make a ruling.
