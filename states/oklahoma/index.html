Oklahoma Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce, Child Custody, and Beyond

Introduction

Family law in Oklahoma encompasses a wide range of complex issues, including divorce, child custody, child support, and property division. As a highly experienced family law attorney, I will provide a thorough overview of the state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance to help individuals navigate the often-challenging process of family law in Oklahoma.

Divorce in Oklahoma

Types of Divorce in Oklahoma

  • No-Fault Divorce: Oklahoma allows for no-fault divorce, which means that a divorce can be granted without assigning fault to either spouse. This type of divorce is most common in Oklahoma.
  • At-Fault Divorce: Oklahoma also allows for at-fault divorce, which means that a divorce can be granted due to the fault of one spouse (i.e., adultery, abuse, etc.). However, this type of divorce is less common in Oklahoma.

Statutes Governing Divorce in Oklahoma

Oklahoma Statutes Title 43 Chapter 101 governs divorce in Oklahoma. Key provisions include:

  • Section 101.1: Oklahoma allows for the granting of a divorce in cases where the spouses have been living separate and apart for a period of not less than six months.
  • Section 101.2: Oklahoma requires that one spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least six months prior to filing for divorce.

Grounds for Divorce in Oklahoma

  • Insanity: Oklahoma allows for divorce in cases where one spouse has been confined to a mental institution for a period of not less than five years.
  • Abandonment: Oklahoma allows for divorce in cases where one spouse has abandoned the other spouse for a period of not less than one year.
  • Adultery: Oklahoma allows for divorce in cases where one spouse has committed adultery.
  • Imprisonment: Oklahoma allows for divorce in cases where one spouse has been imprisoned for a period of not less than five years.

Child Custody in Oklahoma

Types of Child Custody in Oklahoma

  • Joint Custody: Oklahoma allows for joint custody, which means that both parents share physical and legal custody of the child.
  • Split Custody: Oklahoma allows for split custody, which means that multiple children are placed in the custody of one parent, while the other children are placed in the custody of the other parent.

Statutes Governing Child Custody in Oklahoma

Oklahoma Statutes Title 43 Chapter 110 governs child custody in Oklahoma. Key provisions include:

  • Section 110.1: Oklahoma requires that the court consider the best interests of the child when determining custody.
  • Section 110.2: Oklahoma allows for joint custody in cases where both parents are capable of providing a stable and loving environment for the child.

Factors Considered in Child Custody Cases in Oklahoma

  • The wishes of the child
  • The relationship between each parent and the child
  • The ability of each parent to provide a stable and loving environment for the child
  • The geographic location of each parent's residence
  • The availability of extended family members to provide support for the child

Child Support in Oklahoma

Oklahoma Child Support Guidelines

Oklahoma Statutes Title 43 Chapter 110 governs child support in Oklahoma. The state uses a child support guidelines worksheet to determine the amount of child support that must be paid by the non-custodial parent.

The guidelines take into account the following factors:

  • The income of both parents
  • The number of children being supported
  • The age of the children
  • The cost of childcare
  • The cost of health insurance

Statutes Governing Child Support in Oklahoma

  • Section 110.1: Oklahoma requires that the court order child support in cases where the court grants custody of a child to one parent.
  • Section 110.2: Oklahoma allows for the modification of child support orders in cases where there is a change in circumstances.

Property Division in Oklahoma

Oklahoma Statutes Governing Property Division

Oklahoma Statutes Title 43 Chapter 131 governs property division in Oklahoma. Key provisions include:

  • Section 131.1: Oklahoma requires that the court divide property equitably in cases of divorce.
  • Section 131.2: Oklahoma allows for the award of separate property to one spouse in cases where the property was acquired prior to the marriage.

Factors Considered in Property Division Cases in Oklahoma

  • The length of the marriage
  • The value of the property
  • The contribution of each spouse to the acquisition of the property
  • The economic needs of each spouse
