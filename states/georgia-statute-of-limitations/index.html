Georgia Statutes of Limitations for Family Law

Georgia Code § 9-3-33 sets the statute of limitations for divorce, which is typically 6 years from the date of the divorce filing. However, this statute only applies to the divorce itself, not to related issues such as child custody and support.

For child custody disputes, the court may apply the statute of limitations set forth in Georgia Code § 9-3-24, which states that a parent or guardian must file a petition to modify a custody order within 1 year of the date of the order or 6 months after the date of the child's 14th birthday, whichever is later.

Georgia Code § 9-11-42 sets the statute of limitations for child support, which is 2 years from the date of the last support payment made by the obligor (the person responsible for paying support). However, this statute only applies to the support itself, not to related issues such as custody and visitation.

Common Legal Proceedings in Family Law Cases

Family law cases in Georgia typically involve the following proceedings:

  • Divorce: A court proceeding to dissolve a marriage, which may involve issues such as property division, spousal support, and child custody.
  • Child Custody: A court proceeding to determine the rights and responsibilities of parents or guardians regarding their child's care and well-being.
  • Child Support: A court proceeding to determine the amount of support one parent or guardian must pay to the other for the benefit of their child.
  • Spousal Support: A court proceeding to determine the amount of support one spouse must pay to the other, which may be based on factors such as income, expenses, and the length of the marriage.
  • Property Division: A court proceeding to divide marital property, which may involve issues such as the valuation and distribution of assets and debts.

Step-by-Step Guide to Navigating Family Law Cases in Georgia

Here is a step-by-step guide to navigating family law cases in Georgia:

  1. Consult with an Attorney: Schedule a consultation with an experienced family law attorney to discuss your case and determine the best course of action.
  2. File a Petition: If you are seeking to initiate a family law case, file a petition with the court, which must include a detailed statement of your claims and the relief you seek.
  3. Serve the Respondent: If you are seeking to serve the respondent with a petition, you must follow the procedures set forth in Georgia Code § 9-11-4, which may involve personal service or service by certified mail.
  4. Respond to the Petition: If you are the respondent, you must file a response to the petition, which must include a detailed statement of your defenses and any counterclaims you may have.
  5. Discovery: Both parties may engage in discovery, which involves the exchange of information and documents related to the case, such as financial records and witness statements.
  6. Mediation: Both parties may participate in mediation, which involves a neutral third-party mediator who helps the parties reach a settlement.
  7. Trial: If the parties are unable to reach a settlement, the case will proceed to trial, where a judge or jury will hear evidence and render a decision.
  8. Appeal: Either party may appeal the decision of the trial court to a higher court, such as the Georgia Court of Appeals or the Georgia Supreme Court.
