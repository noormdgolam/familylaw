Understanding Family Law Statute of Limitations in Georgia

When dealing with family law matters in Georgia, it's essential to be aware of the statute of limitations (SOL) for various legal actions. The SOL is a deadline by which a plaintiff must file a lawsuit against a defendant. Failure to meet this deadline can result in the dismissal of the case.

Statute of Limitations for Key Family Law Matters in Georgia

The SOL in Georgia varies depending on the type of case. Here are some key family law matters and their corresponding SOLs:

Personal Injury Cases

In Georgia, the SOL for personal injury cases is three (3) years from the date of the injury. This means that if you or a loved one has been injured due to someone else's negligence, you have three years to file a lawsuit. If you fail to do so, you may be barred from seeking compensation.

Property Damage Cases

The SOL for property damage cases in Georgia is four (4) years from the date of the damage. This means that if someone has damaged your property, you have four years to file a lawsuit to seek compensation for the damages.

Breach of Contract Cases

The SOL for breach of contract cases in Georgia is three (3) years from the date of the breach. This means that if someone has failed to fulfill their contractual obligations, you have three years to file a lawsuit to seek compensation for the damages.

Important Considerations for Family Law Matters in Georgia

While the SOLs mentioned above provide a general guideline, there are several important considerations to keep in mind when dealing with family law matters in Georgia:

Discovery Rule

In some cases, the SOL may begin to run when the injury or damage is discovered, rather than when it occurred. This is known as the "discovery rule." For example, if you were injured in a car accident but didn't discover the extent of your injuries until later, the SOL may begin to run from the date you discovered the injury.

Tolling of the SOL

The SOL may be tolled, or paused, in certain circumstances. For example, if the defendant is a minor, the SOL may be tolled until the defendant reaches the age of majority. Similarly, if the plaintiff is mentally incapacitated, the SOL may be tolled until the plaintiff is no longer incapacitated.
