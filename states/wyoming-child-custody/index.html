Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Wyoming: A Comprehensive Guide

In Wyoming, child custody laws and guidelines are designed to prioritize the best interests of the child. Understanding these laws can help parents navigate the complexities of divorce or separation and ensure that their child's needs are met. In this guide, we'll delve into the key factors that influence child custody decisions in Wyoming, including joint custody presumptions and the age at which children can choose their own custodial arrangements.

Primary Custody Factor: Best Interests of the Child

The best interests of the child are the primary consideration in Wyoming child custody cases. This principle is enshrined in Wyoming Statute 20-2-201, which states that "the court shall determine custody in accordance with the best interests of the child." To determine the best interests of the child, courts consider a range of factors, including:

  • Age and sex of the child
  • Wishes of the parents and child
  • Relationship between the child and each parent
  • Ability of each parent to provide a stable home environment
  • History of domestic violence or child abuse
  • Availability of resources to the parents
  • Other relevant factors

Joint Custody Presumption

In Wyoming, there is a joint custody presumption, which means that the court will presume that joint custody is in the best interests of the child unless there is clear evidence to the contrary. This presumption is based on the idea that joint custody promotes a stable and loving relationship between the child and both parents.

Joint Custody Requirements

For joint custody to be ordered, both parents must agree to share custody and provide for the child's physical, emotional, and financial needs. In addition, both parents must:

  • Communicate with each other about the child's needs and well-being
  • Cooperate with each other in making decisions about the child's education, healthcare, and extracurricular activities
  • Share responsibility for the child's financial support and expenses
  • Not unreasonably interfere with the other parent's custodial rights

Age at Which Children Can Choose Custodial Arrangements

In Wyoming, children can choose their own custodial arrangements when they reach the age of 12. However, this does not mean that the child's wishes are automatically granted. The court will still consider the child's wishes as one of several factors in determining the best interests of the child.
