Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Missouri: A Comprehensive Guide

Missouri's child custody laws prioritize the best interests of the child in determining custody arrangements. Understanding these laws is crucial for parents navigating the often-complex process of child custody determination.

Understanding Missouri's Child Custody Laws

Missouri's child custody laws are governed by the Missouri Revised Statutes, specifically Sections 452.375 to 452.392. The state's primary custody factor is the best interests of the child, which is defined as:

  • The love, affection, and other emotional ties between each parent and the child;
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to facilitate a close and continuing relationship between the child and the other parent;
  • The child's wishes, taking into account the child's age and maturity;
  • The physical, emotional, and educational needs of the child;
  • The reasonable preference of the child, if the child is sufficiently mature to express a preference;
  • The stability and permanence of the child's home environment and the capacity and disposition of each parent to provide a stable and permanent home for the child;
  • The motivation and good faith of each parent in seeking or opposing custody;
  • The geographical location and distance between the residences of the parents, which shall be considered in terms of the potential impact on the child's relationship with each parent;
  • The existence of any history of acts of domestic violence or child abuse;
  • The extent to which each parent has maintained a consistent presence in the child's life;
  • The extent to which each parent has fulfilled their obligations to support the child financially, emotionally, and otherwise; and
  • Any other factors relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

Joint Custody Presumption in Missouri

Missouri has a joint custody presumption, which means that the court will start with the assumption that joint custody is in the best interests of the child. However, the court may order sole custody if it determines that joint custody is not in the best interests of the child.

Age of the Child in Determining Custody

Missouri does not have a set age for when the child can choose their custodial parent. The court will consider the child's wishes, taking into account the child's age and maturity, when determining custody. However, the child's preference may not be the sole determining factor.

Factors in Determining Custody

When determining custody, the court will consider the following factors:

  • The relationship between the child and each parent;
  • The ability of each parent to provide a stable and permanent home for the child;
  • The motivation and good faith of each parent in seeking or opposing custody;
  • The geographical location and distance between the residences of the parents;
  • The existence of any history of acts of domestic violence or child abuse;
  • The extent to which each parent has maintained a consistent presence in the child's life;
  • The extent to which each parent has fulfilled their obligations to support the child financially, emotionally, and otherwise;
  • Any other factors relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

Custody Arrangements in Missouri

Missouri law allows for various custody arrangements, including:

  • Sole custody, where one parent has exclusive physical and legal custody of the child;
  • Joint custody, where both parents share physical and legal custody of the child;
  • Shared physical custody, where both parents have physical custody of the child, but one parent has primary physical custody;
  • Shared legal custody, where both parents have legal custody of the child, but one parent has primary legal custody.

Custody Evaluation in Missouri

In some cases, the court may appoint a custody evaluator to assess the situation and make recommendations for custody. The custody evaluator will consider the factors mentioned above and may interview the child, parents, and other relevant parties.

Modifying a Custody Order in Missouri

A parent may seek to modify a custody order if there has been a significant change in circumstances since the original order was entered. The court will consider the best interests of the child when determining whether to modify the custody order.
