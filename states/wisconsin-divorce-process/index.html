Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Wisconsin: A Comprehensive Guide

Getting a divorce can be a complex and emotionally charged process, but understanding the requirements and steps involved can make it easier. In Wisconsin, the divorce filing process is governed by state law, and this guide will walk you through the key requirements and steps to take.

Residency Requirement

In Wisconsin, one of the spouses must have been a resident of the state for at least six months before filing for divorce. This is known as the residency requirement. This means that if you or your spouse have recently moved to Wisconsin, you may not be eligible to file for divorce yet.

Waiting Period

Once the residency requirement has been met, the next step is to wait for 90 days before the divorce can be finalized. This is known as the waiting period. During this time, both spouses must be separated and not cohabiting.

Fault Grounds Allowed

In Wisconsin, fault grounds are allowed, which means that one spouse can claim that the other's behavior was the cause of the marriage breakdown. Some common fault grounds include adultery, abandonment, and physical or emotional abuse. However, even if one spouse claims fault, the other spouse may still be entitled to alimony or other forms of support.

Steps to File for Divorce in Wisconsin

Step 1: Meet the Residency Requirement

To file for divorce in Wisconsin, you or your spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least six months. This means that you must have a valid Wisconsin driver's license, registered to vote in Wisconsin, or have filed a tax return in Wisconsin.

Step 2: File the Petition for Divorce

Once you have met the residency requirement, you can file the petition for divorce with the circuit court in the county where you or your spouse lives. You will need to provide the court with a copy of your marriage certificate and a completed petition for divorce.

Step 3: Wait for 90 Days

After filing the petition for divorce, you and your spouse must wait for 90 days before the divorce can be finalized. During this time, both spouses must be separated and not cohabiting.

Step 4: Finalize the Divorce

Once the waiting period has expired, you can file a request for a final hearing with the court. At the hearing, you and your spouse will be asked to present your case and come to an agreement on issues such as property division, child custody, and alimony. If you are unable to agree, the court will make a decision for you.

Divorce Options

No-Fault Divorce

In Wisconsin, you can file for a no-fault divorce, which means that you do not need to claim fault grounds. This is the easiest and quickest way to get a divorce, but it may not be the best option for everyone.

Fault Divorce

Alternatively, you can file for a fault divorce, which means that you claim that your spouse's behavior was the cause of the marriage breakdown. This can be a more complicated process and may require more evidence.

Child Custody and Support

When it comes to child custody and support, the court's primary concern is the best interests of the child. This means that the court will consider factors such as the child's age, health, and relationship with each parent. In Wisconsin, the court can award joint custody, sole custody, or a combination of both, as well as order child support payments.

Property Division

When it comes to property division, the court will consider factors such as the length of the marriage, the income and earning potential of each spouse, and the value of the marital property. In Wisconsin, the court can award joint ownership of property, sell marital property, or award one spouse a larger share of the property.

Alimony

When it comes to alimony, the court will consider factors such as the length of the marriage, the income and earning potential of each spouse, and the standard of living established during the marriage. In Wisconsin, the court can award permanent alimony, temporary alimony, or a lump sum payment.

Divorce Costs

The cost of a divorce in Wisconsin can vary depending on the complexity of the case, the attorney fees, and the court costs. On average, the cost of a divorce in Wisconsin can range from $5,000 to $20,000 or more.

Conclusion

Filing for divorce in Wisconsin can be a complex and emotionally charged process, but understanding the requirements and steps involved can make it easier. By meeting the residency requirement, waiting for 90 days, and finalizing the divorce, you can bring your marriage to an end and start a new chapter in your life.
