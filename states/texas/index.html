Family law in Texas is governed by the Texas Family Code, which outlines the rights and responsibilities of family members, including spouses and children. In this article, we will discuss the various aspects of family law in Texas, including divorce, child custody, and other related topics.

Texas Divorce Laws

In Texas, divorce is known as a "dissolution of marriage." To file for divorce, one spouse must be a resident of Texas for at least six months and a resident of the county where the divorce is filed for at least 90 days. The grounds for divorce in Texas include: adultery, cruelty, desertion, confinement in a penitentiary, living apart for a certain period of time, and incurable insanity.

Types of Divorce in Texas

  • No-Fault Divorce: A no-fault divorce is one in which neither spouse is at fault for the breakdown of the marriage. This type of divorce is faster and less expensive than a fault-based divorce.
  • A fault-based divorce is one in which one spouse is at fault for the breakdown of the marriage. This type of divorce is more complex and may take longer to resolve.
  • An uncontested divorce is one in which both spouses agree on all aspects of the divorce, including property division, child custody, and spousal support.

Child Custody in Texas

Child custody in Texas refers to the rights and responsibilities of parents regarding their children. In Texas, child custody is determined by the best interest of the child standard. This standard considers various factors, including the child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs, the parents' ability to provide a stable home environment, and the child's wishes (if the child is old enough to express a preference).

Types of Custody in Texas

  • Sole custody is when one parent has the exclusive right to make decisions regarding the child's care and well-being.
  • Joint custody is when both parents share the right to make decisions regarding the child's care and well-being.
  • A possessory conservatorship is when one parent has the right to visitation and possession of the child, but not the exclusive right to make decisions regarding the child's care and well-being.

Property Division in Texas

Property division in Texas refers to the distribution of marital property and debts between spouses in a divorce. In Texas, property division is determined by the community property doctrine, which states that all property acquired during marriage is community property and must be divided equally between spouses.

Community Property in Texas

  • Marital property is any property acquired during marriage, including real estate, personal property, and financial assets.
  • Separate property is any property owned by one spouse before marriage or acquired during marriage through gift or inheritance.

Spousal Support in Texas

Spousal support in Texas refers to the payment of financial support from one spouse to the other after a divorce. In Texas, spousal support is determined by the court's discretion and is based on various factors, including the length of the marriage, the income of each spouse, and the standard of living established during the marriage.

Types of Spousal Support in Texas

  • Temporary spousal support is paid during the pendency of the divorce proceedings.
  • Permanent spousal support is paid after the divorce is final and may continue for a specified period of time.

Child Support in Texas

Child support in Texas refers to the payment of financial support from one parent to the other for the benefit of their child. In Texas, child support is determined by the court's discretion and is based on various factors, including the income of each parent, the number of children, and the standard of living established during the marriage.

Common Legal Proceedings in Texas Family Law

Common legal proceedings in Texas family law include:

  • A TRO is a court order that temporarily restricts one spouse's access to the marital home, children, or other marital assets.
  • A preliminary injunction is a court order that temporarily restricts one spouse's access to the marital home, children, or other marital assets.
  • A final hearing is the final court proceeding in a divorce case, where the court makes a final decision regarding property division, child custody, and spousal support.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Texas Family Law

Here is a step-by-step guide for Texas family law:

  1. File a petition for divorce or other family law action.
  2. Serve the other party with the petition and notice of the upcoming court hearing.
  3. Attend an MDRC to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  4. Attend a final hearing to present evidence and arguments to the court.
  5. Obtain a final decree of divorce or other family law order.
