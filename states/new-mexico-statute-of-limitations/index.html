Family Law Statute of Limitations in New Mexico: A Comprehensive Guide

In New Mexico, a Statute of Limitations (SOL) is a time period within which a person can file a lawsuit for a particular type of claim. This guide will provide an in-depth examination of the SOLs relevant to family law in New Mexico, highlighting key facts, requirements, and a comparison of different legal actions.

Understanding the Statute of Limitations in New Mexico

The SOL in New Mexico varies depending on the type of claim. Based on the aggregated data, the following SOLs are applicable:

  • Personal Injury: 3 years
  • Property Damage: 5 years
  • Breach of Contract: 7 years

Family Law and Its Connection to Statute of Limitations

Family law encompasses a range of issues, including divorce, child custody, child support, and property division. While the SOLs mentioned above apply to specific types of claims, family law cases often involve multiple claims and issues. Here's how the SOLs might impact family law cases in New Mexico:

Divorce and Property Division

When it comes to divorce and property division, the SOLs mentioned above may not directly apply. However, if a party files a lawsuit related to the divorce, such as a claim for property division or alimony, the applicable SOL would be the one relevant to the specific claim. For example, if the party claims that their spouse concealed assets during the divorce proceedings, the applicable SOL would be 7 years (Breach of Contract).

Child Custody and Support

Child custody and support cases are typically governed by the New Mexico Children's Code. While the SOLs mentioned above do not directly apply to child custody and support cases, the court may consider claims related to the child's well-being, such as claims of child abuse or neglect. In such cases, the applicable SOL would be the one relevant to the specific claim, such as 3 years for Personal Injury if the child suffered physical harm.

Other Family Law Issues

Other family law issues, such as adoption, guardianship, and will contests, may also involve specific SOLs. For example, a will contest would be governed by the SOL for Breach of Contract (7 years), while an adoption case would be governed by the New Mexico Adoption Code.

FAQs

Q: What is the Statute of Limitations for personal injury claims in New Mexico?

A: The SOL for personal injury claims in New Mexico is 3 years.

Q: Can I file a lawsuit for property damage in New Mexico if it occurred more than 5 years ago?

A: No, the SOL for property damage claims in New Mexico is 5 years. If the claim is filed after the 5-year period, it may be barred by the SOL.

Q: What is the SOL for breach of contract claims in New Mexico?

A: The SOL for breach of contract claims in New Mexico is 7 years.

Q: Can I file a lawsuit for child custody or support in New Mexico if the child is now an adult?

A: In general, the SOL for child custody and support cases in New Mexico is governed by the New Mexico Children's Code. However, the court may consider claims related to the child's well-being, and the applicable SOL would be the one relevant to the specific claim.

Q: Can I contest a will in New Mexico if the deceased person passed away more than 7 years ago?

A: No, the SOL for will contests in New Mexico is 7 years. If the claim is filed after the 7-year period, it may be barred by the SOL.
