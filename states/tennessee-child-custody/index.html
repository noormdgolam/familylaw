Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Tennessee: A Comprehensive Guide

In the state of Tennessee, the laws and guidelines governing child custody are designed to prioritize the best interests of the child. This guide provides an in-depth look at the key factors and procedures involved in determining child custody in Tennessee.

Primary Custody Factor: Best Interests of the Child

The primary consideration in determining child custody in Tennessee is the best interests of the child. Tennessee Code Annotated (TCA) § 36-6-106 states that the court shall consider the following factors in making a custody determination:

  • The love, affection, and bond between the child and each parent;
  • The disposition of each parent to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care, and other necessities;
  • The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment with the person seeking custody;
  • The permanence of the child's home or homes;
  • The moral fitness of the parents;
  • The mental and physical health of the parents;
  • The home, school, and community record of the child;
  • The reasonable preference of the child, if the court deems the child to be of sufficient age to express a preference;
  • The willingness and ability of each parent to facilitate a close and continuing relationship between the child and the other parent;
  • Any evidence of domestic abuse, as defined in TCA § 36-3-601, by either parent toward the child or the other parent;
  • Any other factors relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

Joint Custody Presumption

In Tennessee, there is a joint custody presumption, which means that the court will initially order joint custody unless one of the parents can show that it would not be in the best interests of the child.

Age of Child's Choice

The age at which a child can choose which parent to live with is 12 years old. However, the court will still consider the child's preference if they are older, but also take into account their maturity level and what is in the best interests of the child.

Types of Child Custody Arrangements

There are several types of child custody arrangements that can be ordered by the court in Tennessee, including:

  • Joint Custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child.
  • Sole Custody: One parent has physical and legal custody of the child, while the other parent may have visitation rights.
  • Split Custody: The parents share physical custody of the child, but the child lives with each parent separately.
  • Shared Custody: The parents share physical custody of the child, but one parent has primary custody.

Changing Child Custody Arrangements

Child custody arrangements can be modified if there has been a significant change in circumstances since the original order was entered. The court will consider the best interests of the child in determining whether to modify the custody arrangement.

Requirements for Modifying a Child Custody Arrangement

To modify a child custody arrangement in Tennessee, the following requirements must be met:

  • A significant change in circumstances must have occurred since the original order was entered.
  • The change must affect the best interests of the child.
  • The court must find that the modification is in the best interests of the child.

Child Custody Lawsuit Timeline

The following is a general outline of the timeline for a child custody lawsuit in Tennessee:

Step Description Timeline
1. Filing of Complaint The parent seeking custody files a complaint with the court. 1-2 weeks
2. Service of Process The other parent is served with the complaint. 1-2 weeks
3. Temporary Custody Hearing The court holds a temporary custody hearing to determine temporary custody of the child. 1-2 weeks
4. Discovery The parties engage in discovery to gather evidence and information. 2-6 months
5. Trial The court holds a trial to determine permanent custody of the child. 2-6 months
6. Entry of Final Order The court enters a final order determining permanent custody of the child. 1-2 weeks

FAQs

Q: What is the primary consideration in determining child custody in Tennessee?

A: The primary consideration in determining child custody in Tennessee is the best interests of the child.

Q: Can I move out of state with my child if I have sole custody?

A: No, you cannot move out of state with your child without the permission of the other parent or the court.

Q: How do I modify a child custody arrangement in Tennessee?

A: To modify a child custody arrangement in Tennessee, you must show that there has been a significant change in circumstances since the original order was entered, and the change must affect the best interests of the child.

Q: What is the age at which a child can choose which parent to live with?

A: The age at which a child can choose which parent to live with is 12 years old.

Q: Can I file a child custody lawsuit if I am not married to the other parent?

A: Yes, you can file a child custody lawsuit if you are not married to the other parent, but you must have a legitimate reason for doing so, such as a significant change in circumstances or a dispute over custody.
