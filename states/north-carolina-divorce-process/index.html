Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in North Carolina

Are you considering filing for divorce in North Carolina? Understanding the state's divorce laws and requirements can help you navigate the process with confidence. In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through the divorce filing process and requirements in North Carolina, based on the state's laws and general legal principles.

Understanding North Carolina's Divorce Laws

The state of North Carolina has specific laws governing divorce, including residency requirements, waiting periods, and grounds for divorce. Here's a brief overview of what you need to know:

Residency Requirement

According to North Carolina law, at least one spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least 6 months before filing for divorce. This means that you must have established a permanent home in North Carolina and intend to remain there for the foreseeable future.

Waiting Period

North Carolina has a 90-day waiting period before a divorce can be finalized. This means that even if you and your spouse agree on all the terms of the divorce, you must wait at least 90 days before the divorce can be finalized.

Fault Grounds

North Carolina is a "no-fault" state, which means that you don't need to prove fault or wrongdoing to get a divorce. You can file for divorce on the grounds of "irreconcilable differences," which essentially means that you and your spouse are unable to resolve your differences and continue living together.

The Divorce Filing Process in North Carolina

Here's a step-by-step guide to the divorce filing process in North Carolina:

Step 1: Prepare the Divorce Papers

To start the divorce process, you'll need to prepare the necessary paperwork, including a complaint for divorce, a summons, and a certification of service. You can obtain these forms from the North Carolina courts or online through a divorce preparation service.

Step 2: File the Divorce Papers

Once you have the necessary paperwork, you'll need to file it with the court in the county where you reside. You'll need to pay a filing fee, which varies depending on the county.

Step 3: Serve the Divorce Papers

After filing the divorce papers, you'll need to serve your spouse with the summons and complaint. This can be done by a process server or by certified mail.

Step 4: Wait for the 90-Day Period

Once the divorce papers have been served, you'll need to wait at least 90 days before the divorce can be finalized. During this time, you can continue to work on the terms of the divorce, including property division, child custody, and spousal support.

Step 5: Finalize the Divorce

After the 90-day period has passed, you can proceed to finalize the divorce. You'll need to attend a hearing with a judge, where you'll discuss the terms of the divorce and answer any questions the judge may have.

FAQs

Here are some common questions about divorce in North Carolina:

Q: How long does it take to get a divorce in North Carolina?

A: The divorce process in North Carolina typically takes at least 90 days, but can take longer depending on the complexity of the case.

Q: Do I need to have a lawyer to get a divorce in North Carolina?

A: No, you don't need to have a lawyer to get a divorce in North Carolina. However, it's highly recommended that you consult with a lawyer to ensure that your rights are protected.

Q: Can I get a divorce if my spouse is not in North Carolina?

A: Yes, you can get a divorce in North Carolina even if your spouse is not in the state. However, you'll need to follow the state's residency requirements and waiting period.

Q: How is property divided in a North Carolina divorce?

A: In a North Carolina divorce, property is divided based on the principle of equitable distribution, which means that the court will divide the property in a fair and just manner, but not necessarily equally.

Q: Can I get spousal support in a North Carolina divorce?

A: Yes, you may be eligible for spousal support in a North Carolina divorce, depending on your financial situation and the ability of your spouse to pay.

Q: How do I file for divorce in North Carolina?

A: To file for divorce in North Carolina, you'll need to prepare the necessary paperwork, including a complaint for divorce, a summons, and a certification of service. You can obtain these forms from the North Carolina courts or online through a divorce preparation service.
