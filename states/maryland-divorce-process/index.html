Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Maryland: A Data-Driven Guide

Divorce can be a complex and emotionally challenging experience, but understanding the requirements and process can help alleviate some of the stress. In Maryland, the divorce laws are governed by the Maryland Code, Title 7. In this comprehensive guide, we will walk you through the divorce filing process and requirements in Maryland, based on the most up-to-date data and general legal principles.

Residency Requirement

In Maryland, one of the spouses must meet the residency requirement in order to file for divorce. The residency requirement is 6 months, which means that one of the spouses must have been a resident of Maryland for at least 6 months before filing for divorce. This requirement is typically met by showing proof of residency through a driver's license, utility bills, or other documents.

Waiting Period

After filing for divorce, there is a 90-day waiting period, also known as the "cooling-off" period. During this time, neither party can remarry, and there can be no changes to the divorce agreement. This waiting period is intended to give both parties time to reflect on their decision and make sure they are making the right choice.

Fault Grounds

Unlike some states, Maryland allows for fault grounds in divorce cases. This means that one spouse can claim that the other spouse is at fault for the breakdown of the marriage. Fault grounds can include adultery, cruelty, desertion, and other forms of misconduct. If a spouse is seeking a fault-based divorce, they must provide evidence to support their claim.

The Divorce Filing Process

The divorce filing process in Maryland typically begins with a Petition for Divorce, which is filed with the circuit court in the county where one of the spouses resides. The petition must include information about the marriage, the grounds for divorce, and any property or assets that are to be divided. The other spouse will then be served with a copy of the petition and will have the opportunity to respond.

Types of Divorce in Maryland

There are several types of divorce in Maryland, including:

  • Uncontested Divorce: A divorce where both spouses agree to the terms of the divorce.
  • Contested Divorce: A divorce where one or both spouses dispute the terms of the divorce.
  • No-Fault Divorce: A divorce where neither spouse is at fault for the breakdown of the marriage.
  • Fault-Based Divorce: A divorce where one spouse is at fault for the breakdown of the marriage.

FAQs

Here are some frequently asked questions about divorce in Maryland:

Q: How long does it take to get a divorce in Maryland?

A: The length of time it takes to get a divorce in Maryland can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. On average, it can take 6-12 months to finalize a divorce in Maryland.

Q: Do I need a lawyer to get a divorce in Maryland?

A: It is not strictly necessary to have a lawyer to get a divorce in Maryland, but it is highly recommended. A lawyer can help guide you through the process and ensure that your rights are protected.

Q: Can I get a divorce in Maryland if my spouse lives out of state?

A: Yes, you can get a divorce in Maryland if your spouse lives out of state. However, you will need to file for divorce in the county where you reside and follow the same procedures as if your spouse lived in Maryland.

Q: Can I change my mind and stop the divorce process in Maryland?

A: Yes, you can change your mind and stop the divorce process in Maryland, but you will need to file a motion to withdraw the petition for divorce with the court. This can be a complex process, and it is highly recommended that you consult with a lawyer before proceeding.

Q: How are assets divided in a divorce in Maryland?

A: In Maryland, assets are divided equitably, which means that the court will divide the assets in a way that is fair and reasonable, but not necessarily equal. The court will consider factors such as the length of the marriage, the earning capacity of each spouse, and the contributions of each spouse to the marriage.
