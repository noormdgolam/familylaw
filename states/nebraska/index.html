Nebraska Family Law: An Overview

Nebraska is a "no-fault" divorce state, which means that a divorce can be granted based on the grounds of "irreconcilable differences" rather than on fault-based grounds such as adultery or desertion. The Nebraska Revised Statutes (NRS) governs Family Law and provides a framework for the division of property, spousal support, child custody, and other related matters.

Statutes Governing Nebraska Family Law

  • NRS 42-301: Grounds for divorce
  • NRS 42-322: Residency requirements
  • NRS 42-335: Filing and service of divorce petitions
  • NRS 42-338: Temporary orders
  • NRS 42-339: Permanent orders
  • NRS 42-340: Division of property
  • NRS 42-341: Spousal support
  • NRS 42-342: Child custody

Common Legal Proceedings in Nebraska Family Court

The Nebraska Family Court system is designed to provide a fair and efficient process for resolving Family Law disputes. The following are common legal proceedings that may be encountered in a Nebraska Family Court:

Dissolution of Marriage (Divorce)

A dissolution of marriage is the process by which a marriage is terminated. This can be done through a contested or uncontested divorce. A contested divorce is one in which the parties cannot agree on the terms of the divorce, while an uncontested divorce is one in which the parties have reached a mutual agreement.

Child Custody and Visitation

Child custody and visitation refer to the rights and responsibilities of parents regarding their minor children. The primary considerations in determining child custody are the best interests of the child, including the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being.

Spousal Support (Alimony)

Spousal support is a financial support paid by one spouse to the other after a divorce. The purpose of spousal support is to ensure that both spouses have a similar standard of living after the divorce.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Navigating the Nebraska Family Court System

The following is a step-by-step guide to help individuals navigate the Nebraska Family Court system:

Step 1: Determine Your Grounds for Divorce

The first step in the divorce process is to determine your grounds for divorce. In Nebraska, a divorce can be granted based on the grounds of "irreconcilable differences." You will need to file a petition for divorce with the district court in the county where you reside.

Step 2: Gather Relevant Documents

Gathering relevant documents is essential in a divorce case. You will need to collect financial statements, tax returns, and other relevant documents related to your marriage and divorce. You may also need to obtain documents related to your children, such as birth certificates and educational records.

Step 3: Determine Child Custody and Visitation

Determining child custody and visitation is a critical aspect of the divorce process. You will need to consider the best interests of your child and develop a plan for custody and visitation that works for both parties.

Step 4: Negotiate Spousal Support (Alimony)

Negotiating spousal support can be a complex and contentious issue. You will need to consider your financial situation, your spouse's financial situation, and the length of your marriage to determine the appropriate level of spousal support.

Step 5: File a Petition for Dissolution of Marriage

The final step in the divorce process is to file a petition for dissolution of marriage. This petition will outline the grounds for your divorce, your desire for child custody and visitation, and your request for spousal support (if applicable).

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) about Nebraska Family Law

The following are frequently asked questions about Nebraska Family Law:

Q: What is the residency requirement for divorce in Nebraska?

A: To file for divorce in Nebraska, one of the parties must have resided in the state for at least one year prior to the filing of the petition for divorce.

Q: Can I file for divorce in Nebraska if I am not a resident?

A: Yes, you can file for divorce in Nebraska even if you are not a resident, but you must meet the residency requirement of one of the parties.

Q: How long does a divorce take in Nebraska?

A: The length of a divorce in Nebraska can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case and the level of agreement between the parties. On average, a divorce can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months to complete.

Q: Can I represent myself in a Nebraska Family Court case?

A: Yes, you can represent yourself in a Nebraska Family Court case, but it is highly recommended that you hire an attorney to ensure that your rights are protected and that the process is navigated efficiently and effectively.

Q: How do I determine child custody and visitation in Nebraska?

A: Determining child custody and visitation in Nebraska involves considering the best interests of the child. The court will consider factors such as the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being, as well as the stability and continuity of the child's care and living arrangements.

Q: Can I modify a divorce decree in Nebraska?

A: Yes, you can modify a divorce decree in Nebraska, but you must meet certain requirements and follow the proper procedures. It is recommended that you hire an attorney to assist you with modifying a divorce decree.
