Wisconsin Family Law and Divorce: A Comprehensive Guide

Overview of Wisconsin Family Law

Family law in Wisconsin is governed by state statutes, court rules, and case law, which provide a framework for resolving disputes related to marriage, divorce, child custody, child support, and property division. The Wisconsin family law system is designed to balance the rights of all parties involved, including parents, children, and spouses.

Grounds for Divorce in Wisconsin

The state of Wisconsin allows for both no-fault and fault-based divorces. To initiate a divorce in Wisconsin, one spouse must file a petition with the court, stating the grounds for the divorce. The grounds for divorce in Wisconsin include:

  • No-fault: Incompatibility, which is defined as a breakdown of the marriage relationship to the extent that the legitimate objects of the marriage cannot be preserved.
  • Fault: Adultery, willful and malicious desertion, or gross neglect of duty.

Child Custody in Wisconsin

Child custody in Wisconsin is determined by considering the best interests of the child. The court will consider several factors, including:

  • The wishes of the child's parents or legal custodians.
  • The wishes of the child, if the child is old enough to express a preference.
  • The child's relationship with each parent, siblings, and any other significant adults in the child's life.
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved.
  • The degree to which each parent has fulfilled their parental responsibilities.

The court may grant one or both parents physical custody of the child, or it may award joint legal custody, which gives both parents the right to make major decisions for the child.

Child Support in Wisconsin

Child support in Wisconsin is determined by the court based on the income of both parents and the needs of the child. The court will consider the following factors:

  • The income and earning capacity of each parent.
  • The needs of the child, including education expenses and medical expenses.
  • The standard of living of both parents.
  • The amount of time each parent spends with the child.

The court may order either parent to pay child support, and the amount of support will be based on a percentage of the non-custodial parent's income.

Property Division in Wisconsin

Property division in Wisconsin is governed by the state's equitable distribution statute, which requires the court to divide property fairly and equitably between spouses. The court will consider the following factors:

  • The length of the marriage.
  • The income and earning capacity of both spouses.
  • The contribution of each spouse to the acquisition and management of the property.
  • The extent to which one spouse has forgone personal career goals or education to care for the children or support the other spouse's career.

The court may award one spouse all or part of the property, or it may divide the property equally between the spouses.

Attorney Costs and Fees in Wisconsin

Attorney costs and fees in Wisconsin can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case, the experience of the attorney, and the location of the attorney. Clients can expect to pay hourly rates ranging from $200 to $500 per hour, with additional costs for court filing fees, expert witness fees, and other expenses.

The state of Wisconsin allows attorneys to charge a flat fee for certain services, such as drafting a divorce agreement or preparing a child custody plan. Clients should discuss their budget and fees with their attorney at the outset of the case.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Divorce in Wisconsin

The following is a step-by-step guide for divorce in Wisconsin:

  1. Meet with an attorney: Clients should meet with an experienced family law attorney to discuss their case and determine the best course of action.
  2. File a petition: The client's attorney will draft and file a petition for divorce with the court, stating the grounds for the divorce.
  3. Wait for a response: The other spouse will have an opportunity to respond to the petition and file a counter-petition.
  4. Exchange financial information: Both spouses will be required to exchange financial information, including income statements, expense reports, and asset valuations.
  5. Attend a hearing: The court will schedule a hearing to determine the issues of custody, support, and property division.
  6. Finalize the divorce: The court will issue a final judgment of divorce, which will be recorded with the county clerk's office.

FAQs About Family Law in Wisconsin

Here are some frequently asked questions about family law in Wisconsin:

  1. Can I get a divorce in Wisconsin without an attorney? While it is possible to represent oneself in court, it is highly recommended that clients seek the advice of an experienced family law attorney to ensure their rights are protected.
  2. How long does a divorce take in Wisconsin? The length of a divorce in Wisconsin can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. Clients can expect the process to take several months to a year or more.
  3. What is the cost of a divorce in Wisconsin? The cost of a divorce in Wisconsin can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case, the experience of the attorney, and the location of the attorney. Clients can expect to pay several thousand dollars or more.
  4. Can I modify a divorce decree in Wisconsin? Yes, clients can modify a divorce decree in Wisconsin by filing a motion with the court and demonstrating a change in circumstances that justifies the modification.

Conclusion

Family law in Wisconsin is governed by a complex set of statutes, court rules, and case law. Clients seeking a divorce in Wisconsin should seek the advice of an experienced family law attorney to ensure their rights are protected. This comprehensive guide provides a detailed overview of the divorce process in Wisconsin, including the laws and procedures that govern child custody, child support, property division, and divorce procedure.
