Family Law Statute of Limitations in Massachusetts: A Comprehensive Guide

Understanding the statute of limitations (SOL) in Massachusetts is crucial for individuals navigating family law disputes. This guide provides an in-depth look at the SOL for various family law actions in the Bay State.

Statute of Limitations Overview

The statute of limitations is the time period within which a plaintiff must file a lawsuit against a defendant after the cause of action has accrued. In Massachusetts, the SOL varies depending on the type of claim.

Personal Injury and Property Damage Claims

For personal injury and property damage claims, the SOL in Massachusetts is 2 years. This means that a plaintiff must file a lawsuit within 2 years from the date of the incident or the date the injury was discovered.

Personal Injury Claims

Personal injury claims encompass a wide range of cases, including:

  • Slip and fall accidents
  • Car accidents
  • Medical malpractice
  • Product liability

Property Damage Claims

Property damage claims involve damage to real or personal property, including:

  • Damage to a home or building
  • Damage to a vehicle
  • Theft or vandalism of property

Breach of Contract Claims

The SOL for breach of contract claims in Massachusetts is 4 years. This means that a plaintiff must file a lawsuit within 4 years from the date the contract was breached.

Family Law Actions

While the SOL for family law actions is not explicitly stated in the provided data, we can make some general observations. Family law actions often involve complex and time-sensitive issues, such as:

  • Divorce
  • Child custody and support
  • Adoption
  • Domestic violence

Exceptions to the Statute of Limitations

There are several exceptions to the SOL in Massachusetts, including:

  • Tolling of the SOL due to disability or mental incapacity
  • Tolling of the SOL due to concealment or fraud
  • Tolling of the SOL due to minority or infancy

Presumption of Death

In Massachusetts, there is a presumption of death after 7 years from the date of disappearance. This presumption can be rebutted with evidence of the person's survival.

Conclusion

The statute of limitations in Massachusetts is a critical concept for individuals navigating family law disputes. Understanding the SOL for various family law actions can help individuals plan and prepare for potential lawsuits.

FAQs

Q: What is the statute of limitations for a personal injury claim in Massachusetts?

A: The SOL for personal injury claims in Massachusetts is 2 years.

Q: Can the statute of limitations be tolled in Massachusetts?

A: Yes, the SOL can be tolled in Massachusetts due to disability or mental incapacity, concealment or fraud, or minority or infancy.

Q: What is the presumption of death in Massachusetts?

A: In Massachusetts, there is a presumption of death after 7 years from the date of disappearance. This presumption can be rebutted with evidence of the person's survival.

Q: Can I file a lawsuit after the statute of limitations has expired?

A: No, it is generally not possible to file a lawsuit after the SOL has expired. However, there may be exceptions or circumstances that allow for an extension of the SOL.

Q: How long does it take to resolve a family law case in Massachusetts?

A: The length of time it takes to resolve a family law case in Massachusetts can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. Typically, family law cases can take anywhere from several months to several years to resolve.
