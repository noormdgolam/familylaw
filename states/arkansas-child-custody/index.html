Arising from Divorce: Understanding Arkansas Child Custody Laws

In Arkansas, child custody is determined by the best interests of the child, as outlined in Arkansas Code Annotated § 9-13-101 et seq.

The Best Interests of the Child

  • The child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs.
  • The child's age, sex, and physical, mental, and emotional health.
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • The child's relationship with each parent and any other person who may significantly affect the child's best interests.
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to facilitate a close and continuing relationship between the child and the other parent.
  • The geographical location and distance between the parents' residences.
  • The child's wishes, if the child is of sufficient age and maturity to express reasoned and independent preferences as to which parent to live with.
  • The willingness and ability of each parent to provide a stable and secure environment for the child.
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to cooperate with the other parent in matters of mutual concern.
  • The history of domestic abuse, whether directed at or witnessed by the child.
  • The existence of a history or pattern of domestic violence within the family or between the parents, or between a parent and any individual who has a history of domestic violence against or a current or past relationship with a parent.
  • Any other factors that the court deems necessary to the determination.

Types of Child Custody in Arkansas

In Arkansas, there are two types of child custody:

Joint Custody: Both parents share physical and decision-making responsibilities for the child. This can be either joint legal or joint physical custody.

One parent has physical and decision-making responsibility for the child. This can be either sole legal or sole physical custody.

Common Legal Proceedings in Arkansas Child Custody Cases

Here are some common legal proceedings in Arkansas child custody cases:

Proceeding Description
Filing a Petition for Custody The first step in initiating a child custody case is to file a petition for custody with the court. This petition outlines the reasons why the petitioner believes they should have custody of the child.
Service of Process Once the petition is filed, the court will issue a summons, which must be served on the respondent. The respondent must then answer the petition within the specified time frame.
Discovery Discovery is the process of exchanging information and evidence between the parties. This can include requests for documents, depositions, and other forms of evidence.
Motions and Hearings Motions and hearings are used to resolve issues and disputes that arise during the case. These can include motions for temporary custody, motions to modify custody, and hearings on the merits of the case.
Custody Trial If the parties are unable to reach an agreement, the case will go to trial. At the trial, the parties will present their evidence and arguments to the court, which will then make a decision on custody.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Handling an Arkansas Child Custody Case

Here are some step-by-step guidelines for handling an Arkansas child custody case:

  1. Filing a Petition for Custody: The first step in initiating a child custody case is to file a petition for custody with the court.
  2. Service of Process: Once the petition is filed, the court will issue a summons, which must be served on the respondent.
  3. Discovery: Discovery is the process of exchanging information and evidence between the parties.
  4. Motions and Hearings: Motions and hearings are used to resolve issues and disputes that arise during the case.
  5. Custody Trial: If the parties are unable to reach an agreement, the case will go to trial.
  6. Contempt and Enforcement: If one party fails to comply with the court's order, the other party can file a motion for contempt and enforcement.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q: What is the best way to determine the best interests of the child in an Arkansas child custody case?

A: The best way to determine the best interests of the child is to consider the factors outlined in Arkansas Code Annotated § 9-13-101 et seq.

Q: Can a parent who has been convicted of a crime be awarded custody in an Arkansas child custody case?

A: No, a parent who has been convicted of a crime may not be awarded custody in an Arkansas child custody case unless the court finds that the parent's crime does not pose a risk to the child's safety and well-being.

Q: Can a child's wishes be considered in an Arkansas child custody case?

A: Yes, a child's wishes can be considered in an Arkansas child custody case if the child is of sufficient age and maturity to express reasoned and independent preferences as to which parent to live with.

Q: Can an Arkansas child custody case be appealed?

A: Yes, an Arkansas child custody case can be appealed to the Arkansas Court of Appeals and the Arkansas Supreme Court.
