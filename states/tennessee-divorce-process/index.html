Tennessee Divorce Process: A Comprehensive Guide to Family Law, Divorce, and Child Custody

Marital Property in Tennessee Divorce

In Tennessee, marital property refers to all assets acquired during the marriage, including real estate, personal property, and financial assets. According to Tennessee Code Annotated (TCA) § 36-4-121(a), marital property includes:

  • Real estate owned during the marriage
  • Personal property acquired during the marriage
  • Financial assets, such as bank accounts and investments
  • Business interests and professional practices

When determining the division of marital property in a Tennessee divorce, the court considers the following factors:

  • The length of the marriage
  • The income and earning capacity of each spouse
  • The educational background and job skills of each spouse
  • The contributions of each spouse to the marriage, including homemaker services
  • The value of the marital property

Grounds for Divorce in Tennessee

In Tennessee, there are two primary grounds for divorce: fault-based and no-fault. According to TCA § 36-4-101, a fault-based divorce is granted when the court finds that one spouse has committed one of the following acts:

  • Adultery
  • Desertion
  • Abuse
  • Bigamy
  • Crimes against nature

A no-fault divorce, on the other hand, is granted when the court finds that the marriage is "irreconcilably broken" and that there is no reasonable expectation of reconciliation. According to TCA § 36-4-103, a no-fault divorce may be granted after a six-month waiting period.

Child Custody in Tennessee Divorce

In Tennessee, child custody refers to the physical and decision-making responsibilities of raising a minor child. According to TCA § 36-6-106, the court's primary consideration in determining child custody is the "best interests of the child."

The court considers the following factors when determining child custody:

  • The love, affection, and bonding between the child and each parent
  • The ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs
  • The stability and continuity of the child's home environment
  • The geographic distance between the parents' residences
  • The child's wishes, if the child is old enough to express a preference

There are two primary types of child custody arrangements: joint custody and sole custody. Joint custody refers to a shared physical and decision-making responsibility between both parents, while sole custody refers to a single parent having physical and decision-making responsibility.

Child Support in Tennessee Divorce

In Tennessee, child support refers to the financial support provided by one parent to the other for the benefit of the child. According to TCA § 36-5-101, the court's primary consideration in determining child support is the "best interests of the child."

The court considers the following factors when determining child support:

  • The income and earning capacity of each parent
  • The number of children and their ages
  • The needs and expenses of the child
  • The standard of living the child would have enjoyed if the parents were still married

Tennessee uses the Income Shares Model to calculate child support, which takes into account the income of both parents and the number of children.

Tennessee Divorce Process: Step-by-Step Guidance

The Tennessee divorce process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Separation: The couple separates and begins living apart.
  2. Filing: One spouse files a complaint for divorce with the court, which includes the grounds for divorce and any other relevant information.
  3. Serving: The other spouse is served with the complaint, which gives them notice of the divorce proceeding.
  4. Pleading: The other spouse files a response to the complaint, which may include counterclaims or defenses.
  5. Discovery: The parties engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and evidence related to the divorce.
  6. Mediation: The parties may engage in mediation to resolve disputes and reach a settlement.
  7. Trial: If the parties cannot reach a settlement, the case goes to trial, where the court makes a final decision.
  8. Judgment: The court enters a final judgment, which includes the terms of the divorce, including property division, child custody, and child support.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) about Tennessee Divorce

Here are some frequently asked questions about Tennessee divorce:

  • Q: How long does a Tennessee divorce take?
    • A: The length of a Tennessee divorce can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case and the level of litigation. On average, a Tennessee divorce can take 6-12 months.
  • Q: Do I need an attorney for a Tennessee divorce?
    • A: Yes, it is highly recommended to have an attorney represent you in a Tennessee divorce. An attorney can help you navigate the complex divorce process and protect your rights.
  • Q: Can I get a divorce in Tennessee if I am a resident of another state?
    • A: Yes, you can get a divorce in Tennessee if you are a resident of another state. However, you must meet the residency requirements of the state, which is typically 6-12 months.
  • Q: How is child custody determined in Tennessee divorce?
    • A: Child custody is determined based on the "best interests of the child" and involves considering factors such as the love, affection, and bonding between the child and each parent.
  • Q: How is child support calculated in Tennessee divorce?
    • A: Child support is calculated using the Income Shares Model, which takes into account the income of both parents and the number of children.
