Understanding Family Law Statute of Limitations in Missouri

Missouri has a unique set of laws governing family law, including divorce, child custody, and property division. However, in this guide, we will focus on the statute of limitations (SOL) that applies to various family law-related claims. By understanding these time limits, individuals can navigate the complexities of Missouri family law with greater confidence.

Missouri Statute of Limitations: A Primer

The statute of limitations is a legal doctrine that restricts the time period within which a claim must be brought to court. In Missouri, the SOL varies depending on the type of claim, ranging from 2 to 6 years. It's essential to note that these time limits can be subject to tolling or suspension, depending on the specific circumstances of the case.

Personal Injury and Property Damage Claims

Missouri has a relatively short statute of limitations for personal injury and property damage claims. In both cases, the SOL is 3 years from the date of the accident or discovery of the injury.

Personal Injury Claims: 3 years from the date of the accident or discovery of the injury.

Property Damage Claims: 2 years from the date of the damage or discovery of the damage.

Breach of Contract Claims

Breach of contract claims in Missouri have a longer statute of limitations, with a 6-year SOL. This means that individuals have six years from the date of the breach to file a lawsuit.

Breach of Contract Claims: 6 years from the date of the breach.

Family Law Claims in Missouri

Family law claims in Missouri, such as divorce, child custody, and property division, do not have a specific statute of limitations. However, these claims are often filed concurrently with other family law claims, which can include personal injury, property damage, or breach of contract claims.

Important Considerations

It's essential to note that the statute of limitations can be tolled or suspended in certain circumstances, such as:

  • Minority or disability: In cases where the injured party is a minor or disabled, the SOL may be tolled until they reach the age of majority or regain capacity.
  • Concealment: If the defendant conceals their identity or involvement in the claim, the SOL may be tolled until the claimant discovers the truth.
  • Intervening events: In some cases, the SOL may be tolled or suspended due to intervening events, such as a change in the law or a new discovery.

Conclusion

Understanding the statute of limitations in Missouri is crucial for individuals navigating family law claims. By knowing the time limits for personal injury, property damage, and breach of contract claims, individuals can ensure that they file their claims within the applicable SOL. Remember to consult with an attorney to determine the specific SOL that applies to your case.
