Family Law Statute of Limitations in New Hampshire: A Comprehensive Guide

When dealing with family law matters in New Hampshire, understanding the statute of limitations (SOL) is crucial. This guide will provide an in-depth overview of the SOLs applicable to various family law actions in the state, helping you navigate the complex landscape of New Hampshire's laws.

Understanding the Statute of Limitations

The statute of limitations is a legal deadline by which a plaintiff must file a lawsuit against a defendant. If the deadline is missed, the plaintiff may be barred from pursuing the case. In New Hampshire, the SOLs for various family law actions are as follows:

Key Statutes of Limitations in New Hampshire

Action Statute of Limitations
Personal Injury 4 years (RSA 508:4)
Property Damage 6 years (RSA 508:4)
Breach of Contract 3 years (RSA 508:4)
Custody Disputes Generally, no SOL applies, but the court may consider the child's best interests and the passage of time.
Spousal Support No SOL applies, but the court may consider the parties' changing circumstances and the passage of time.
Divorce No SOL applies, but the court may consider the parties' changing circumstances and the passage of time.

Personal Injury Claims

For personal injury claims in New Hampshire, the SOL is 4 years. This means that a plaintiff has 4 years from the date of the injury to file a lawsuit against the defendant. If the plaintiff fails to file within this timeframe, they may be barred from pursuing the case.

Examples of Personal Injury Claims

  • Medical malpractice
  • Car accidents
  • Slip and fall accidents
  • Product liability

Property Damage Claims

For property damage claims in New Hampshire, the SOL is 6 years. This means that a plaintiff has 6 years from the date of the damage to file a lawsuit against the defendant. If the plaintiff fails to file within this timeframe, they may be barred from pursuing the case.

Examples of Property Damage Claims

  • Damage to real property
  • Damage to personal property
  • Environmental contamination

Breach of Contract Claims

For breach of contract claims in New Hampshire, the SOL is 3 years. This means that a plaintiff has 3 years from the date of the breach to file a lawsuit against the defendant. If the plaintiff fails to file within this timeframe, they may be barred from pursuing the case.

Examples of Breach of Contract Claims

  • Failed business agreements
  • Failed lease agreements
  • Failed employment contracts

Custody Disputes

Custody disputes in New Hampshire are subject to the best interests of the child standard. While there is no SOL applicable to custody disputes, the court may consider the child's best interests and the passage of time when making a decision.

Factors Considered in Custody Disputes

  • The child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs
  • The child's relationship with each parent
  • The parents' ability to communicate and cooperate
  • The child's stability and continuity

Spousal Support

Spousal support in New Hampshire is subject to the parties' changing circumstances and the passage of time. While there is no SOL applicable to spousal support, the court may consider the following factors when making a decision:

Factors Considered in Spousal Support

  • The parties' income and earning capacity
  • The parties' financial resources and expenses
  • The parties' standard of living during the marriage
  • The length of the marriage

Divorce

Divorce in New Hampshire is subject to the parties' changing circumstances and the passage of time. While there is no SOL applicable to divorce, the court may consider the following factors when making a decision:

Factors Considered in Divorce

  • The parties' income and earning capacity
  • The parties' financial resources and expenses
  • The parties' standard of living during the marriage
  • The length of the marriage
