Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Nebraska: A Comprehensive Guide

Understanding the complex and often emotional process of child custody can be daunting, especially for those navigating the Nebraska court system. This guide aims to provide a data-driven overview of the key factors, laws, and guidelines governing child custody in the Cornhusker State.

Primary Custody Factor: Best Interests of the Child

In Nebraska, the primary factor in determining child custody is the best interests of the child. This principle is enshrined in the Nebraska Revised Statutes, Section 42-326.

When determining the best interests of the child, the court will consider factors such as:

  • Each parent's ability to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs
  • The child's age and needs
  • The parents' physical and mental health
  • The parents' ability to cooperate and communicate with each other
  • Any history of domestic violence or abuse
  • The child's wishes, if the child is old enough to express a preference

Joint Custody Presumption

Nebraska law also includes a joint custody presumption, which means that the court will assume that joint custody is in the best interests of the child unless one or both parents demonstrate that it is not.

The Nebraska Revised Statutes, Section 42-325, states that the court shall order joint custody unless there is clear and convincing evidence that joint custody would not be in the best interests of the child.

No Set Age for Child's Choice

Unlike some other states, Nebraska does not have a specific age at which a child can choose their own custodian. Instead, the court will consider the child's wishes as one of many factors in determining the best interests of the child.

Types of Custody Arrangements

Nebraska courts may award one or both parents custody of the child, depending on the circumstances. The following are the main types of custody arrangements:

  • Physical Custody: Determines which parent the child will live with
  • Legal Custody: Determines which parent has the right and responsibility to make decisions about the child's education, healthcare, and welfare
  • Sole Custody: Awards one parent physical and legal custody
  • Joint Custody: Awards both parents physical and legal custody, with either equal or shared responsibilities
  • Split Custody: Awards one parent physical and legal custody of one or more children, while the other parent has physical and legal custody of the remaining children

Steps to Obtain Custody of a Child in Nebraska

If you are seeking to obtain custody of a child in Nebraska, follow these steps:

  1. Contact a qualified attorney to guide you through the process
  2. File a petition for custody with the court
  3. Attend a hearing to present your case to the judge
  4. Wait for the judge's decision and follow the court's order

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the primary factor in determining child custody in Nebraska?

A: The primary factor in determining child custody in Nebraska is the best interests of the child.

Q: Can I choose my own custodian if I am 18 or older?

A: No, Nebraska does not have a specific age at which a child can choose their own custodian. The court will consider the child's wishes as one of many factors in determining the best interests of the child.

Q: What is joint custody, and how does it work in Nebraska?

A: Joint custody is a custody arrangement in which both parents share physical and legal custody of the child. In Nebraska, the court shall assume joint custody is in the best interests of the child unless otherwise demonstrated.

Q: Can I modify my child custody arrangement in Nebraska?

A: Yes, you can modify your child custody arrangement in Nebraska, but you will need to file a motion with the court and demonstrate a change in circumstances that warrants a modification.

Q: What is the process for enforcing a child custody order in Nebraska?

A: If you need to enforce a child custody order in Nebraska, you will need to file a motion with the court and provide evidence of the other parent's non-compliance. The court may then issue a contempt citation, which can be enforced through the Nebraska Department of Corrections.
