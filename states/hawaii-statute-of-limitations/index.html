Statutes of Limitations in Hawaii Family Law

The Hawaii Revised Statutes outline specific time limits for initiating various family law actions. Familiarizing yourself with these statutes is crucial to avoid missing crucial deadlines. Here are some key statutes of limitations in Hawaii family law:

  • Hawaiian Marriage Act (HRS § 572-1 to 572-11): 3-year statute of limitations for contesting a marriage license
  • Annulment (HRS § 580-1 to 580-6): 1-year statute of limitations from the date of the marriage
  • Divorce (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): No statute of limitations for filing a divorce complaint. However, parties must comply with the 90-day waiting period after the divorce complaint is served.
  • Child Custody (HRS § 584-1 to 584-14): No statute of limitations for filing a child custody action. However, courts consider the best interests of the child when making custody decisions.
  • Child Support (HRS § 584-15 to 584-23): No statute of limitations for filing a child support action. However, courts may consider the amount of support and the ability of the parties to pay.
  • Property Division (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): No statute of limitations for filing a property division action. However, courts must consider the parties' property rights and the best interests of the family.

Common Legal Proceedings in Hawaii Family Law

Family law proceedings in Hawaii can be complex and emotionally challenging. Understanding the common legal procedures can help you navigate the process. Here are some common legal proceedings in Hawaii family law:

  • Divorce Complaint (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): A party initiates a divorce action by filing a complaint with the court, which must contain the grounds for divorce and other relevant information.
  • Answer and Counterclaims (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): The responding party files an answer to the complaint, which may include counterclaims and allegations.
  • Discovery (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): Both parties engage in discovery, which includes exchanging information, interrogatories, and requests for production of documents.
  • Motion for Temporary Relief (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): A party may file a motion for temporary relief, such as temporary custody or support, to address an urgent issue.
  • Trial (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): The court holds a trial to determine the issues in dispute, including custody, support, and property division.
  • Appeal (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): A party may appeal a court decision to a higher court, such as the Hawaii Intermediate Court of Appeals or the Hawaii Supreme Court.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Hawaii Family Law

Family law proceedings in Hawaii can be complex and emotionally challenging. Here is a step-by-step guide to help you navigate the process:

  1. Consult with an Attorney (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): Schedule a consultation with a family law attorney to discuss your case and determine the best course of action.
  2. File a Divorce Complaint (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): If you decide to proceed with a divorce, your attorney will help you file a complaint with the court.
  3. Engage in Discovery (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): Both parties will engage in discovery, which includes exchanging information, interrogatories, and requests for production of documents.
  4. File a Motion for Temporary Relief (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): If you need urgent relief, such as temporary custody or support, file a motion with the court.
  5. Attend a Trial (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): The court will hold a trial to determine the issues in dispute, including custody, support, and property division.
  6. Review and Appeal a Court Decision (HRS § 580-47 to 580-55): If you disagree with the court's decision, you may appeal to a higher court.

FAQs in Hawaii Family Law

Here are some frequently asked questions in Hawaii family law:

  • Q: What is the waiting period for divorce in Hawaii?

    A: The 90-day waiting period for divorce in Hawaii begins after the divorce complaint is served.

  • Q: How do I obtain a divorce in Hawaii?

    A: To obtain a divorce in Hawaii, you must file a complaint with the court, which must contain the grounds for divorce and other relevant information.

  • Q: Can I modify a court order in Hawaii?

    A: Yes, you can modify a court order in Hawaii by filing a motion with the court and demonstrating a change in circumstances.

  • Q: How do I calculate child support in Hawaii?

    A: Child support in Hawaii is calculated using a formula that considers the parents' incomes, the number of children, and other relevant factors.

  • Q: Can I appeal a court decision in Hawaii?

    A: Yes, you can appeal a court decision in Hawaii to a higher court, such as the Hawaii Intermediate Court of Appeals or the Hawaii Supreme Court.
