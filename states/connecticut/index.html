Connecticut Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

 

Families in Connecticut face unique challenges when navigating the complex and often contentious world of family law. Whether you are considering divorce or seeking guidance on child custody arrangements, it is essential to understand the laws and procedures that govern family law in Connecticut. State-Specific Statutes

 

Connecticut's family law statutes are governed by Chapter 815 of the Connecticut General Statutes. Some key statutes include:

  • Sec. 46b-14. (Formerly Sec. 46b-59). Dissolution of marriage, legal separation, and annulment. (a) A judgment dissolving a marriage or declaring a legal separation may be rendered in a civil action. (b) The action may be brought in the judicial district where the plaintiff resides or in the judicial district where the parties last resided together.
  • Sec. 46b-56. (Formerly Sec. 46b-81). Custody of minor children. (a) In any proceeding where the custody of a minor child is at issue, the court shall consider the best interests of the child.
  • Sec. 46b-56a. (Formerly Sec. 46b-81a). Visitation rights of parents. (a) In any proceeding where the custody of a minor child is at issue, the court shall consider the visitation rights of each parent.

 

Common Legal Proceedings

 

When navigating family law in Connecticut, you may encounter several common legal proceedings, including:

  • Dissolution of Marriage: A dissolution of marriage is a legal process that terminates a marriage. In Connecticut, dissolution of marriage is governed by Sec. 46b-14.
  • Legal Separation: A legal separation is a voluntary agreement between spouses to live separately while remaining married. Connecticut law recognizes legal separation, which is governed by Sec. 46b-14.
  • Annulment: An annulment is a legal declaration that a marriage is invalid. In Connecticut, annulment is governed by Sec. 46b-14.
  • Custody and Visitation: Custody and visitation proceedings involve determining the rights and responsibilities of parents regarding their minor children. In Connecticut, custody and visitation are governed by Secs. 46b-56 and 46b-56a.
  • Child Support: Child support proceedings involve determining the amount of financial support one parent must pay to the other parent for the benefit of their minor children. In Connecticut, child support is governed by Sec. 46b-84.
  • Alimony: Alimony proceedings involve determining the amount of financial support one spouse must pay to the other spouse. In Connecticut, alimony is governed by Sec. 46b-82.

Step-by-Step Guidance

 

Here is a step-by-step guide to navigating family law in Connecticut:

  1. File a Complaint: If you are seeking a dissolution of marriage, legal separation, or annulment, you must file a complaint with the court. If you are seeking custody and visitation, child support, or alimony, you must also file a complaint.
  2. Court-Ordered Mediation: Many courts in Connecticut require parties to participate in court-ordered mediation before proceeding to trial.
  3. Discovery: During the discovery process, both parties will exchange information and documents related to the case.
  4. Pre-Trial Conference: Before trial, both parties will attend a pre-trial conference to discuss the case and any outstanding issues.
  5. Trial: If the case cannot be resolved through mediation or settlement, it will proceed to trial.
  6. Judgment: After trial, the court will render a judgment in the case.

 

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

 

Here are some frequently asked questions (FAQs) about family law in Connecticut:

  • Q: What is the minimum residency requirement for a dissolution of marriage in Connecticut? A: The minimum residency requirement is 12 months.
  • Q: Can I file for a dissolution of marriage without an attorney? A: Yes, but it is highly recommended that you seek the advice of an attorney to ensure your rights are protected.
  • Q: What is the difference between custody and visitation? A: Custody refers to the rights and responsibilities of parents regarding their minor children, while visitation refers to the schedule and manner in which parents spend time with their minor children.
  • Q: How is child support calculated in Connecticut? A: Child support is calculated based on the income of both parents, the number of children, and other factors.
  • Q: Can I modify a custody or visitation order in Connecticut? A: Yes, but you must demonstrate a substantial change in circumstances and show that the modification is in the best interests of the child.
