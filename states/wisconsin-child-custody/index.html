Wisconsin Child Custody Laws

In Wisconsin, child custody is governed by the Wisconsin Statutes, specifically Chapter 767. The primary goal of the court is to make decisions that prioritize the best interests of the child.

Types of Child Custody in Wisconsin

There are two types of child custody in Wisconsin:

  • Physical Custody: Refers to the physical care and supervision of the child. This can be sole or joint custody.
  • Legal Custody: Refers to the decision-making authority regarding the child's upbringing, education, and healthcare.

Wisconsin's Best Interests Standard

The court will consider the following factors to determine the best interests of the child in a child custody dispute:

Factor Description
1. The love, affection, and other emotional ties between each parent and the child The court will consider the emotional bond between each parent and the child.
2. The capacity and disposition of each parent to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care, and other necessities The court will assess each parent's ability to provide for the child's basic needs.
3. The quality of each parent's relationship with the child The court will evaluate the quality of each parent's interactions with the child.
4. The geographical location and any special needs of the child The court will consider the child's geographical location and any special needs.
5. The willingness and ability of each parent to cooperate with the other parent in the exercise of parental responsibilities The court will evaluate each parent's willingness and ability to cooperate with the other parent.
6. The domestic abuse, as defined in s. 813.12 (1) (a) 2., of either parent towards the other parent or the child The court will consider allegations of domestic abuse.

Child Custody Proceedings in Wisconsin

Child custody proceedings in Wisconsin typically begin with a petition for divorce or paternity filed with the court. The court will then schedule a hearing to determine the best interests of the child.

Typical Child Custody Proceedings

The following are the typical steps involved in a child custody proceeding in Wisconsin:

  • Initial Hearing: The court will hold an initial hearing to determine temporary custody and support arrangements.
  • Discovery: Both parties will engage in the discovery process, which involves exchanging information and evidence relevant to the case.
  • Custody Evaluation: A custody evaluator may be appointed by the court to conduct an evaluation of the parties and their relationship with the child.
  • Final Hearing: The court will hold a final hearing to determine the permanent custody arrangement.

FAQs about Child Custody in Wisconsin

Here are some frequently asked questions about child custody in Wisconsin:

Q: What is the best interests standard in Wisconsin?

A: The best interests standard in Wisconsin is a comprehensive evaluation of the child's needs and circumstances, considering factors such as the child's love, affection, and emotional ties with each parent, the quality of each parent's relationship with the child, and the willingness and ability of each parent to cooperate with the other parent.

Q: Can I change the custody arrangement after a divorce?

Q: Yes, you can change the custody arrangement after a divorce, but you will need to file a motion with the court and provide evidence to support the change.

Q: How do I choose a child custody attorney in Wisconsin?

A: To choose a child custody attorney in Wisconsin, research and interview several attorneys, considering factors such as experience, reputation, and communication style.

Q: What is the cost of hiring a child custody attorney in Wisconsin?

A: The cost of hiring a child custody attorney in Wisconsin can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the attorney's experience, but estimated costs range from $5,700 to $12,000 or more.

Q: Can I represent myself in a child custody proceeding in Wisconsin?

A: Yes, you can represent yourself in a child custody proceeding in Wisconsin, but it is highly recommended that you hire an attorney to ensure your rights are protected and your interests are represented.

Q: How long does a child custody proceeding take in Wisconsin?

A: The length of a child custody proceeding in Wisconsin can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule, but it can take several months to a year or more to resolve.

Q: Can I get custody of my child if I am not the biological parent?

A: Yes, you can get custody of a child if you are not the biological parent, but you will need to demonstrate that it is in the child's best interests to award you custody.
