Colorado Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce, Child Custody, & More

Introduction to Colorado Family Law

In Colorado, family law governs the relationships and rights of family members, including parents, children, spouses, and other relatives. The state's family law statutes, found in the Colorado Revised Statutes (CRS), outline procedures for divorce, child custody, child support, and other family-related matters. As a highly experienced family law attorney and legal consultant, this article provides an in-depth guide to Colorado family law, including state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance.

Divorce in Colorado

Grounds for Divorce

Colorado is a no-fault divorce state, which means that one spouse does not need to prove fault or misconduct by the other spouse to obtain a divorce. The grounds for divorce in Colorado include:

  • Irreconcilable differences
  • Dishonesty or infidelity
  • Abuse or neglect
  • Separation for at least 20 months

Divorce Proceedings

The divorce process in Colorado typically involves the following steps:

  1. Filing a Petition for Dissolution of Marriage: One spouse (the petitioner) files a petition with the court, stating the grounds for divorce and requesting relief.

  2. Serving the Other Spouse: The petitioner must serve the other spouse (the respondent) with a copy of the petition and a summons to appear in court.

  3. Respondent's Response: The respondent may file a response to the petition, asserting their position on the grounds for divorce and any other relevant issues.

  4. Discovery: Both spouses may engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and evidence relevant to the divorce proceedings.

  5. Mediation or Negotiation: The spouses may attempt to reach a settlement through mediation or negotiation.

  6. Court Hearing: If the spouses cannot reach a settlement, the court will hold a hearing to resolve any outstanding issues.

  7. Divorce Decree: The court will issue a divorce decree, outlining the terms of the divorce, including property division, child custody, and child support.

Child Custody in Colorado

Types of Custody

Colorado family law recognizes two types of custody: legal custody and physical custody.

  • Legal Custody: Refers to the right to make important decisions about a child's life, including education, healthcare, and religion.

  • Physical Custody: Refers to the physical care and supervision of a child, including where the child lives and how often the other parent spends time with the child.

Custody Proceedings

The custody process in Colorado typically involves the following steps:

  1. Initial Custody Order: The court will issue an initial custody order, which may be temporary or permanent.

  2. Custody Evaluation: The court may order a custody evaluation, which involves a neutral third-party assessing the parents' fitness and the child's best interests.

  3. Custody Hearing: The court will hold a hearing to determine the custody arrangement, considering factors such as the child's best interests, the parents' fitness, and any history of abuse or neglect.

  4. Custody Decree: The court will issue a custody decree, outlining the terms of custody, including the allocation of legal and physical custody.

Child Support in Colorado

Child Support Guidelines

Colorado child support guidelines are found in CRS 14-10-115. The guidelines consider factors such as:

  • The parents' income

  • The number of children

  • The children's needs

  • The parents' expenses

Child Support Proceedings

The child support process in Colorado typically involves the following steps:

  1. Filing a Child Support Petition: One parent (the petitioner) files a child support petition with the court, requesting child support from the other parent (the respondent).

  2. Respondent's Response: The respondent may file a response to the petition, asserting their position on child support and any other relevant issues.

  3. Child Support Calculation: The court will calculate child support using the guidelines, considering the factors outlined above.

  4. Child Support Order: The court will issue a child support order, outlining the amount of child support and any other relevant terms.

Property Division in Colorado

Community Property

Colorado is a community property state, which means that all property acquired during marriage is considered community property, owned equally by both spouses.

Separate Property

Separate property includes property acquired before marriage, property acquired by gift or inheritance, and property acquired by one spouse during marriage with separate funds.

Property Division Proceedings

The property division process in Colorado typically involves the following steps:

  1. Filing a Petition for Dissolution of Marriage: One spouse (the petitioner) files a petition with the court, requesting property division.

  2. Discovery: Both spouses may engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and evidence relevant to property division.

  3. Mediation or Negotiation: The spouses may attempt to reach a settlement through mediation or negotiation.

  4. Court Hearing: If the spouses cannot reach a settlement, the court will hold a hearing to resolve any outstanding issues.

  5. Property Division Order: The court will issue a property division order, outlining the terms of property division, including the allocation of community and separate property.

Attorney Costs in Colorado

The cost of hiring an attorney in Colorado can vary widely, depending on factors such as:

  • The complexity of the case

  • The attorney's experience and qualifications

  • The attorney's hourly rate

Average hourly rates for family law attorneys in Colorado range from $200 to $500 per hour. Total costs for a divorce case can range from $2,000 to $20,000 or more.
