Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Nebraska: A Comprehensive Guide

In the state of Nebraska, divorce can be a complex and overwhelming process, especially for those unfamiliar with the requirements and procedures involved. As an editorial assistant, it's essential to provide a data-driven guide that educates readers on the necessary steps to take when filing for divorce in Nebraska. This comprehensive guide will cover the residency requirement, waiting period, fault grounds, and other essential aspects of the divorce process.

Residency Requirement in Nebraska

According to Nebraska state law, at least one spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least one year before filing for divorce. This means that either you or your spouse must have lived in Nebraska for a minimum of 12 months before initiating the divorce process.

Waiting Period in Nebraska

Nebraska has a mandatory waiting period of 30 days before a divorce can be finalized. This allows both parties time to reconcile or makes arrangements for child custody, spousal support, and property division.

Fault Grounds in Nebraska

Nebraska allows fault grounds for divorce, which means that one spouse can claim that the other spouse is responsible for the breakdown of the marriage. Common fault grounds include adultery, abandonment, and cruelty. However, it's essential to note that proving fault grounds can be challenging and may require substantial evidence.

Types of Divorce in Nebraska

There are two main types of divorce in Nebraska: contested and uncontested. A contested divorce occurs when both parties cannot agree on the terms of the divorce, which may require a trial to resolve the issues. An uncontested divorce occurs when both parties agree on the terms of the divorce, making the process less complicated and less expensive.

Divorce Filing Process in Nebraska

Step 1: File the Petition for Divorce

The first step in filing for divorce in Nebraska is to file a petition for divorce with the district court in the county where one of the spouses resides. The petition must include basic information about the spouses, including their names, addresses, and ages.

Step 2: Serve the Other Spouse

Once the petition is filed, the other spouse must be served with a copy of the petition. This can be done through a process server or by certified mail.

Step 3: Wait for the Waiting Period

After serving the other spouse, the waiting period begins. This is a mandatory 30-day period during which both parties must wait before the divorce can be finalized.

Step 4: Complete the Divorce Proceedings

During the waiting period, both parties can file additional documents, such as a response to the petition or a counterclaim. They can also begin making arrangements for child custody, spousal support, and property division.

Divorce Documents in Nebraska

Some essential divorce documents in Nebraska include:

  • Petition for Divorce
  • Response to Petition
  • Counterclaim
  • Divorce Decree
  • Marital Settlement Agreement

Child Custody and Support in Nebraska

When it comes to child custody and support, Nebraska courts prioritize the best interests of the child. Custody can be joint or sole, and support is usually determined based on the income of both parents.

Property Division in Nebraska

Nebraska is an equitable distribution state, which means that property is divided fairly but not necessarily equally. Factors considered in property division include the length of the marriage, the income of both spouses, and the needs of both parties.

Spousal Support in Nebraska

Nebraska courts can order spousal support, also known as alimony, in certain cases. Factors considered in determining spousal support include the length of the marriage, the income of both spouses, and the needs of both parties.

Divorce Filing Fees in Nebraska

The cost of filing for divorce in Nebraska varies depending on the court and the complexity of the case. On average, the filing fee for a divorce petition is around $350.
