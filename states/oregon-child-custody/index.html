Oregon Child Custody Laws and Guidelines

Compiled by the Legal Data Aggregation Team

Last updated: July 2026

Disclaimer: This guide aggregates public state laws and guidelines for Oregon. We are not a law firm. This is not legal advice.

The state of Oregon operates under a rebuttable presumption that joint custody is in the best interest of the child, and that both parents should share joint physical and legal custody, unless the court finds that joint custody is not in the best interest of the child. In Oregon, family law is governed by the Oregon Revised Statutes (ORS) Chapter 107, specifically ORS 107.105 to 107.705.

Understanding Oregon Child Custody Law

Child custody laws in Oregon prioritize the child's best interest and focus on promoting a stable and loving environment for the child. The court will consider the following factors when determining child custody:

  • The wishes of the child's parents;
  • The wishes of the child, considering the child's age and maturity;
  • The child's relationship with each parent and any other person who may significantly affect the child's best interest;
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community;
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved;
  • The capacity and disposition of each parent to provide a stable, loving, and healthy environment for the child;
  • The level of cooperation between the parents and whether the parents can cooperate in the decision-making process regarding the child's needs;
  • The willingness and ability of each parent to facilitate a close and continuing relationship between the child and the other parent;
  • Any history of or potential for child abuse or neglect in either parent's home or any other person's home who may significantly affect the child's best interest;
  • Any other factors the court determines are relevant to the child's best interest.

Oregon's Child Custody Process

The process of obtaining child custody in Oregon typically involves the following steps:

  1. Initial Filing: One parent files a Petition for Dissolution of Marriage or a Petition for Custody with the court, requesting child custody and visitation arrangements.
  2. Service: The other parent is served with the petition and must respond within a specified time period.
  3. Discovery: Both parents engage in the discovery process, exchanging financial information, medical records, and other relevant documents.
  4. Custody Evaluations: A custody evaluation may be ordered by the court to assess the child's best interests and provide a recommendation for custody and visitation arrangements.
  5. Mediation: The parents may participate in mediation to resolve disputes and reach an agreement on custody and visitation.
  6. Court Hearing: The court holds a hearing to determine child custody and visitation arrangements, considering the factors outlined in ORS 107.105 to 107.705.
  7. Permanent Order: The court issues a permanent order outlining child custody and visitation arrangements.

Types of Custody in Oregon

There are several types of custody arrangements in Oregon, including:

Joint Custody

Joint custody means that both parents share physical and legal custody of the child. Joint custody can be:

  • Joint Physical Custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, with the child living with both parents on a regular basis.
  • Joint Legal Custody: Both parents share decision-making authority regarding the child's needs, education, and health.
  • Sole Physical and Joint Legal Custody: One parent has physical custody of the child, but both parents share decision-making authority.
  • Sole Physical and Sole Legal Custody: One parent has both physical and legal custody of the child.

Sole Custody

Sole custody means that one parent has both physical and legal custody of the child.

Shared Parenting

Shared parenting is a type of custody arrangement in which parents share physical and legal custody of the child, but also maintain a relationship with the child.

Supervised Visitation

Supervised visitation means that one parent has limited contact with the child, with a third party present to supervise the visit.

Restrictions on Custody

In some cases, the court may impose restrictions on custody due to concerns about the child's safety or well-being. Restrictions may include:

  • No Overnight Visitation: One parent is not allowed to have overnight visitation with the child.
  • Supervised Visitation: One parent must have a third party present during visitation.
  • Limited Visitation: One parent is only allowed to have visitation on certain days or at certain times.

Oregon's Child Support Guidelines

Child support in Oregon is governed by the Oregon Child Support Guidelines (ORS 25.001 to 25.080). The guidelines calculate child support based on:

  • Income: The income of both parents, including wages, self-employment income, and other sources of income.
  • Number of Children: The number of children in the household.
  • Other Expenses: Other expenses, such as health insurance and child care costs.

Oregon's Attorney Fee Awards

Attorney fees in Oregon are governed by ORS 20.075. The court may award attorney fees to one party as a sanction or as a result of a settlement agreement. The court will consider:

  • Reasonable Attorney Fees: The reasonableness of the attorney fees and expenses incurred by the party seeking an award.
  • Merit of the Claim: The merit of the claim or defense.
  • Good Faith: The good faith of the parties and their attorneys.

Oregon's Mediation and Arbitration

Mediation and arbitration are alternative dispute resolution methods that can be used to resolve child custody and support disputes in Oregon. Mediation involves a neutral third party helping the parties negotiate an agreement, while arbitration involves a neutral third party making a binding decision.

Oregon's Family Law Forms

Family law forms are available for use in Oregon family law cases. These forms include:

  • Separation Agreement: A written agreement between the parties regarding property division and other issues.
  • Custody Agreement: A written agreement between the parties regarding child custody and visitation.
  • Child Support Agreement:

    Child support agreement is a written agreement between the parties regarding child support payments.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    Q: What is the Oregon Child Support Guidelines?

    A: The Oregon Child Support Guidelines are a set of rules used to calculate child support payments based on income, number of children, and other expenses.

    Q: Can I modify my child custody agreement in Oregon?

    A: Yes, you may be able to modify your child custody agreement in Oregon by filing a petition with the court and demonstrating a change in circumstances that warrants a modification.

    Q: How do I enforce a child support order in Oregon?

    A: You can enforce a child support order in Oregon by filing a petition with the court and requesting that the other parent be held in contempt for failing to pay child support.

    Q: Can I have joint custody of my child in Oregon if I have a history of domestic violence?

    A: No, if you have a history of domestic violence, the court may not grant you joint custody of your child in Oregon. The court will consider the safety and well-being of the child when making a custody decision.

    Q: What is a custody evaluation in Oregon?

    A: A custody evaluation is a process in which a neutral third party assesses the child's best interests and provides a recommendation for custody and visitation arrangements.
