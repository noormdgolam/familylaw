Hawaii Family Law and Divorce: A Comprehensive Guide

Hawaii is a unique state with its own set of laws and regulations governing family law, divorce, and child custody. As a highly experienced family law attorney and legal consultant, I will provide a comprehensive guide to help you navigate the complexities of these issues in the Aloha State.

Overview of Hawaii Family Law

Familial relationships in Hawaii are subject to the state's family code, which is codified in Title 31 of the Hawaii Revised Statutes (HRS). The Hawaii family code covers a wide range of topics, including marriage, divorce, child custody, child support, and property division.

Marriage and Divorce in Hawaii

In Hawaii, marriage is considered a civil contract between two consenting adults. To get married in Hawaii, couples must obtain a marriage license from the Department of Health and meet the state's residency requirements. If a couple decides to end their marriage, they must file for divorce in the state's family court.

The grounds for divorce in Hawaii are as follows:

  • No-fault divorce: This type of divorce is available to couples who have been separated for at least two years and meet certain other requirements.
  • Irreconcilable differences: This is the most common ground for divorce in Hawaii.
  • Abandonment: If one spouse has abandoned the other for a period of one year or more, this can be grounds for divorce.
  • Adultery: If one spouse has committed adultery, this can be grounds for divorce.
  • Physical or mental abuse: If one spouse has been physically or mentally abused by the other, this can be grounds for divorce.

In Hawaii, divorce proceedings typically involve the following steps:

  1. Filing a petition for divorce: One spouse files a petition with the family court, stating the grounds for divorce and requesting a divorce.
  2. Serving the other spouse: The petition must be served on the other spouse, who then has a certain amount of time to respond.
  3. Discovery: Both spouses must disclose their financial information and other relevant details to the court.
  4. Mediation: The court may require the couple to attend mediation to try to resolve their differences.
  5. Trial: If mediation is unsuccessful, the case goes to trial, where a judge will make a decision on the divorce.

Child Custody in Hawaii

Child custody is one of the most important issues in any divorce proceeding. In Hawaii, the court's primary concern is the best interests of the child. The court will consider a range of factors, including:

  • The child's age and needs.
  • The relationship between the child and each parent.
  • The ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs.
  • The child's wishes, if the child is old enough to express a preference.

There are two types of child custody in Hawaii:

  • Joint custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child.

In Hawaii, the court may also order a custody arrangement that is less than joint or sole custody, such as:

  • Split custody: The child spends part of their time with each parent.
  • Shared custody: Both parents have joint physical custody, but one parent has primary responsibility for the child's daily care.

Child Support in Hawaii

Child support is an important issue in any divorce proceeding. In Hawaii, the court will determine child support based on a variety of factors, including:

  • The income of both parents.
  • The number of children involved.
  • The children's needs, including their education and healthcare expenses.
  • The standard of living of both parents.

The court may order one parent to pay child support to the other parent, and may also order the parent paying child support to provide health insurance for the child.

Property Division in Hawaii

Property division is another important issue in any divorce proceeding. In Hawaii, the court will divide property based on the principle of equitable distribution. This means that the court will divide property fairly, but not necessarily equally.

The court will consider a range of factors, including:

  • The length of the marriage.
  • The income and earning potential of both spouses.
  • The contributions of each spouse to the marriage, including childcare and household duties.
  • The value of the property.

The court may also consider the following types of property:

  • Real property, such as homes and land.
  • Personal property, such as furniture and jewelry.
  • Financial assets, such as bank accounts and investments.
  • Business interests.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Divorce in Hawaii

Here is a step-by-step guide to help you navigate the divorce process in Hawaii:

  1. Filing a petition for divorce: File a petition with the family court, stating the grounds for divorce and requesting a divorce.
  2. Serving the other spouse: Serve the petition on the other spouse, who then has a certain amount of time to respond.
  3. Discovery: Disclose financial information and other relevant details to the court.
  4. Mediation: Attend mediation to try to resolve differences with the other spouse.
  5. Trial: If mediation is unsuccessful, the case goes to trial, where a judge will make a decision on the divorce.

FAQs about Divorce in Hawaii

Here are some frequently asked questions about divorce in Hawaii:

Q: How long does a divorce take in Hawaii?

A: The length of time it takes to get a divorce in Hawaii can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case. In general, a divorce can take anywhere from a few months to a year or more.

Q: Do I need a lawyer to get a divorce in Hawaii?

A: No, you do not need a lawyer to get a divorce in Hawaii. However, it is highly recommended that you hire an experienced divorce attorney to ensure that your rights are protected and your interests are represented.

Q: What is the process for getting a divorce in Hawaii?

A: The process for getting a divorce in Hawaii involves filing a petition for divorce, serving the other spouse, attending mediation, and going to trial if necessary.

Q: How is child custody determined in Hawaii?

A: Child custody is determined based on the best interests of the child. The court will consider a range of factors, including the child's age and needs, the relationship between the child and each parent, and the ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs.
