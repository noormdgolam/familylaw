Understanding Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Florida

In Florida, child custody laws and guidelines are designed to prioritize the best interests of the child. This comprehensive guide will delve into the intricacies of Florida's child custody laws, helping you navigate the complexities of joint custody, custody determinations, and more.

The Primary Custody Factor in Florida

The primary custody factor in Florida is the best interests of the child. This means that the court's primary consideration when determining child custody is what arrangement will best promote the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being. In making this determination, the court will consider a variety of factors, including:

  • The child's wishes, taking into account their maturity level and ability to make informed decisions
  • The child's relationship with each parent
  • The parents' ability to communicate and cooperate with each other
  • The parents' physical and mental health
  • The child's education, extracurricular activities, and other important life aspects
  • The parents' work schedules and other commitments
  • The living situation and stability of each parent's home
  • Any history of domestic violence or other safety concerns
  • Other relevant factors that may impact the child's best interests

Joint Custody in Florida

Florida law presumes that joint custody is in the best interests of the child. Joint custody means that both parents will share decision-making authority and physical custody of the child. However, joint custody can be either:

  • Shared joint custody, where both parents have equal decision-making authority and physical custody
  • Sole joint custody, where one parent has primary decision-making authority, but both parents share physical custody

The Age of the Child in Custody Determinations

Unlike some other states, Florida does not have a specific age at which the child's wishes become a determining factor in custody determinations. Instead, the court will consider the child's wishes and maturity level in making its decision.

Custody Determinations and the Court Process

Custody determinations in Florida typically involve the following process:

  1. The parties file a petition for custody with the court
  2. The court schedules a hearing to consider the petition
  3. The parties present evidence and testimony to the court, including expert testimony and witness statements
  4. The court makes a custody determination based on the best interests of the child
  5. The court may order a custody evaluation or other expert testimony to assist in making its decision

Modifying a Custody Order in Florida

Florida law allows for modification of a custody order if there has been a substantial change in circumstances since the original order was entered. To modify a custody order, the moving party must demonstrate that:

  • There has been a significant change in circumstances since the original order
  • The change is not a result of the party's own actions
  • The modification is in the best interests of the child

FAQs About Child Custody in Florida

Q: What is the primary custody factor in Florida?

A: The primary custody factor in Florida is the best interests of the child.

Q: Can I request sole custody of my child in Florida?

A: Yes, but you must demonstrate that joint custody would not be in the best interests of the child.

Q: How long does a custody evaluation take in Florida?

A: The length of a custody evaluation varies depending on the complexity of the case and the availability of the evaluator.

Q: Can I relocate with my child in Florida without the other parent's permission?

A: No, you must obtain the other parent's permission or seek court approval before relocating with your child.

Q: How do I modify a custody order in Florida?

A: You must demonstrate a substantial change in circumstances since the original order was entered and show that the modification is in the best interests of the child.
