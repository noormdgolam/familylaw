Family Law in Florida: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

Florida's family law system provides a framework for resolving disputes related to divorce, child custody, support, and property division. As a highly experienced Family Law attorney and legal consultant, we understand the complexities involved in navigating these proceedings. In this article, we will provide an in-depth guide to Florida's family law, including state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance.

Overview of Florida's Family Law System

Florida's family law system is governed by the Florida Statutes, specifically Chapter 61, which deals with marriage and domestic relations. The Florida Supreme Court has established the following principles to guide family law proceedings:

  • No person shall be denied due process of law in any matter that affects the rights of a minor.
  • No person shall be denied equal protection of the law in any matter that affects the rights of a minor.
  • The best interests of the minor shall be the primary consideration in any matter that affects the rights of a minor.

Grounds for Divorce in Florida

In Florida, divorce can be obtained on the grounds of:

  • Irreconcilable differences
  • Adultery
  • Bigamy
  • Voluntary absence for a continuous period of four years
  • Imprisonment for a crime that results in a sentence of one year or more
  • Incurable insanity

Residency Requirements for Divorce in Florida

To file for divorce in Florida, either spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least six months prior to filing the petition. This requirement applies to both in-state and out-of-state residents.

Divorce Proceedings in Florida

Florida divorce proceedings typically involve the following steps:

Step 1: Filing the Petition

The petition for divorce is filed with the circuit court in the county where one of the spouses resides. The petition must be signed by the petitioner and verified under oath.

Step 2: Serving the Respondent

The respondent must be served with a copy of the petition and a summons. This can be done by a process server or by certified mail, return receipt requested.

Step 3: Filing the Answer

The respondent has 20 days to file an answer to the petition. If the respondent fails to respond, the petitioner may file a default.

Step 4: Discovery

Both parties engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and documents related to the divorce. This can include financial statements, tax returns, and other relevant documents.

Step 5: Mediation and Settlement

The parties may participate in mediation or settlement negotiations to resolve disputes and reach a mutually acceptable agreement.

Step 6: Trial

If the parties are unable to reach a settlement, the case will proceed to trial. At trial, both parties will present evidence and testimony to support their positions.

Child Custody in Florida

Florida law prioritizes the best interests of the child in determining custody arrangements. The court will consider the following factors:

  • The love, affection, and other emotional ties between the parents and the child
  • The child's preference, if the child is of sufficient age to express a preference
  • The child's home, school, and community relationships
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved
  • Any history of domestic violence or abuse
  • Any other factors that may be relevant

The court may award joint or sole custody. Joint custody involves both parents sharing decision-making authority and time with the child, while sole custody involves one parent having exclusive decision-making authority and time with the child.

Child Support in Florida

Florida law requires both parents to contribute financially to the support of their child. The court will consider the following factors when determining child support:

  • The income of both parents
  • The number of children
  • The cost of health insurance
  • The cost of child care
  • Any other factors that may be relevant

Property Division in Florida

Florida is an equitable distribution state, which means that the court will divide marital property fairly, but not necessarily equally. The court will consider the following factors:

  • The contribution of each spouse to the marriage
  • The economic circumstances of each spouse
  • The length of the marriage
  • The age and health of each spouse
  • Any other factors that may be relevant

Attorney Costs in Florida

The cost of hiring a family law attorney in Florida can vary widely, depending on the complexity of the case, the attorney's experience, and the location. On average, hourly rates for family law attorneys in Florida range from $200 to $500 per hour. The total cost of the case can range from $5,000 to $50,000 or more, depending on the complexity and duration of the proceedings.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Filing for Divorce in Florida

Here is a step-by-step guide to filing for divorce in Florida:

Step 1: Gather Documents

Collect all relevant documents, including:

  • Marriage certificate
  • Divorce petition
  • Summons
  • Financial statements
  • Tax returns
  • Other relevant documents

Step 2: File the Petition

File the divorce petition with the circuit court in the county where one of the spouses resides.

Step 3: Serve the Respondent

Serve the respondent with a copy of the petition and a summons.

Step 4: File the Answer

File the respondent's answer to the petition.

Step 5: Engage in Discovery

Exchange information and documents related to the divorce.

Step 6: Mediate and Settle

Participate in mediation or settlement negotiations to resolve disputes and reach a mutually acceptable agreement.

Step 7: Go to Trial

Attend trial and present evidence and testimony to support your position.

FAQs about Family Law in Florida

Here are some frequently asked questions about family law in Florida:

Q: Can I file for divorce in Florida if I am not a resident of the state?

A: Yes, but you must have been a resident of the state for at least six months prior to filing the petition.

Q: How long does a divorce take in Florida?

A: The length of a divorce in Florida can vary widely, depending on the complexity of the case and the parties' willingness to settle.

Q: Can I change my child's custody arrangement after a divorce?

A: Yes, but you must file a petition to modify the custody arrangement and demonstrate a change in circumstances that justify the modification.

Q: Do I need an attorney to file for divorce in Florida?

A: While it is possible to file for divorce without an attorney, it is highly recommended that you hire an experienced family law attorney to guide you through the process and protect your rights.

Q: How much does a divorce cost in Florida?

A: The cost of a divorce in Florida can vary widely, depending on the complexity of the case, the attorney's experience, and the location. On average, the total cost of the case can range from $5,000 to $50,000 or more.

Q: Can I get alimony in Florida?

A: Yes, but alimony is only awarded in cases where one spouse is significantly dependent on the other spouse for financial support and the dependent spouse has a lower net income.

Conclusion

Florida's family law system provides a framework for resolving disputes related to divorce, child custody, support, and property division. By understanding the state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, and expected attorney costs, you can navigate the process with confidence. If you are considering filing for divorce in Florida, it is essential to hire an experienced family law attorney to guide you through the process and protect your rights.
