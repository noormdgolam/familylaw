Missouri Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

Missouri is one of the states with the highest divorce rates in the country, with over 40% of marriages ending in divorce. As a state-specific guide, this article will delve into the intricacies of Missouri family law, covering divorce, child custody, and the associated legal processes.

Missouri Divorce Laws

Missouri is a no-fault divorce state, meaning that a divorce can be granted without the need to prove fault or wrongdoing by one spouse. The grounds for divorce in Missouri include:

  • No Fault: Mutual consent, or irreconcilable differences
  • Grounds: Adultery, abandonment, cruelty, and felony conviction

Divorce Proceedings in Missouri

The divorce process in Missouri typically begins with the filing of a petition for divorce. The petitioner (the spouse who files the petition) must provide the court with the following information:

Information Required Description
Name and address of the parties The names and addresses of both spouses must be provided in the petition.
Grounds for divorce The grounds for divorce, either no-fault or fault-based, must be stated in the petition.
Property division and custody The petitioner must provide an estimate of the marital property and indicate how it should be divided, as well as their proposed custody arrangement for any children.
Support and maintenance The petitioner must indicate whether they are seeking spousal support or maintenance.

Service of Process

Once the petition is filed, it must be served on the respondent (the spouse who did not file the petition). Service can be accomplished through personal service, certified mail, or publication in a newspaper.

Response to the Petition

The respondent has 30 days to file a response to the petition, which must include their answer to the grounds for divorce and any counterclaims they wish to make.

Child Custody in Missouri

Child custody in Missouri is determined on a case-by-case basis, with the court considering the best interests of the child. The court may award one parent sole custody or joint custody, depending on the circumstances.

Types of Custody Arrangements

Missouri recognizes several types of custody arrangements:

  • Sole Custody: One parent has primary physical and legal custody of the child
  • Joint Custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child
  • Split Custody: Each parent has primary physical custody of one child
  • Joint Legal Custody: Both parents share decision-making authority for the child
  • Joint Physical Custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child

Factors Considered in Child Custody Cases

The court considers the following factors in determining child custody:

  • The wishes of the parents
  • The wishes of the child, if old enough to express a preference
  • The child's relationship with each parent
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community
  • The mental and physical health of the parents and child
  • The parents' work schedules and ability to care for the child
  • The parents' ability to communicate and cooperate
  • The child's needs and best interests

Missouri Child Support Laws

Missouri follows the "income shares" model for calculating child support. This means that both parents' incomes are considered in determining the amount of child support owed.

Factors Considered in Child Support Cases

The court considers the following factors in determining child support:

  • The parents' combined income
  • The number of children to be supported
  • The amount of time each parent spends with the child
  • The parents' work-related expenses
  • The child's needs and expenses

Step-by-Step Guide to Missouri Divorce Proceedings

Here is a step-by-step guide to Missouri divorce proceedings:

Step 1: Determine Grounds for Divorce

Determine the grounds for divorce, either no-fault or fault-based.

Step 2: File the Petition

File the petition for divorce with the court, providing the required information.

Step 3: Serve the Respondent

Have the respondent served with the petition, either personally or by certified mail.

Step 4: Respond to the Petition

File a response to the petition, including any counterclaims.

Step 5: Mediate or Arbitrate

Participate in mediation or arbitration to resolve disputes and reach a settlement.

Step 6: Go to Trial

Attend trial, where a judge will make a final decision on the issues.

FAQs

Q: Can I get a divorce in Missouri without a lawyer?

A: Yes, but it is not recommended. A lawyer can provide guidance and representation throughout the process.

Q: How long does a Missouri divorce take?

A: The length of a Missouri divorce can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule.

Q: Do I have to go to court for a Missouri divorce?

A: Not necessarily. Many divorces are settled through mediation or arbitration, avoiding the need for a court appearance.

Q: Can I change my child custody arrangement in Missouri?

A: Yes, but it is not automatic. A court order must be modified, and the other parent must be notified.

Q: Do I have to pay child support in Missouri if I have joint custody?

A: Yes, even with joint custody, the court may still order one parent to pay child support based on their income and the child's needs.

Q: Can I get alimony in Missouri?

A: Possibly. The court may award alimony to one spouse if the other spouse has a higher income and the receiving spouse is unable to support themselves.

Q: Do I have to disclose my financial information in Missouri divorce proceedings?

A: Yes, as part of the discovery process, both spouses must disclose their financial information to the court and the other spouse.

Q: Can I appeal a Missouri divorce decision?

A: Yes, either spouse can appeal a divorce decision to a higher court, but the grounds for appeal are limited.

Q: Can I get a restraining order in Missouri during divorce proceedings?

A: Yes, if you feel threatened or harassed by your spouse, you can seek a restraining order to protect yourself.
