Maine Statute of Limitations

In Maine, the statute of limitations for various family law claims and actions can vary significantly. Here are some key examples:

Divorce: There is no statute of limitations for divorce in Maine. A divorce can be filed at any time, provided that the parties have been separated for at least 60 days or the grounds for divorce have occurred within the past 12 months.

Child Custody: A parent or guardian must file a petition for custody within one year of a child's 18th birthday, or the child will be considered an adult and have the right to determine their own custody arrangements.

Child Support: A parent or guardian must file a petition for support within three years of a child's birth, or the child will be considered an adult and have no entitlement to support.

Spousal Support: A spouse must file a petition for support within one year of the divorce decree, or the right to support will be deemed waived.

Adoption: The statute of limitations for adoption varies depending on the type of adoption. For example, a stepparent adoption must be completed within one year of the child's 18th birthday, while a private adoption must be completed within one year of the child's birth.

Other Matters: The statute of limitations for other family law matters, such as guardianship, conservatorship, and paternity actions, can also vary depending on the specific circumstances.

Common Legal Proceedings

Family law proceedings in Maine can be complex and emotionally charged, involving a range of legal issues and disputes. Here are some common legal proceedings that may arise in a family law matter:

  • Divorce proceedings: These involve the filing of a divorce complaint, the issuance of a summons and complaint, and the holding of a hearing to determine the terms of the divorce.
  • Child custody disputes: These involve the filing of a petition for custody, the issuance of a summons and complaint, and the holding of a hearing to determine the best interests of the child.
  • Child support disputes: These involve the filing of a petition for support, the issuance of a summons and complaint, and the holding of a hearing to determine the amount and duration of support.
  • Spousal support disputes: These involve the filing of a petition for support, the issuance of a summons and complaint, and the holding of a hearing to determine the amount and duration of support.
  • Adoption proceedings: These involve the filing of a petition for adoption, the issuance of a summons and complaint, and the holding of a hearing to determine the suitability of the adoptive parents.
