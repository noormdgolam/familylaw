Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Delaware: A Comprehensive Guide

In the state of Delaware, divorce is a complex and often emotional process. However, understanding the requirements and procedures involved can make a significant difference in navigating this challenging situation. This guide is designed to provide a comprehensive overview of the divorce filing process and requirements in Delaware, based on the state's laws and general legal principles.

Eligibility for Divorce in Delaware

To be eligible for divorce in Delaware, one or both spouses must have been residents of the state for at least one year prior to filing the divorce petition. This residency requirement is essential to establish jurisdiction and ensure that the court has the authority to hear the case.

Types of Divorce in Delaware

Delaware is a "no-fault" divorce state, meaning that the court does not consider fault grounds when granting a divorce. However, the state also allows for fault grounds, which may be used to establish grounds for divorce. The following are the types of divorce in Delaware:

  • No-fault divorce: This type of divorce is granted when the court finds that the marriage has become irretrievable broken, with no hope of reconciliation.
  • Fault divorce: This type of divorce is granted when one spouse is found to be at fault for the breakdown of the marriage, such as adultery, abuse, or abandonment.

Grounds for Divorce in Delaware

Delaware allows for both no-fault and fault grounds for divorce. The following are some of the grounds for fault divorce in Delaware:

  • Adultery
  • Abuse (physical, emotional, or verbal)
  • Abandonment
  • Imprisonment for a felony
  • Separation for a period of two years or more

Divorce Filing Process in Delaware

The divorce filing process in Delaware involves several steps, including:

Step 1: Filing the Divorce Petition

The divorce petition is the initial document filed with the court to initiate the divorce process. The petition must be signed by the plaintiff (the spouse filing for divorce) and must include the following information:

  • The names and addresses of both spouses
  • The date and place of marriage
  • The grounds for divorce
  • A request for a divorce

Step 2: Serving the Divorce Petition

Once the divorce petition is filed, it must be served on the defendant (the spouse being divorced). The defendant must be served personally, by certified mail, or by a process server.

Step 3: Filing a Response

After being served, the defendant must file a response with the court within 20 days. The response must include an admission or denial of the allegations in the divorce petition, as well as any counterclaims.

Step 4: Waiting Period

Delaware has a mandatory waiting period of 60 days before a divorce can be granted. During this time, both spouses must not remarry or have any further contact with each other.

Step 5: Final Hearing

After the waiting period has expired, the court will schedule a final hearing to determine the terms of the divorce. During this hearing, both spouses will have the opportunity to present evidence and testimony.

FAQs

Q: How long does it take to get a divorce in Delaware?

A: The length of time it takes to get a divorce in Delaware can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. However, the minimum waiting period is 60 days.

Q: Do I need to hire an attorney to get a divorce in Delaware?

A: While it is not required to hire an attorney, having one can be beneficial in navigating the divorce process and ensuring that your rights are protected.

Q: Can I get a divorce in Delaware if I am not a resident?

A: No, both spouses must have been residents of Delaware for at least one year prior to filing the divorce petition.

Q: What happens if my spouse does not respond to the divorce petition?

A: If your spouse does not respond to the divorce petition, the court may enter a default judgment, which can grant the divorce and determine the terms of the divorce.

Q: Can I get a divorce in Delaware if my spouse is incarcerated?

A: Yes, you can still file for divorce in Delaware if your spouse is incarcerated. However, the divorce process may be delayed due to the difficulty in serving the divorce petition.
