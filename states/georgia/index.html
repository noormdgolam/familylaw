Family Law in Georgia: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

In the state of Georgia, family law is governed by the Georgia Code Annotated, specifically Title 19, which covers domestic relations. As a highly experienced Family Law attorney and legal consultant, I will provide a detailed overview of the divorce and child custody process in Georgia, including state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance.

Grounds for Divorce in Georgia

Georgia is a no-fault divorce state, meaning that a divorce can be granted without citing grounds for the divorce. However, the state does permit couples to cite fault grounds if they wish. The following are the grounds for divorce in Georgia:

  • Irreconcilable differences
  • Adultery
  • Habitual intoxication or drug addiction
  • Imprisonment for a crime involving moral turpitude
  • Willful desertion for a period of one year
  • Willful neglect to provide for the wife's support
  • Conviction of a felony and imprisonment for a term of two years or more

Divorce Proceedings in Georgia

The divorce process in Georgia typically begins with a petition for divorce filed with the Superior Court of the county where the parties reside. The petition must include the grounds for divorce, the parties' names and addresses, and a request for a divorce. The petition must be served on the respondent, who has 30 days to respond to the petition.

Once the respondent has responded, the court will schedule a hearing to determine the issues of divorce, including property division, spousal support, and child custody. The court may also appoint a guardian ad litem to represent the interests of any minor children involved in the divorce.

Child Custody in Georgia

Child custody in Georgia is governed by the Georgia Code Annotated, specifically O.C.G.A. § 19-9-1 et seq. The court's primary concern is the best interest of the child, and the court will consider the following factors when determining custody:

  • The love, affection, and bonding between the child and each parent
  • The child's age and sex
  • The child's needs and educational needs
  • The child's physical and emotional well-being
  • The parents' ability to provide a stable and loving home environment
  • The parents' ability to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs

The court may award one of the following types of custody:

  • Sole custody: One parent has the exclusive right to make decisions for the child
  • Joint custody: Both parents share the right to make decisions for the child
  • Joint physical custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child
  • Shared physical custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child

Child Support in Georgia

Child support in Georgia is governed by the Georgia Code Annotated, specifically O.C.G.A. § 19-6-15. The court will calculate child support based on the following factors:

  • The income of both parents
  • The number of children involved
  • The children's ages
  • The parents' health insurance costs
  • The parents' childcare costs

The court may also consider the following expenses when calculating child support:

  • Clothing and personal expenses
  • Healthcare expenses
  • Education expenses
  • Extracurricular activities expenses

Property Division in Georgia

Property division in Georgia is governed by the Georgia Code Annotated, specifically O.C.G.A. § 19-5-1 et seq. The court will divide the marital property in a fair and equitable manner, taking into account the following factors:

  • The length of the marriage
  • The income and earning capacity of each spouse
  • The contributions of each spouse to the marriage
  • The tax consequences of the division
  • The ability of each spouse to earn a living

The court may consider the following types of property when dividing it:

  • Real estate
  • Personal property
  • Business interests
  • Retirement accounts
  • Stocks and bonds

Attorney Costs in Georgia

The cost of hiring a divorce attorney in Georgia can vary depending on the complexity of the case, the attorney's experience, and the location. On average, a divorce attorney in Georgia can cost between $200 and $500 per hour. The total cost of a divorce can range from $2,000 to $20,000 or more, depending on the issues involved and the level of conflict between the parties.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Georgia Divorce and Child Custody

The following is a step-by-step guide to the divorce and child custody process in Georgia:

Step 1: Determine the Grounds for Divorce

Determine whether you and your spouse have irreconcilable differences or if there are grounds for fault divorce. If you are unsure, consult with an attorney.

Step 2: File a Petition for Divorce

File a petition for divorce with the Superior Court of the county where you and your spouse reside. The petition must include the grounds for divorce, your spouse's name and address, and a request for a divorce.

Step 3: Serve Your Spouse

Serve your spouse with the petition and a summons. Your spouse has 30 days to respond to the petition.

Step 4: Determine Child Custody

Determine whether you and your spouse can agree on a custody arrangement or whether the court will need to determine custody. If the court needs to determine custody, the court will schedule a hearing to determine the best interests of the child.

Step 5: Determine Child Support

Determine whether you and your spouse can agree on child support or whether the court will need to determine child support. If the court needs to determine child support, the court will schedule a hearing to determine the amount of child support.

Step 6: Divide Marital Property

Determine whether you and your spouse can agree on a property division or whether the court will need to determine property division. If the court needs to determine property division, the court will schedule a hearing to determine the fair and equitable division of marital property.

Step 7: Finalize the Divorce

Once the issues of divorce, child custody, child support, and property division have been resolved, the court will schedule a final hearing to finalize the divorce.
