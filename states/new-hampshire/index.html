New Hampshire Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce, Child Custody, and More

Introduction to New Hampshire Family Law

New Hampshire family law governs the relationships between spouses, parents, and children within the state. As a highly experienced family law attorney and legal consultant, I will guide you through the complexities of divorce, child custody, and other essential topics in New Hampshire.

Grounds for Divorce in New Hampshire

New Hampshire recognizes both no-fault and fault grounds for divorce. No-fault grounds include: irreconcilable differences, irretrievable breakdown of the marriage, and living separate and apart for one year. Fault grounds include: adultery, imprisonment, desertion, cruel and abusive treatment, and gross misbehavior.

No-Fault Divorce in New Hampshire

No-fault divorce in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 51:3-201. To file for no-fault divorce, you must demonstrate that your marriage has broken down irretrievably or that you and your spouse have lived separate and apart for at least one year.

Fault Divorce in New Hampshire

Fault divorce in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 51:3-202. To file for fault divorce, you must demonstrate one of the following grounds: adultery, imprisonment, desertion, cruel and abusive treatment, or gross misbehavior.

Divorce Proceedings in New Hampshire

Divorce proceedings in New Hampshire typically begin with the filing of a divorce complaint with the court. The complaint must include the grounds for divorce, the parties' names and addresses, and any other relevant information.

Service of Process in New Hampshire

Service of process in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 21:2-9. You may serve your spouse with process by certified mail, private process server, or sheriff's service.

Divorce Hearing in New Hampshire

The divorce hearing in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 51:3-405. At the hearing, the court will consider the parties' testimony, evidence, and arguments to determine the grounds for divorce, property division, spousal support, and child custody.

Child Custody in New Hampshire

Child custody in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:2-2. The court's primary concern is the best interests of the child, including the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being.

Types of Child Custody in New Hampshire

New Hampshire recognizes the following types of child custody: joint custody, sole custody, physical custody, and legal custody.

Joint Custody in New Hampshire

Joint custody in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:2-1. Joint custody means that both parents have equal decision-making authority and physical custody of the child.

Sole Custody in New Hampshire

Sole custody in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:2-3. Sole custody means that one parent has sole decision-making authority and physical custody of the child.

Physical Custody in New Hampshire

Physical custody in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:2-4. Physical custody refers to the physical residence of the child.

Legal Custody in New Hampshire

Legal custody in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:2-5. Legal custody refers to the decision-making authority regarding the child's education, healthcare, and other important decisions.

Factors Considered in Child Custody Determinations in New Hampshire

The court considers the following factors in child custody determinations in New Hampshire: the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being, the parents' ability to provide a stable and loving environment, the child's relationship with each parent, and any history of domestic violence or abuse.

Spousal Support in New Hampshire

Spousal support in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:6-1. Spousal support refers to the court-ordered payment of one spouse to the other to support themselves after divorce.

Factors Considered in Spousal Support Determinations in New Hampshire

The court considers the following factors in spousal support determinations in New Hampshire: the parties' income, assets, and property, the length of the marriage, the parties' age and health, the parties' earning capacity, and any other relevant factors.

Property Division in New Hampshire

Property division in New Hampshire is governed by RSA 458:6-2. The court will divide the parties' property in a fair and equitable manner, considering the parties' contributions to the marriage and their financial circumstances.

Factors Considered in Property Division Determinations in New Hampshire

The court considers the following factors in property division determinations in New Hampshire: the parties' income, assets, and property, the length of the marriage, the parties' contributions to the marriage, and any other relevant factors.

FAQs about Divorce in New Hampshire

Q: What is the process for filing for divorce in New Hampshire?

A: The process for filing for divorce in New Hampshire begins with the filing of a divorce complaint with the court, followed by service of process on your spouse.

Q: What are the grounds for divorce in New Hampshire?

A: New Hampshire recognizes both no-fault and fault grounds for divorce, including irreconcilable differences, irretrievable breakdown of the marriage, and living separate and apart for one year, as well as adultery, imprisonment, desertion, cruel and abusive treatment, and gross misbehavior.
