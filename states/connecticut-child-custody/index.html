Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Connecticut: A Comprehensive Guide

In Connecticut, child custody laws are designed to prioritize the best interests of the child. This guide will walk you through the key factors and guidelines that govern child custody decisions in the state.

Primary Custody Factor: Best Interests of the Child

The best interests of the child are the primary consideration in all child custody decisions in Connecticut. This means that the court's primary goal is to make a decision that will provide a stable, loving, and nurturing environment for the child.

Factors Considered in Determining Best Interests

  • Age and sex of the child
  • Needs of the child
  • Wishes of the child, considering age and maturity
  • Capacity and interest of each parent to act as a custodial parent
  • Ability of each parent to provide a stable home environment
  • Length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment
  • Permanence as a family unit
  • Mothers and fathers have equal rights and responsibilities
  • History of substance abuse by a parent
  • History of family violence by a parent

Joint Custody Presumption in Connecticut

In Connecticut, there is a joint custody presumption, which means that the court will presume that joint custody is in the best interests of the child, unless it would be detrimental to the child. This means that the court will generally start with a presumption of joint custody and then consider the specific circumstances of the case to determine whether joint custody is feasible and in the best interests of the child.

Age of the Child and Custody Decisions

As the child gets older, they may have more input in custody decisions. In Connecticut, a child is considered to have the maturity to make informed decisions about their custody at the age of 12. However, this is not a hard and fast rule, and the court will consider the child's individual maturity level and circumstances when making a decision.

Types of Custody Arrangements in Connecticut

Residential Custody

Residential custody refers to the parent with whom the child lives. This is also known as "physical custody."

Legal Custody

Legal custody refers to the parent's right to make decisions about the child's life, including education, healthcare, and extracurricular activities.

Shared Custody

Shared custody refers to a situation where both parents have significant periods of time with the child and share decision-making responsibilities.

Steps to Obtain Custody in Connecticut

Filing a Petition for Custody

To obtain custody in Connecticut, you will need to file a petition for custody with the court. This petition must include information about the child, the parents, and the reasons why you believe you should have custody.

Custody Evaluation

Once the petition is filed, the court may order a custody evaluation. This evaluation is conducted by a neutral third party, such as a psychologist or social worker, who will assess the child's needs and make recommendations to the court.

Court Hearing

After the custody evaluation is complete, the court will schedule a hearing to determine custody. At the hearing, both parents will have the opportunity to present their case and argue why they believe they should have custody.

Common Custody Issues in Connecticut

Modification of Custody Orders

Custody orders can be modified if there has been a significant change in circumstances, such as a change in the child's needs or a change in one of the parents' circumstances.

Enforcement of Custody Orders

Custody orders can be enforced through the court system. If one parent is not complying with the custody order, the other parent can file a motion with the court to enforce the order.

Conclusion

Child custody laws in Connecticut are designed to prioritize the best interests of the child. By understanding the primary custody factor, joint custody presumption, and age of the child, you can better navigate the custody process in Connecticut.
