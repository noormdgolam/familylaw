Understanding Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in Alaska

As a parent, determining child custody can be a complex and emotionally challenging process. In Alaska, the primary factor in determining child custody is the "best interests of the child." This comprehensive guide will walk you through the key factors and guidelines that courts consider when making custody decisions in Alaska.

The Best Interests of the Child Standard

In Alaska, the "best interests of the child" standard is the primary consideration when determining child custody. This standard takes into account various factors, including:

  • The love, affection, and interaction between the child and each parent
  • The child's wishes, taking into account the child's age and maturity
  • The child's needs for a stable and consistent home environment
  • The ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs
  • The distance between the parents' residences and the effect this distance may have on the child's life
  • Any history of domestic violence or child abuse
  • The mental and physical health of each parent

Joint Custody in Alaska

While Alaska has a joint custody presumption, it does not automatically apply in all cases. Joint custody is typically awarded when both parents can cooperate and work together to make joint decisions regarding the child's care and well-being.

The Age of Choice

In Alaska, the child's age is a significant factor in determining whether they can choose their preferred living arrangement. A child of the age of 14 can express their preference for living with one parent over the other. However, the court may still consider other factors and may not necessarily award custody based solely on the child's preference.

Types of Custody Arrangements in Alaska

Physical Custody

Physical custody refers to the parent with whom the child resides. In Alaska, courts may award sole physical custody to one parent or joint physical custody, where the child spends significant time with both parents.

Legal Custody

Legal custody refers to the right to make decisions regarding the child's care and well-being. In Alaska, courts may award sole legal custody to one parent or joint legal custody, where both parents share decision-making responsibilities.

Custody Evaluation Process in Alaska

When determining child custody, Alaska courts often consider the following steps:

  1. Initial pleadings and discovery: The parties exchange information and pleadings regarding the custody dispute.
  2. Custody evaluation: A court-appointed evaluator assesses the parties and the child to determine the best interests of the child.
  3. Recommendations: The evaluator provides a written report with recommendations for custody and visitation arrangements.
  4. Court hearing: The parties and their attorneys present evidence and arguments to the court.
  5. Custody order: The court issues a written order determining custody and visitation arrangements.
