Wyoming Divorce Laws and Statutes

The Wyoming divorce laws are governed by the Wyoming Statutes, specifically Chapter 20 of the Wyoming Statutes, which deals with divorce, annulment, and separation. Some key state-specific statutes include:

  • Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-2-101: This statute defines the grounds for divorce, including irreconcilable differences, adultery, cruelty, and abandonment.
  • Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-2-102: This statute outlines the requirements for filing a divorce petition, including the necessary documents and fees.
  • Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-2-103: This statute deals with the service of process and the response to a divorce petition.
  • Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-2-104: This statute outlines the grounds for granting a divorce, including the court's discretion to grant a divorce based on the circumstances of the case.

Grounds for Divorce in Wyoming

In Wyoming, there are several grounds for divorce, including:

  • Irreconcilable differences: This is the most common ground for divorce in Wyoming and requires a showing that the marriage is beyond repair.
  • Adultery: This ground requires a showing of infidelity and is subject to certain requirements and restrictions.
  • Cruelty: This ground requires a showing of physical or emotional abuse and is subject to certain requirements and restrictions.
  • Abandonment: This ground requires a showing of intentional abandonment by one spouse and is subject to certain requirements and restrictions.

Wyoming Divorce Proceedings

The Wyoming divorce process typically involves several steps, including:

  • Filing a divorce petition: This is the first step in the divorce process and involves filing a petition with the court, which outlines the grounds for divorce and the relief sought.
  • Service of process: This involves serving the divorce petition on the other spouse, either personally or by certified mail.
  • Response to the divorce petition: The other spouse has a limited time to respond to the divorce petition, either by agreeing to the divorce or contesting the grounds.
  • Discovery: This involves the exchange of information and evidence between the parties, including financial documents, witness statements, and expert testimony.
  • Mediation: Many divorce cases in Wyoming involve mediation, which is a process where the parties work with a neutral third-party mediator to reach a settlement agreement.
  • Trial: If the parties are unable to reach a settlement agreement, the case may proceed to trial, where a judge will make a final decision on the divorce.

Wyoming Child Custody Laws

In Wyoming, child custody laws are governed by Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-5-302, which states that the court shall award custody in accordance with the best interests of the child. Some key factors in determining child custody in Wyoming include:

  • The love, affection, and bonding between the child and each parent.
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs.
  • The length of time the child has lived in a stable, suitable environment and the desire to maintain continuity.
  • The child's needs for stability, security, and permanence.
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to cooperate with the other in matters related to the child's care and well-being.

Types of Child Custody in Wyoming

In Wyoming, there are several types of child custody arrangements, including:

  • Sole custody: This involves one parent having sole physical and legal custody of the child.
  • Joint custody: This involves both parents sharing physical and legal custody of the child, either equally or unequally.
  • Shared custody: This involves both parents sharing physical custody of the child, but not necessarily legal custody.
  • Physical custody: This involves one or both parents having physical custody of the child, including decision-making authority.
  • Legal custody: This involves one or both parents having legal custody of the child, including decision-making authority.

Wyoming Child Support Laws

In Wyoming, child support laws are governed by Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-6-201, which states that the court shall order child support in an amount that is reasonable and necessary for the support of the child. Some key factors in determining child support in Wyoming include:

  • The income of both parents.
  • The number of children involved.
  • The physical and emotional needs of the children.
  • The standard of living and financial situation of both parents.
  • The ability of both parents to provide for the children's needs.

Wyoming Spousal Support Laws

In Wyoming, spousal support laws are governed by Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 20-2-112, which states that the court may order spousal support in an amount that is reasonable and necessary for the support of the spouse. Some key factors in determining spousal support in Wyoming include:

  • The length of the marriage.
  • The income and earning capacity of both spouses.
  • The standard of living and financial situation of both spouses.
  • The physical and emotional needs of both spouses.
  • The ability of both spouses to provide for their own needs.
