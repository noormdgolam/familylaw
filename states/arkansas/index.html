Family Law in Arkansas: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

Overview of Family Law in Arkansas

Family law in Arkansas governs the rights and responsibilities of family members, including married couples, parents, and children. The state's family law is designed to protect the welfare and best interests of children, while also providing a fair and efficient process for resolving family disputes. In this article, we will provide an overview of the key aspects of family law in Arkansas, including divorce, child custody, and other related topics.

Divorce in Arkansas

Grounds for Divorce

In Arkansas, a divorce can be granted based on the following grounds:

  • No fault divorce: This type of divorce is based on the irreconcilable differences between the spouses.
  • Separation: The spouses must have lived separate and apart for a period of 18 months or more.
  • Adultery: One spouse must have committed adultery.
  • Cruel and inhuman treatment: One spouse must have subjected the other to cruel and inhuman treatment.
  • Abandonment: One spouse must have abandoned the other for a period of 18 months or more.
  • Imprisonment: One spouse must have been imprisoned for a felony for a period of two years or more.

Divorce Procedures in Arkansas

The divorce process in Arkansas typically involves the following steps:

  1. Filing a petition: One spouse files a petition for divorce with the court, stating the grounds for the divorce.
  2. Service of process: The petition is served on the other spouse, who has 20 days to respond.
  3. Temporary orders: The court may issue temporary orders regarding custody, visitation, and support during the pendency of the divorce.
  4. Discovery: Both spouses exchange financial information and other relevant documents.
  5. Mediation or settlement: The spouses may attempt to reach a settlement through mediation or negotiation.
  6. Trial: If the spouses cannot reach a settlement, the case will proceed to trial.
  7. Dissolution of marriage: The court issues a final decree of divorce, dissolving the marriage.

Child Custody in Arkansas

Types of Custody

In Arkansas, there are two types of custody: legal custody and physical custody.

  • Legal custody: Refers to the decision-making authority regarding a child's upbringing, including education, healthcare, and religion.
  • Physical custody: Refers to the physical care and supervision of a child.

Custody Determination

The court's primary consideration in determining custody is the best interests of the child. In Arkansas, the court considers the following factors:

  • The wishes of the child.
  • The wishes of the parents.
  • The child's physical, emotional, and moral well-being.
  • The child's relationship with each parent.
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • The mental and physical health of the parents.
  • The stability of each parent's home environment.
  • The parents' ability to cooperate and communicate.
  • The parents' work schedules and ability to provide a stable environment.

Custody Arrangements

Custody arrangements in Arkansas can take various forms, including:

  • Joint custody: Both parents share legal and physical custody.
  • Joint physical custody with sole legal custody: Both parents share physical custody, but one parent has sole legal custody.
  • Split custody: Each parent has sole custody of one or more children.

Child Support in Arkansas

Child Support Obligations

In Arkansas, both parents are responsible for supporting their children, regardless of their marital status. Child support is typically paid by the non-custodial parent to the custodial parent. The amount of child support is determined by a formula that takes into account the income of both parents, the number of children, and other factors.

Enforcement of Child Support

Enforcement of Child Support in Arkansas

In Arkansas, the Department of Finance and Administration (DFA) is responsible for enforcing child support orders. The DFA uses various methods to enforce child support, including:

  • Wage garnishment: The DFA can garnish the wages of the non-custodial parent to collect child support.
  • License suspension: The DFA can suspend the driver's license of the non-custodial parent if they fail to pay child support.
  • Property seizure: The DFA can seize the property of the non-custodial parent to collect child support.
  • Contempt of court: The court can hold the non-custodial parent in contempt of court if they fail to pay child support.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Divorce and Child Custody in Arkansas

Step 1: Gather Information

Before filing for divorce or child custody, gather the following information:

  • Marital history, including date of marriage, children, and separation date.
  • Financial information, including income, expenses, and assets.
  • Child custody and visitation arrangements.
  • Any relevant documentation, such as tax returns and employment records.

Step 2: Choose an Attorney

Choose an attorney who is experienced in family law and has a good reputation in your community. Consider factors such as:

  • Experience with cases similar to yours.
  • Communication style and availability.
  • Cost and payment structure.

Step 3: File the Petition

File the petition for divorce or child custody with the court, providing the necessary documentation and information.

Step 4: Serve the Respondent

Serve the respondent with the petition and any accompanying documents.

Step 5: Attend Mediation or Negotiation

Attend mediation or negotiation with the respondent to attempt to reach a settlement.

Step 6: Attend Trial

Attend trial if a settlement cannot be reached. The court will make a final decision on the divorce or child custody case.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q: What is the waiting period for divorce in Arkansas?

A: In Arkansas, there is a 30-day waiting period after filing the petition for divorce before the court can grant a final decree of divorce.

Q: Can I get a divorce without going to court?

A: Yes, you can get a divorce without going to court through a process called "uncontested divorce." This typically involves agreeing on all issues and filing a single document with the court.

Q: How do I change a child custody order in Arkansas?

A: To change a child custody order in Arkansas, you must file a petition to modify the existing order and follow the procedures outlined in Arkansas Code Annotated § 9-13-301 et seq.
