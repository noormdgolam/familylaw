Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in New Hampshire: A Comprehensive Guide

New Hampshire's child custody laws aim to prioritize the best interests of the child, ensuring a stable and secure environment for their development. As an essential guide, we'll delve into the state's custody laws, joint custody presumption, and age at which a child can choose their custodial parent.

Understanding New Hampshire's Child Custody Laws

Under New Hampshire Revised Statutes Annotated (RSA) 458:17-a, the court must determine the child's best interests when making a custody decision. This means that the court will consider various factors, including:

  • The child's physical, emotional, and educational needs
  • The child's relationship with each parent and any other individuals who may significantly impact the child's life
  • The child's wishes and preferences, considering their age, maturity, and ability to communicate
  • The parents' ability to cooperate and communicate effectively
  • The parents' employment schedules and ability to provide a stable home environment
  • The child's adjustment to their home, school, and community

Joint Custody Presumption in New Hampshire

New Hampshire law presumes that joint custody is in the best interests of the child, as stated in RSA 458:17-a. Joint custody means that both parents share physical and legal custody, making important decisions together about the child's upbringing. This presumption can be rebutted if one parent can demonstrate a compelling reason why joint custody would not be in the child's best interests.

Age at Which a Child Can Choose Their Custodial Parent

Unlike some states, New Hampshire does not have a set age at which a child can choose their custodial parent. The court will consider the child's wishes and preferences, taking into account their age, maturity, and ability to communicate. Ultimately, the court's decision remains based on the child's best interests.

Steps to Take During a Child Custody Dispute in New Hampshire

If you're involved in a child custody dispute in New Hampshire, follow these steps to navigate the process:

  1. Contact the New Hampshire Department of Justice's child support and custody unit for guidance and support
  2. File a complaint with the court, providing detailed information about the dispute and your child's needs
  3. Attend court hearings and mediation sessions to resolve the dispute amicably
  4. Present evidence and witnesses to support your custody claim
  5. Follow the court's orders and adhere to any agreements reached during mediation

Robust FAQ Section

Q1: What is the primary factor considered in New Hampshire child custody decisions?

A: The best interests of the child.

Q2: Is joint custody presumed in New Hampshire?

A: Yes, RSA 458:17-a presumes that joint custody is in the best interests of the child.

Q3: Can a child in New Hampshire choose their custodial parent at a certain age?

A: No, there is no set age at which a child can choose their custodial parent in New Hampshire. The court will consider the child's wishes and preferences, taking into account their age, maturity, and ability to communicate.

Q4: How can I navigate a child custody dispute in New Hampshire?

A: Contact the New Hampshire Department of Justice's child support and custody unit for guidance and support, file a complaint with the court, attend court hearings and mediation sessions, present evidence and witnesses to support your custody claim, and follow the court's orders.

Q5: What evidence do I need to present in a New Hampshire child custody case?

A: You may need to present evidence and witness testimony to support your custody claims, such as proof of employment schedules, home environments, and the child's needs and preferences.

Q6: How long does a New Hampshire child custody case typically take?

A: The length of a child custody case in New Hampshire can vary, but it typically takes several months to a year or more to resolve, depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule.
