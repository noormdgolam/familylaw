Family Law in West Virginia: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

West Virginia is a state with a unique set of laws and regulations governing family law, divorce, and child custody. As a highly experienced family law attorney and legal consultant, I will provide you with a comprehensive guide to the complex process of navigating family law in West Virginia.

Introduction to Family Law in West Virginia

Family law in West Virginia is governed by the West Virginia Code, specifically Title 48, which outlines the laws and procedures for divorce, child custody, child support, and other family-related matters.

Grounds for Divorce in West Virginia

In West Virginia, the grounds for divorce are outlined in West Virginia Code §48-2-201. There are two types of divorces: limited divorce and absolute divorce.

For a limited divorce, the grounds are:

  • No-fault: The parties have lived separate and apart for one year and there are no minor children from the marriage.
  • Separation: The parties have lived separate and apart for one year.
  • Abuse: One party has committed domestic violence or abuse against the other party.
  • Abandonment: One party has abandoned the other party for a period of one year.

For an absolute divorce, the grounds are:

  • No-fault: The parties have lived separate and apart for one year and there are no minor children from the marriage.
  • Irreconcilable differences: The parties have lived separate and apart for one year and there are no minor children from the marriage.

Child Custody in West Virginia

In West Virginia, child custody is determined by the best interests of the child. The court considers the following factors when determining custody:

  • The wishes of the child's parents or legal custodians as to their custody.
  • The wishes of the child as to their custody.
  • The relationship between the child and each parent or other person, including any person who may be considered for custody of the child.
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved.
  • The willingness and ability of each parent to facilitate a close and continuing relationship between the child and the other parent.
  • The history of domestic violence or child abuse.
  • The existence of a history or pattern of substance abuse or child neglect or abandonment.

The court may award joint custody or sole custody. Joint custody means that both parents share custody, while sole custody means that one parent has sole authority to make decisions for the child.

Child Support in West Virginia

In West Virginia, child support is calculated using the West Virginia Child Support Guidelines. The guidelines consider the following factors:

  • The income of both parents.
  • The number of children.
  • The age of the children.
  • The cost of healthcare for the children.
  • The cost of education for the children.

The court may order the non-custodial parent to pay child support in the form of a lump sum or installments.

Common Legal Proceedings in West Virginia Family Law

There are several common legal proceedings in West Virginia family law, including:

  1. Divorce: A court proceeding to dissolve a marriage.
  2. Child Custody: A court proceeding to determine the custody of a child.
  3. Child Support: A court proceeding to determine the amount of child support owed by the non-custodial parent.
  4. Spousal Support: A court proceeding to determine the amount of spousal support owed by one spouse to the other.
  5. Property Division: A court proceeding to divide marital property.

The legal proceedings in West Virginia family law typically involve the following steps:

  1. File a Complaint: The first step in a family law proceeding is to file a complaint with the court. This is usually done by the plaintiff (the party starting the case).
  2. Serve the Defendant: The plaintiff must serve the defendant (the other party) with a copy of the complaint and any supporting documents.
  3. Answer and Counterclaims: The defendant must respond to the complaint by filing an answer and any counterclaims.
  4. Discovery: The parties must exchange information and documents relevant to the case through the process of discovery.
  5. Motions and Hearings: The parties may file motions and attend hearings to resolve any issues or disputes in the case.
  6. Trial: If the parties cannot resolve their issues through negotiation or mediation, the case may go to trial.
  7. Judgment: After the trial, the court will enter a judgment based on the evidence presented.
