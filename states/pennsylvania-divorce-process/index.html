Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Pennsylvania: A Comprehensive Guide

Divorce can be a complex and emotionally challenging process, but understanding the requirements and procedures can make it easier to navigate. In Pennsylvania, the divorce process involves several steps, including meeting the state's residency requirement, waiting period, and grounds for divorce. This guide will walk you through the divorce filing process and requirements in Pennsylvania, providing you with the necessary information to proceed with confidence.

Residency Requirement

In Pennsylvania, you must be a resident of the state for at least one year before you can file for divorce. This means that you must have been living in Pennsylvania for 12 months prior to filing your divorce petition. This requirement is essential to establish the court's jurisdiction over your divorce case.

Waiting Period

Once you have met the residency requirement, you must wait for 60 days before your divorce can be finalized. This waiting period is designed to give you and your spouse time to reconsider your decision and try to reconcile. However, if you and your spouse have reached a mutually agreeable settlement, you can waive the waiting period.

Fault Grounds Allowed

Pennsylvania allows fault grounds for divorce, which means that you can file for divorce based on your spouse's misconduct or fault. Some common fault grounds for divorce in Pennsylvania include adultery, desertion, and cruel and harsh treatment. However, if you and your spouse have been separated for at least 2 years, you can file for divorce on the grounds of indignities.

Divorce Filing Process

To file for divorce in Pennsylvania, you must follow these steps:

Step 1: Determine the Type of Divorce

You can file for one of two types of divorce in Pennsylvania: uncontested divorce or contested divorce. An uncontested divorce is a divorce where both spouses agree on all aspects of the divorce, including property division, child custody, and spousal support. A contested divorce, on the other hand, is a divorce where one or both spouses disagree on one or more aspects of the divorce.

Step 2: Gather Required Documents

You will need to gather several documents to file for divorce in Pennsylvania, including your marriage certificate, identity documents, and financial records. You will also need to complete a divorce petition form, which outlines the reasons for your divorce and the relief you seek.

Step 3: File the Divorce Petition

Once you have gathered all the necessary documents, you must file the divorce petition with the court. You can file in person or by mail, but you must ensure that you follow the court's filing instructions carefully.

Step 4: Serve Your Spouse

After filing the divorce petition, you must serve your spouse with a copy of the petition and a summons. This notifies your spouse of the divorce proceedings and gives them an opportunity to respond.

Step 5: Attend a Court Hearing

After your spouse has been served, you must attend a court hearing to finalize your divorce. At the hearing, you will be required to provide evidence to support your divorce petition and respond to any objections your spouse may have raised.

Divorce Forms and Petitions

When filing for divorce in Pennsylvania, you will need to complete several forms and petitions. Some of the most common forms include:

  • Divorce Petition (PA Form 1925)
  • Complaint for Divorce (PA Form 1926)
  • Answer to Divorce Petition (PA Form 1927)
  • Divorce Decree (PA Form 1928)

Divorce Costs and Fees

The cost of filing for divorce in Pennsylvania can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's filing fees. However, you can expect to pay between $150 and $300 to file for divorce. Additionally, you may need to pay for lawyer fees, court reporter fees, and other expenses associated with the divorce process.

Divorce Statistics in Pennsylvania

According to the American Community Survey (ACS) 2019 estimates, there were 34,116 divorces in Pennsylvania. The divorce rate in Pennsylvania is 2.9 per 1,000 married couples. The most common reasons for divorce in Pennsylvania include lack of communication, infidelity, and financial problems.
