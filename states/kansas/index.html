Kansas Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

In the state of Kansas, family law governs the rights and responsibilities of spouses and parents, particularly in relation to divorce, child custody, and child support. This article provides an in-depth examination of Kansas family law, including state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance on navigating the divorce and child custody process.

State-Specific Statutes

Kansas family law is governed by the Kansas Statutes Annotated (K.S.A.). The following statutes are relevant to divorce and child custody:

  • K.S.A. 23-2701 - 23-2714: Divorce and Annulment
  • K.S.A. 23-2801 - 23-2814: Child Custody and Support
  • K.S.A. 23-2901 - 23-2914: Property Division and Spousal Support

Common Legal Proceedings

The following are common legal proceedings in Kansas family law cases:

  • Divorce**: A divorce is a legal dissolution of a marriage. In Kansas, there are two types of divorce: no-fault divorce and fault divorce. No-fault divorce is based on the ground of incompatibility, while fault divorce is based on the ground of marital misconduct.
  • Child Custody**: Child custody refers to the physical and legal care and control of a minor child. In Kansas, child custody is determined by a court based on the best interests of the child.
  • Child Support**: Child support is the financial contribution made by a parent to support their child. In Kansas, child support is determined by a court based on the income of both parents and the needs of the child.
  • Property Division**: Property division refers to the division of property, including real estate, personal property, and financial assets, between spouses in a divorce. In Kansas, property division is based on the principle of equitable distribution.
  • Spousal Support**: Spousal support, also known as alimony, is the financial support paid by one spouse to the other after a divorce. In Kansas, spousal support is determined by a court based on the income, property, and earning capacity of both spouses.

Step-by-Step Guidance

The following is a step-by-step guide to navigating the divorce and child custody process in Kansas:

Step 1: Determine if You Qualify for a Divorce

To qualify for a divorce in Kansas, you must meet one of the following requirements:

  • You have been married for at least one year;
  • You have been a resident of Kansas for at least 60 days;
  • You and your spouse have entered into a written agreement waiving the residency requirement.

Step 2: Choose a Legal Grounds for Divorce

In Kansas, there are two types of divorce: no-fault divorce and fault divorce.

  • No-Fault Divorce: No-fault divorce is based on the ground of incompatibility. This means that there is no fault or wrongdoing by either spouse.
  • Fault Divorce: Fault divorce is based on the ground of marital misconduct, such as adultery, cruelty, or desertion.

Step 3: File for Divorce

To file for divorce in Kansas, you must file a Petition for Divorce with the district court in the county where you reside.

Step 4: Serve Your Spouse

After filing for divorce, you must serve your spouse with a Summons and Petition. This is usually done by a sheriff or private process server.

Step 5: Attend a Mandatory Disclosure Hearing

After serving your spouse, you must attend a mandatory disclosure hearing with a judge. This is a brief hearing where you and your spouse must disclose financial information and other relevant documents.

Step 6: Negotiate a Settlement

After the mandatory disclosure hearing, you and your spouse can negotiate a settlement. This can be done with the help of an attorney or mediator.

Step 7: Attend a Final Hearing

After negotiating a settlement, you must attend a final hearing with a judge. This is where the judge will review the settlement and make a final decision on the divorce and other issues.

Step 8: Finalize the Divorce

After the final hearing, the judge will sign a Dissolution of Marriage order, finalizing the divorce.

Step 9: Establish Child Custody and Support

After the divorce is finalized, you and your spouse must establish child custody and support arrangements. This can be done through a Child Custody and Support Order or a separate agreement.

Step 10: Review and Update the Agreement

Finally, you and your spouse must review and update the agreement to ensure it is fair and reasonable for both parties.

FAQs

Here are some frequently asked questions about family law in Kansas:

  • Q: What is the average cost of a divorce in Kansas?
    • A: The average cost of a divorce in Kansas is between $5,000 and $10,000.
  • Q: How long does a divorce take in Kansas?
    • A: The length of a divorce in Kansas can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the efficiency of the court. On average, a divorce can take between 3-6 months to complete.
  • Q: Can I file for divorce on my own in Kansas?
    • A: Yes, you can file for divorce on your own in Kansas. However, it is highly recommended that you hire an attorney to help navigate the process and ensure your rights are protected.
  • Q: How is child custody determined in Kansas?
    • A: Child custody is determined by a court based on the best interests of the child. The court will consider factors such as the child's age, health, and educational needs, as well as the parents' ability to provide a stable and loving environment.
  • Q: Can I change my child support payments in Kansas?
    • A: Yes, you can change your child support payments in Kansas. However, you must file a Modification of Child Support Order with the court and demonstrate a change in circumstances that justifies the modification.
