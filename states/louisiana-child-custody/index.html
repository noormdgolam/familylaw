Family Law and Divorce Proceedings in Louisiana: A Comprehensive Guide to Child Custody

Overview of Family Law in Louisiana

Louisiana law governs family relationships, including marriage, divorce, and child custody. The state's family law is designed to protect the rights and interests of all parties involved, including parents, children, and spouses. In this article, we will focus on child custody, a crucial aspect of family law in Louisiana.

Louisiana Child Custody Laws

The Louisiana Child Custody Law, found in the Louisiana Revised Statutes, Title 9, Chapter 21, defines child custody as "the legal right and duty to exercise physical and legal control over a child." 1

According to Louisiana law, child custody can be classified into two types: sole custody and joint custody.

Sole Custody Joint Custody
One parent has physical and legal custody of the child. Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child.

Factors Considered in Child Custody Decisions

The Louisiana courts consider several factors when making child custody decisions. These factors are outlined in Louisiana Revised Statutes, Title 9, Chapter 21, Section 1301.1:

  • The wishes of the child, if the child is of sufficient age to express a preference;
  • The love and affection existing between the child and each parent;
  • The child's needs, including educational, emotional, and physical needs;
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to provide for the child's needs;
  • The home environment and stability of each parent's home;
  • The moral character of each parent;
  • The mental and physical health of each parent;
  • The child's relationship with each parent, including visitation rights;
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community;
  • The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment;
  • The permanence of the child's home environment;
  • The motivation of each parent in seeking or opposing custody;
  • The physical violence or threat of physical violence by the parent against the other parent, the child, or others in the household;
  • The reckless use, exhibition, or display of a firearm by the parent in the presence of the child;
  • The history of substance abuse by the parent;
  • The conviction of the parent for a crime involving moral turpitude;
  • The commission of domestic violence, including stalking, by the parent against the other parent, the child, or others in the household;
  • The existence of a history of domestic violence, including stalking, in the household or between the parties involved.

Types of Child Custody Arrangements

Louisiana law recognizes several types of child custody arrangements, including:

1. Joint Custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child. Joint custody can be further divided into:

  • Physical joint custody: Both parents have joint physical custody, meaning the child spends an equal amount of time with each parent.
  • Joint legal custody: Both parents have joint legal custody, meaning both parents have equal rights to make decisions regarding the child's education, healthcare, and other important decisions.

2. Sole Custody: One parent has physical and legal custody of the child.

3. Shared Custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child, but one parent has primary physical custody and the other parent has regular visitation rights.

4. Split Custody: Both parents have custody of different children, with one parent having custody of one child and the other parent having custody of the other child.

Child Custody Proceedings

Child custody proceedings typically involve the following steps:

  1. Petition for Custody: One parent files a petition for custody in the court, requesting that the court grant them custody of the child.
  2. Service of Process: The other parent is served with a copy of the petition and a summons, requiring them to appear in court.
  3. Temporary Custody Order: The court may grant a temporary custody order, which is a short-term order that determines custody until a final decision is made.
  4. Discovery: Both parents engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and documents related to the case.
  5. Depositions: Both parents may be required to give depositions, which are sworn statements that are used as evidence in court.
  6. Mediation: The parties may participate in mediation, which is a process where a neutral third-party helps the parties reach an agreement.
  7. Trial: The case goes to trial, where both parents present their case to the court.
  8. Final Custody Order: The court makes a final decision regarding custody, which is usually a permanent order.

Step-by-Step Guidance

Here is a step-by-step guide to help you navigate child custody proceedings in Louisiana:

  1. Contact a qualified family law attorney to discuss your case and understand your options.
  2. File a petition for custody with the court, requesting that the court grant you custody of the child.
  3. Attend a hearing to request a temporary custody order and to present your case to the court.
  4. Participate in discovery and depositions to gather evidence and information.
  5. Attend mediation to attempt to reach an agreement with the other parent.
  6. Prepare for and attend trial, where the court will make a final decision regarding custody.
  7. Follow the court's order and comply with any requirements or restrictions.

FAQs

Here are some frequently asked questions about child custody in Louisiana:

  • Q: What is the process for filing a petition for custody in Louisiana? A: The process involves filing a petition for custody with the court, serving the other parent with a copy of the petition and a summons, and attending a hearing to request a temporary custody order.
  • Q: What factors will the court consider when making a custody decision? A: The court will consider the wishes of the child, the love and affection existing between the child and each parent, the child's needs, and other factors outlined in Louisiana Revised Statutes, Title 9, Chapter 21, Section 1301.1.
  • Q: Can I represent myself in a child custody case? A: While it is possible to represent yourself, it is highly recommended that you hire a qualified family law attorney to ensure that your rights are protected and that you receive the best possible outcome.
  • Q: How long does a child custody case typically take? A: The length of time it takes to resolve a child custody case can vary widely, depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule.
  • Q: Can I request a temporary custody order while my case is pending? A: Yes, you can request a temporary custody order, which is a short-term order that determines custody until a final decision is made.
