Child Custody Laws and Guidelines in New Mexico: A Comprehensive Guide

Child custody laws in New Mexico are governed by the state's child custody statute, which prioritizes the best interests of the child above all else. In this guide, we will delve into the specifics of New Mexico's child custody laws, including the factors that are considered when determining primary custody, the presumption of joint custody, and the age at which a child can choose their own custody arrangement.

Primary Custody Factor: Best Interests of the Child

In New Mexico, the primary factor that is considered when determining child custody is the best interests of the child. This means that the court's primary concern is ensuring that the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being are protected and promoted. When making a custody determination, the court will consider the following factors:

  • The wishes of the child
  • The relationship between the child and each parent
  • The ability and willingness of each parent to provide for the child's needs
  • The moral fitness of each parent
  • The mental and physical health of each parent
  • The home environment offered by each parent
  • The child's age and needs
  • The geographical location of each parent's residence

Joint Custody Presumption: No

In New Mexico, there is no presumption of joint custody. This means that joint custody is not automatically assumed, and the court will make a decision based on the best interests of the child. However, joint custody can be awarded if it is deemed to be in the best interests of the child.

Age of Child's Custody Choice: 14

In New Mexico, a child can choose their own custody arrangement when they reach the age of 14. However, this does not automatically change the custody arrangement, and the court will still consider the best interests of the child when making a decision.

Types of Child Custody in New Mexico

New Mexico recognizes the following types of child custody:

  • Sole custody: One parent has primary physical and decision-making responsibility for the child.
  • Joint custody: Both parents share physical and decision-making responsibility for the child.
  • Split custody: One parent has primary physical and decision-making responsibility for one child, while the other parent has primary physical and decision-making responsibility for another child.
  • Shared custody: Both parents share physical responsibility for the child, but not decision-making responsibility.

How to Petition for Child Custody in New Mexico

To petition for child custody in New Mexico, you will need to file a petition with the court. You will need to provide documentation, including:

  • A sworn statement outlining your request for custody
  • A description of the child's needs and circumstances
  • A list of the witnesses you plan to call to testify on your behalf
  • A detailed plan for how you will provide for the child's needs

Child Custody Hearings and Trials in New Mexico

Child custody hearings and trials are held in front of a judge, who will make a decision based on the best interests of the child. The hearing or trial will involve the following steps:

  • Opening statements
  • Witness testimony
  • Exhibits and evidence
  • Closing statements
  • Jury deliberation (if applicable)
