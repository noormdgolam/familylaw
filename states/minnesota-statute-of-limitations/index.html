Minnesota Statute of Limitations: Overview

Understanding the Statute of Limitations in Minnesota Family Law

The statute of limitations in Minnesota is a time limit that applies to various family law matters, including divorce, child custody, and child support. It's essential to understand the statute of limitations to navigate the complexities of family law in Minnesota. In this article, we'll delve into the specifics of the statute of limitations in Minnesota family law, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, step-by-step guidance, and frequently asked questions.

Minnesota Statute of Limitations for Divorce

The statute of limitations for divorce in Minnesota is 5 years from the date of separation. According to Minnesota Statutes Section 518.06, a divorce petition must be filed within 5 years of the date of separation. If the parties separate and reconcile, the 5-year period may be tolled. However, if the parties separate and one party files a divorce petition within 5 years of the date of separation, the other party cannot defend the divorce on the grounds that the parties reconciled.

Statute of Limitations for Divorce in Minnesota Time Limit
Mn. Stat. § 518.06 5 years from the date of separation

Minnesota Statute of Limitations for Child Custody

The statute of limitations for child custody in Minnesota is 3 years from the date of the last order or judgment. According to Minnesota Statutes Section 518.18, a party may request a modification of a child custody order within 3 years of the date of the last order or judgment. If the parties agree to a modification, the 3-year period may be tolled. However, if the parties do not agree to a modification, the 3-year period begins to run from the date of the last order or judgment.

Statute of Limitations for Child Custody in Minnesota Time Limit
Mn. Stat. § 518.18 3 years from the date of the last order or judgment

Minnesota Statute of Limitations for Child Support

The statute of limitations for child support in Minnesota is 6 years from the date of the last order or judgment. According to Minnesota Statutes Section 518.24, a party may request a modification of a child support order within 6 years of the date of the last order or judgment. If the parties agree to a modification, the 6-year period may be tolled. However, if the parties do not agree to a modification, the 6-year period begins to run from the date of the last order or judgment.

Common Legal Proceedings in Minnesota Family Law

Common legal proceedings in Minnesota family law include:

  • Divorce: A divorce is a legal proceeding that dissolves a marriage. There are two types of divorce: uncontested and contested. An uncontested divorce is a divorce where the parties agree on all issues, whereas a contested divorce is a divorce where the parties do not agree on all issues.
  • Child Custody: Child custody is a legal proceeding that determines who has the right to make decisions for a child. There are two types of child custody: sole custody and joint custody. Sole custody is where one parent has the right to make decisions for the child, whereas joint custody is where both parents have the right to make decisions for the child.
  • Child Support: Child support is a legal proceeding that determines the amount of money a parent must pay to support their child. The amount of child support is determined by the court based on the parents' income and expenses.
  • Paternity: Paternity is a legal proceeding that determines the biological father of a child. Paternity can be established through a voluntary acknowledgment of paternity or through a court order.
