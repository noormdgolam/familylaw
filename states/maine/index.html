Maine Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

Family law in Maine is governed by the Maine Revised Statutes, Annotated (M.R.S.A.). The laws regarding divorce, child custody, and other family-related matters are outlined in Title 19, Chapter 7, 9, 11, and 13 of the Maine Revised Statutes.

Grounds for Divorce in Maine

Under Maine law, there are several grounds for divorce, including:

  • Irreconcilable differences (19 M.R.S.A. § 2923)
  • Adultery (19 M.R.S.A. § 2922)
  • Extreme cruelty (19 M.R.S.A. § 2921)
  • Abandonment (19 M.R.S.A. § 2925)
  • Imprisonment of a spouse for a felony (19 M.R.S.A. § 2924)
  • Mental illness or institutionalization of a spouse (19 M.R.S.A. § 2926)
  • Irreconcilable differences with a spouse who has been confined to a mental institution for 5 years or more (19 M.R.S.A. § 2927)

Residency Requirements for Divorce in Maine

To file for divorce in Maine, at least one spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least 6 months prior to the commencement of the action (19 M.R.S.A. § 2911).

Divorce Proceedings in Maine

The divorce process in Maine typically involves the following steps:

  1. Filing a complaint for divorce: The plaintiff (the spouse filing for divorce) files a complaint with the court, stating the grounds for divorce and requesting the court to grant a divorce.
  2. Serving the complaint: The plaintiff must serve the complaint on the defendant (the other spouse) by delivering a copy of the complaint to the defendant's attorney or to the defendant personally.
  3. Defendant's answer: The defendant must file an answer to the complaint within 20 days of being served, stating whether they agree or disagree with the grounds for divorce.
  4. Discovery: Both parties will engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and documents related to the divorce, including financial records and custody arrangements.
  5. Mediation: The parties may be required to attend a mediation session to attempt to resolve any disputes related to the divorce.
  6. Trial: If the parties are unable to reach a settlement, the case will proceed to trial, where a judge will make a decision on the grounds for divorce and any other issues related to the divorce.

Child Custody in Maine

Child custody in Maine is governed by the "best interests of the child" standard (19 M.R.S.A. § 2921-A). The court will consider the following factors when determining custody:

  • The child's wishes, if the child is old enough to express a preference
  • The child's physical, emotional, and educational needs
  • The parents' ability to provide a stable and loving environment
  • The parents' ability to provide adequate housing and financial support
  • The presence of any history of domestic violence or abuse
  • The child's relationship with each parent

Types of Custody in Maine

There are several types of custody in Maine, including:

  • Sole custody: One parent has physical and legal custody of the child, and the other parent has visitation rights.
  • Joint custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child, and both parents make decisions about the child's care and well-being.
  • Co-parenting: Both parents work together to make decisions about the child's care and well-being, and both parents have equal rights and responsibilities.

Custody Disputes in Maine

Custody disputes in Maine can be resolved through mediation or litigation. Mediation involves a neutral third-party who helps the parties reach a mutually acceptable agreement. Litigation involves a court-ordered custody arrangement, which may be more adversarial and expensive.

Child Support in Maine

Child support in Maine is governed by the Maine Child Support Guidelines (19 M.R.S.A. § 2921-A). The guidelines take into account the following factors when determining child support:

  • The parents' income
  • The number of children
  • The children's needs

Parental Rights and Responsibilities in Maine

In Maine, both parents have parental rights and responsibilities, including:

  • The right to make decisions about the child's care and well-being
  • The responsibility to provide a stable and loving environment
  • The responsibility to provide adequate housing and financial support
  • The responsibility to ensure the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs are met

Child Abduction in Maine

Child abduction in Maine is a serious crime that can result in criminal charges and loss of parental rights. If a parent takes a child from the other parent without permission, the other parent can file a petition with the court to enforce the custody order and recover the child.

FAQs about Maine Family Law

Here are some frequently asked questions about Maine family law:

  • Q: What is the difference between a divorce and a separation?
    A: A divorce is a legal dissolution of a marriage, whereas a separation is a temporary arrangement between spouses where they live apart but are still married.
  • Q: Can I file for divorce in Maine if I am not a resident?
    A: No, you must be a resident of Maine for at least 6 months prior to filing for divorce.
  • Q: How long does a divorce in Maine take?
    A: The length of time it takes to complete a divorce in Maine can vary widely, depending on the complexity of the case and the experience of the attorneys involved.
  • Q: Can I get a divorce in Maine if my spouse does not agree?
    A: Yes, you can file for divorce even if your spouse does not agree, but you must follow the proper procedures to ensure that your rights are protected.
