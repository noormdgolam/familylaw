Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Missouri

Missouri is one of the states in the United States that allows individuals to file for divorce, also known as dissolution of marriage. If you're considering divorce, it's essential to understand the requirements and process involved. In this guide, we'll walk you through the divorce filing process and requirements in Missouri, based on the state's laws and regulations.

Eligibility to File for Divorce in Missouri

According to Missouri law, a person must meet the residency requirement and waiting period to file for divorce. Here are the details:

  • Residency Requirement: The person filing for divorce must have been a resident of Missouri for at least 1 year.
  • Waiting Period: There is a 30-day waiting period after the divorce petition is filed before the divorce can be granted.

Fault Grounds for Divorce in Missouri

Missouri is a "fault" state, which means that a person can file for divorce based on fault grounds. Here are some examples of fault grounds in Missouri:

  • Abandonment: One spouse has abandoned the other for at least 6 months.
  • Cruel Treatment: One spouse has subjected the other to cruel and inhumane treatment.
  • Adultery: One spouse has committed adultery.
  • Impotency: One spouse is physically incapable of performing their marital duties.
  • Drug or Alcohol Addiction: One spouse is addicted to drugs or alcohol and has become a habitual drunkard or addict.
  • Prison Sentence: One spouse has been sentenced to prison for a felony and has not been pardoned or released.

Divorce Filing Process in Missouri

To file for divorce in Missouri, you'll need to follow these steps:

  1. File a Petition: The person filing for divorce, known as the petitioner, must file a petition for divorce with the court. The petition must include the grounds for divorce, the names and ages of any minor children, and the names and addresses of the spouses.
  2. Serve the Respondent: The petitioner must serve the respondent, the other spouse, with a copy of the petition and a summons. The summons will inform the respondent of the divorce proceedings and require them to appear in court.
  3. Respond to the Petition: The respondent must file a response to the petition within 30 days of being served. The response will typically include a denial of the allegations in the petition or an admission of the allegations.
  4. Mediation or Negotiation: The court may order the parties to attend mediation or negotiation to attempt to resolve any disputes.
  5. Trial: If the parties are unable to reach an agreement, the case will proceed to trial. The court will hear evidence and testimony from both parties and make a decision on the divorce.
  6. Divorce Decree: If the court grants the divorce, it will issue a divorce decree that outlines the terms of the divorce, including property division, child custody, and spousal support.

Property Division in Missouri

When it comes to property division, Missouri follows the "equitable distribution" principle. This means that the court will divide the marital property fairly, but not necessarily equally. Here are some key factors the court will consider when dividing property:

  • Length of Marriage: The length of the marriage will be a significant factor in determining the division of property.
  • Earned Income: The court will consider the income of each spouse and how it relates to the division of property.
  • Contribution to the Marriage: The court will consider the contributions of each spouse to the marriage, including financial and non-financial contributions.
  • Needs of the Parties: The court will consider the needs of each party, including their financial needs and any disabilities.

Child Custody in Missouri

When it comes to child custody, Missouri follows the "best interests of the child" principle. This means that the court will make a decision that is in the best interests of the child, taking into account the following factors:

  • Age and Needs of the Child: The age and needs of the child will be a significant factor in determining custody.
  • Parental Abilities: The court will consider the abilities and fitness of each parent to care for the child.
  • Relationship with the Child: The court will consider the relationship between the child and each parent.
  • Home Environment: The court will consider the home environment of each parent and how it will affect the child.

Spousal Support in Missouri

When it comes to spousal support, Missouri follows the "needs-based" principle. This means that the court will determine the amount of spousal support based on the needs of the spouse receiving support. Here are some key factors the court will consider:

  • Income and Needs: The court will consider the income and needs of each spouse.
  • Length of Marriage: The length of the marriage will be a significant factor in determining the amount of spousal support.
  • Contribution to the Marriage: The court will consider the contributions of each spouse to the marriage.
