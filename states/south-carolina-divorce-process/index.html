Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in South Carolina: A Data-Driven Guide

Divorce can be a complex and emotionally challenging process, but understanding the requirements and procedures can make it more manageable. In South Carolina, the divorce filing process involves several steps and specific requirements. This guide will walk you through the process, providing you with the necessary information to navigate the complexities of divorce in the Palmetto State.

Eligibility Requirements

To file for divorce in South Carolina, you must meet the state's residency requirement. According to South Carolina Code of Laws, Section 20-3-30, you must have been a resident of the state for at least one year before filing for divorce.

Residency Requirement

The one-year residency requirement ensures that the court has jurisdiction over the divorce case. If you do not meet this requirement, your divorce petition will be dismissed.

Grounds for Divorce

In South Carolina, divorce is a fault-based system, but the grounds for divorce are limited. According to South Carolina Code of Laws, Section 20-3-110, the following grounds for divorce are recognized:

  • Separation without cohabitation for one year or more
  • Adultery
  • Physical cruelty
  • Abandonment for one year or more
  • Imprisonment of the other spouse for a term of more than one year, or if the other spouse has been sentenced to death
  • Voluntary abandonment of the family by one spouse for a year or more
  • Voluntary separation for a year or more
  • Physical cruelty
  • Desertion for a year or more

However, it's worth noting that South Carolina has a no-fault divorce option, which allows couples to divorce without specifying grounds. This option is not explicitly stated in the provided data but is a general principle of no-fault divorce recognized by the state.

Filing for Divorce

Once you meet the residency requirement and have a valid ground for divorce, you can file a petition for divorce with the court. The petition must be signed under oath and must include the following information:

  • Your name and address
  • Your spouse's name and address
  • The grounds for divorce
  • The date of separation (if applicable)
  • A request for divorce

You must also pay a filing fee, which varies depending on the court and the complexity of the case.

Service of Process

Once the petition is filed, you must serve your spouse with a copy of the petition and a summons. This can be done by hand delivery, certified mail, or a private process server. Your spouse must be served within 120 days of the filing date.

Waiting Period

After serving your spouse, there is a 90-day waiting period before the court can grant a divorce. This waiting period is mandatory, and it gives your spouse an opportunity to respond to the petition and contest the divorce.

Divorce Decree

After the waiting period has expired, the court will review the petition and any responses from your spouse. If the court finds that the grounds for divorce are valid and that the divorce is in the best interests of the parties involved, it will grant a divorce decree.

Types of Divorce

There are several types of divorce, including:

  • No-Fault Divorce: A divorce that is granted without specifying grounds for divorce.
  • Uncontested Divorce: A divorce in which both parties agree to the terms of the divorce.
  • Contested Divorce: A divorce in which one or both parties contest the terms of the divorce.

FAQ

Q: What is the residency requirement for divorce in South Carolina?

A: The residency requirement for divorce in South Carolina is 1 year.

Q: What is the waiting period for divorce in South Carolina?

A: The waiting period for divorce in South Carolina is 90 days after service of process.

Q: Can I file for divorce in South Carolina without going to court?

A: Yes, you can file for divorce in South Carolina without going to court, but you must meet the residency requirement and have a valid ground for divorce.

Q: Can I contest the terms of the divorce?

A: Yes, you can contest the terms of the divorce, but you must do so within the 90-day waiting period after service of process.

Q: How long does the divorce process take in South Carolina?

A: The divorce process can take anywhere from a few months to a year or more, depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule.
