Divorce Filing Process and Requirements in Arizona: A Comprehensive Guide

The decision to file for divorce can be a difficult and emotional process. In Arizona, the process is governed by state laws and regulations. This guide will walk you through the divorce filing process and requirements in Arizona, providing you with a deep understanding of the necessary steps and legal considerations.

Understanding the Divorce Process in Arizona

The divorce process in Arizona can be initiated by either spouse. It involves several key steps, including filing a petition for divorce, serving the other spouse, and waiting for the required period before the divorce is finalized.

Residency Requirement

In Arizona, the residency requirement for filing a divorce is 6 months. This means that either spouse must have been a resident of the state for at least 6 months prior to filing for divorce. This requirement ensures that the court has jurisdiction over the divorce proceedings.

Waiting Period

The waiting period for divorce in Arizona is 90 days. This means that after the petition for divorce is filed and served on the other spouse, there must be a 90-day waiting period before the divorce can be finalized. This period allows the spouses to reconcile or seek counseling, if desired.

Filing a Petition for Divorce

To file a petition for divorce in Arizona, the spouse must complete and file a pleading, known as a Petition for Dissolution of Marriage (Form 02-101). The petition must include information about the marriage, including the date of marriage, the length of the marriage, and the reasons for the divorce.

Grounds for Divorce

Arizona is a "no-fault" state, but it also allows for fault grounds in certain circumstances. The grounds for divorce in Arizona include:

  • Irreconcilable differences
  • Adultery
  • Cruelty
  • Desertion
  • Abandonment
  • Imprisonment
  • Bigamy

Service of Process

After the petition for divorce is filed, the other spouse must be served with process, which includes a copy of the petition and a summons. The spouse who files the petition is responsible for serving the other spouse, either personally or through a process server.

Responding to the Petition for Divorce

The spouse who is served with the petition for divorce must respond to the petition within 20 days of service. The response must include a sworn answer, which admits or denies the allegations in the petition. If the spouse fails to respond, the court may enter a default judgment against them.

Divorce Actions and Requirements in Arizona

Divorce Action/Requirement Description
Uncontested Divorce Divorce where both spouses agree on the terms of the divorce, including property division, spousal support, and child custody.
Contested Divorce Divorce where the spouses do not agree on the terms of the divorce, requiring a court trial to resolve the disputes.
Legal Separation A legal agreement between spouses to live separately, but remain married, usually for a period of time before deciding whether to divorce.
Separate Maintenance A court-ordered financial support arrangement for a spouse who is not entitled to spousal support under the divorce agreement.
Child Custody The court's decision regarding which parent will have physical and legal custody of the children, and the terms of visitation for the non-custodial parent.
Property Division The court's decision regarding how to divide the marital property, including real estate, personal property, and other assets.
Spousal Support The court's decision regarding which spouse is entitled to financial support from the other, and the duration and amount of support.

FAQs

Q: How long does it take to get a divorce in Arizona?

A: The waiting period for divorce in Arizona is 90 days, but the actual length of time it takes to get a divorce can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the availability of the court.

Q: Can I file for divorce in Arizona if I am not a resident?

A: No, in order to file for divorce in Arizona, you must be a resident of the state for at least 6 months prior to filing.

Q: What is the difference between a contested and uncontested divorce?

A: A contested divorce is a divorce where the spouses do not agree on the terms of the divorce, requiring a court trial to resolve the disputes. An uncontested divorce is a divorce where both spouses agree on the terms of the divorce, including property division, spousal support, and child custody.

Q: Can I get a divorce in Arizona if my spouse is not willing to sign the divorce papers?

A: Yes, you can still file for divorce in Arizona even if your spouse is not willing to sign the divorce papers. However, you will need to serve the other spouse with process and follow the court's procedures for contested divorces.
