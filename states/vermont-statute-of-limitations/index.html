Family Law Statute of Limitations in Vermont: A Comprehensive Guide

In Vermont, the statute of limitations (SOL) plays a crucial role in determining the time frame within which family law-related claims can be brought to court. Understanding the SOL is essential for individuals navigating family law matters, including divorce, child custody, and property division. In this guide, we will delve into the specific SOLs applicable to various family law-related actions in Vermont, as well as provide a comprehensive overview of the relevant laws and regulations.

Statute of Limitations Overview

The SOL is a law that sets a time limit for filing a lawsuit or taking legal action. In Vermont, the SOL varies depending on the type of action, ranging from 2 to 10 years. The following are the key SOLs relevant to family law in Vermont:

Statute of Limitations for Personal Injury, Property Damage, and Breach of Contract

In Vermont, the SOL for personal injury claims is 2 years, commencing from the date of the injury. For property damage claims, the SOL is 3 years, starting from the date of damage. Lastly, for breach of contract claims, the SOL is 10 years, counting from the date of the breach.

Tolling the Statute of Limitations

The SOL can be tolled (or suspended) under certain circumstances. In Vermont, the SOL may be tolled if:

  • The claimant is a minor (under the age of 18) or mentally incapacitated.
  • The defendant is absent from the state or has concealed themself to avoid service.
  • The claimant is unaware of the injury or damage.
  • The claimant is prevented from bringing a lawsuit due to war, civil unrest, or other extraordinary circumstances.

Exceptions to the Statute of Limitations

While the SOL generally applies to family law-related actions, there are exceptions. In Vermont, the following actions are exempt from the SOL:

  • Actions for child support and maintenance.
  • Actions for spousal support and maintenance.
  • Actions for domestic violence and abuse.
