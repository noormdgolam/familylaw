Utah Family Law: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

 

Introduction

Utah family law governs the relationships between individuals, including marriages, divorce, child custody, and child support. In this article, we will delve into the specifics of Utah family law, focusing on divorce and child custody. We will explore state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and provide step-by-step guidance to help individuals navigate this complex and sensitive area of law.

 

Utah Family Law Statutes

The Utah Code, Title 30, Chapter 3, sets forth the laws governing divorce, child custody, and child support in the state of Utah. Some key statutes include:

 

Divorce

 

30-3-1. Grounds for divorce

The following are grounds for divorce in the state of Utah:

 

Grounds Description
No-fault divorce The couple has been separated for at least 3 years, and there are no minor children of the marriage.
Irreconcilable differences The couple has been separated for at least 90 days, and there are no minor children of the marriage.
Adultery One spouse has committed adultery and the other spouse is not willing to continue the marriage.
Abuse One spouse has committed abuse and the other spouse is not willing to continue the marriage.

 

Child Custody

 

30-3-10. Best interests of the child

The court shall award custody in accordance with the best interests of the child. In determining the best interests of the child, the court shall consider the following factors:

 

  • The wishes of the parents or guardians;
  • The wishes of the child;
  • The child's needs and circumstances; and
  • The ability of each parent or guardian to care for the child.

 

Child Support

 

30-3-10.1. Child support guidelines

The court shall use the following guidelines to determine child support:

 

  • The gross income of both parents;
  • The number of children;
  • The cost of healthcare for the children;
  • The cost of daycare for the children;
  • The cost of education for the children;
  • The amount of time each parent spends with the children; and
  • The ability of each parent to pay child support.

 

Common Legal Proceedings

Utah family law involves a variety of legal proceedings, including:

 

Divorce Proceedings

 

1. Filing a petition for divorce: One spouse files a petition with the court, stating the grounds for divorce.

 

2. Service of process: The other spouse is served with a copy of the petition and a summons to appear in court.

 

3. Temporary orders: The court may issue temporary orders regarding custody, child support, and other issues.

 

4. Discovery: Both spouses exchange information and evidence in preparation for trial.

 

5. Trial: The court hears evidence and testimony from both spouses and makes a final decision.

 

Child Custody Proceedings

 

1. Filing a petition for custody: One spouse files a petition with the court, requesting custody of the child.

 

2. Service of process: The other spouse is served with a copy of the petition and a summons to appear in court.

 

3. Temporary orders: The court may issue temporary orders regarding custody and visitation.

 

4. Discovery: Both spouses exchange information and evidence in preparation for trial.

 

5. Trial: The court hears evidence and testimony from both spouses and makes a final decision.

Step-by-Step Guidance

Here is a step-by-step guide to help individuals navigate the divorce or child custody process in Utah:

 

Step 1: Determine Eligibility

 

Check if you are eligible to file for divorce or child custody in Utah.

 

Step 2: Gather Information

 

Gather all relevant information, including financial records, witness statements, and any other evidence that may be relevant to the case.

 

Step 3: File a Petition

 

File a petition with the court, stating the grounds for divorce or child custody.

 

Step 4: Serve the Other Spouse

 

Serve the other spouse with a copy of the petition and a summons to appear in court.

 

Step 5: Attend a Hearing

 

Attend a hearing with the court to present evidence and testimony.

 

Step 6: Receive a Decision

 

Receive a decision from the court, which may include a final divorce decree or a child custody order.
