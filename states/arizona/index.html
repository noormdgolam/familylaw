Family Law in Arizona: A Comprehensive Guide to Divorce and Child Custody

In the state of Arizona, family law governs all matters related to marriage, divorce, child custody, and child support. As a highly experienced family law attorney and legal consultant, it is my duty to provide a comprehensive guide to help you navigate the complex process of divorce and child custody in Arizona.

Overview of Arizona Family Law

Arizona Revised Statutes (A.R.S.) Title 25 governs family law, including marriage, divorce, child custody, and child support. The Arizona Court of Appeals and the Arizona Supreme Court have also issued numerous decisions that have shaped family law in the state.

Grounds for Divorce in Arizona

In Arizona, both no-fault and fault-based grounds for divorce are recognized. The grounds for a no-fault divorce are:

  • Irreconcilable differences;
  • Irretrievable breakdown of the marriage;
  • Irreconcilable differences of opinion and irreconcilable differences of temper.

The grounds for a fault-based divorce in Arizona are:

  • Adultery;
  • Abandonment;
  • Extreme cruelty;
  • Desertion;
  • Conviction of a felony;
  • Imprisonment for a term of more than one year;
  • Failure to provide for the spouse's support;
  • Excessive use of intoxicating liquors or narcotics;
  • Excessive use of drugs;
  • Excessive use of controlled substances;
  • Excessive use of mood-altering substances.

Steps to File for Divorce in Arizona

To file for divorce in Arizona, follow these steps:

  1. Meet the Residency Requirement: One spouse must have been a resident of Arizona for at least 90 days before filing for divorce.
  2. Choose a Grounds for Divorce: Select a no-fault or fault-based ground for divorce.
  3. Prepare the Petition: The spouse filing for divorce must prepare a petition for dissolution of marriage, which includes:
    • A statement of the grounds for divorce;
    • A statement of the marriage dates;
    • A statement of the children's names and ages (if applicable);
    • A statement of the parties' incomes and assets;
    • A statement of the relief sought (e.g., division of property, child custody, child support).
  4. File the Petition: File the petition with the court and pay the filing fee.
  5. Serve the Other Spouse: The spouse filing for divorce must serve the other spouse with a copy of the petition and a summons.
  6. Wait for a Response: The other spouse has 20 days to respond to the petition.
  7. Participate in the Court Process: Both spouses must participate in the court process, which may include:
    • Discovery (e.g., exchanging financial information, witness statements);
    • Mediation (e.g., settlement negotiations);
    • Court hearings (e.g., trial, contempt proceedings).

Child Custody in Arizona

In Arizona, child custody is determined based on the best interests of the child. The court considers the following factors:

  • The child's wishes (if the child is old enough to express a preference);
  • The child's relationship with each parent;
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community;
  • The mental and physical health of the parents;
  • The parents' ability to provide a stable home environment;
  • The parents' willingness to cooperate with each other;
  • The presence of any history of abuse or neglect;
  • The presence of any other relevant factors.

The court may award one of the following types of child custody:

  • Sole Custody: One parent has sole custody of the child.
  • Joint Custody: Both parents share custody of the child.
  • Shared Custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, but one parent has primary physical custody.
  • Legal Custody: One or both parents have decision-making authority for the child.

Child Support in Arizona

In Arizona, child support is determined based on the Arizona Child Support Guidelines. The court considers the following factors:

  • The parents' incomes;
  • The number of children;
  • The children's ages;
  • The children's needs;
  • The parents' ability to pay;
  • The parents' work schedule;
  • The parents' education and job skills.

Attorney Fees in Arizona

In Arizona, attorney fees are typically awarded to the prevailing party in a family law case. The court considers the following factors:

  • The reasonableness of the attorney's fees;
  • The complexity of the case;
  • The time and effort required by the attorney;
  • The attorney's expertise and qualifications;
  • The amount in controversy;
  • The parties' financial resources.

Average attorney fees for a family law case in Arizona range from $5,000 to $20,000 or more, depending on the complexity of the case and the attorney's experience.

FAQs

Here are some frequently asked questions about family law in Arizona:

Q: How long does a divorce take in Arizona?

A: The length of a divorce in Arizona depends on the complexity of the case and the parties' ability to reach an agreement. A simple divorce can take as little as 30 days, while a complex divorce can take several months or even years.

Q: Can I file for divorce without an attorney?

A: Yes, you can file for divorce without an attorney, but it is highly recommended that you seek the advice of an experienced family law attorney to ensure that your rights are protected.

Q: How do I determine child custody in Arizona?

A: Child custody is determined based on the best interests of the child. The court considers the child's wishes, the child's relationship with each parent, the child's adjustment to home, school, and community, and other relevant factors.

Q: How do I calculate child support in Arizona?

A: Child support is calculated using the Arizona Child Support Guidelines, which take into account the parents' incomes, the number of children, the children's ages, and other relevant factors.

Q: Can I modify my child custody or child support order in Arizona?

A: Yes, you can modify your child custody or child support order in Arizona if there has been a change in circumstances that affects the child's best interests or the parents' ability to pay child support.
