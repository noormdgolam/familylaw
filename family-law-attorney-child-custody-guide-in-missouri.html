Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in Missouri

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Child custody is a crucial aspect of family law in Missouri, affecting the well-being and lives of children and their parents. As a family law attorney, it's essential to understand the complexities of child custody laws in Missouri, the common legal proceedings, and the expected attorney costs.

Missouri Child Custody Laws

In Missouri, the primary consideration in child custody cases is the best interests of the child. The court uses the Missouri Model Family Law Rules of Procedure to guide its decisions. Missouri law also recognizes the importance of maintaining relationships between children and both parents, unless it's detrimental to the child's welfare.

The Missouri Revised Statutes, Chapter 452 outline the following types of custody:

  • Custody of the child, which includes physical possession and control of the child;
  • Visitation rights, which include the right to visit and communicate with the child;
  • Joint custody, which involves shared decision-making and physical possession of the child;
  • Split custody, which involves two or more children being placed in the care of different parents;
  • Sole custody, which involves one parent having sole decision-making and physical possession of the child;

Types of Child Custody Arrangements

There are several types of child custody arrangements that may be considered in Missouri:

  • Joint Custody: Both parents share decision-making and physical possession of the child.
  • Sole Custody: One parent has sole decision-making and physical possession of the child.
  • Split Custody: Two or more children are placed in the care of different parents.
  • Shared Custody: Both parents share decision-making and physical possession of the child, but not necessarily equally.
  • Primary Custody: One parent has primary physical possession and decision-making responsibility.

Child Custody Legal Proceedings in Missouri

Child custody legal proceedings in Missouri typically follow this general outline:

Step 1: Filing a Petition

  • The petitioning parent (petitioner) files a petition for custody with the court.
  • The petition must include the names and ages of the children, the parents' names and addresses, and a statement of the parents' wishes regarding custody.

Step 2: Service of Process

  • The court issues a summons to the non-petitioning parent (respondent).
  • The respondent is served with the summons and petition.

Step 3: Temporary Custody Order

  • The court may issue a temporary custody order while the case is pending.
  • The temporary custody order sets forth the temporary custody arrangement until a final decision is made.

Step 4: Discovery and Depositions

  • The parties engage in discovery, which includes exchanging information and documents related to the custody case.
  • Depositions may be taken to gather information and testimony from witnesses.

Step 5: Mediation and Settlement

  • The parties may engage in mediation to resolve the custody dispute.
  • A settlement may be reached, which includes a custody arrangement and other terms.

Step 6: Trial

  • If the parties are unable to reach a settlement, the case goes to trial.
  • The court hears evidence and testimony from witnesses to make a decision regarding custody.

Factors Considered in Child Custody Cases

The court considers the following factors in child custody cases in Missouri:

Factor Description
Best Interests of the Child The court's primary consideration is the best interests of the child.
Age and Maturity of the Child The court considers the child's age and maturity level when determining custody.
Parent-Child Relationship The court evaluates the relationship between the child and each parent.
Ability of Each Parent to Provide a Stable Home Environment The court considers each parent's ability to provide a stable and nurturing environment for the child.
Parenting Style and Ability The court evaluates each parent's parenting style and ability to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs.
Domestic Violence or Abuse The court considers whether there has been domestic violence or abuse in the home.
Substance Abuse or Addiction The court evaluates whether either parent has a substance abuse or addiction issue.
Geographic Location and Distance Between Parents The court considers the distance between the parents and the feasibility of maintaining a relationship between the child and each parent.
Other Factors The court may consider other factors not listed above, such as the child's physical and emotional needs, the parents' work schedules, and the availability of support systems.

Expected Attorney Costs in Child Custody Cases in Missouri

The cost of hiring an attorney in a child custody case in Missouri can vary widely depending on several factors, including:

  • The complexity of the case;
  • The attorney's experience and expertise;
  • The location of the attorney's office;
  • The number of hours spent on the case.

Hourly Rates

Attorney hourly rates in Missouri can range from $150 to $500 per hour, depending on the attorney's experience and location.

Flat Fees

Some attorneys may charge flat fees for child custody cases, which can range from $1,500 to $5,000 or more, depending on the complexity of the case.

Contingent Fees

Some attorneys may charge contingent fees, which are a percentage of the award or settlement. Contingent fees can range from 20% to 40% of the award or settlement.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Child Custody Cases in Missouri

Here's a step-by-step guide to help you navigate a child custody case in Missouri:

  1. Gather Information: Collect all relevant documents, including the child's birth certificate, Social Security number, and any relevant medical or educational records.
  2. Contact an Attorney: Hire an experienced family law attorney to represent you in the case.
  3. File a Petition: The attorney will help you file a petition for custody with the court.
  4. Wait for Service: The court will issue a summons to the non-petitioning parent, and they will be served with the petition.
  5. Temporary Custody Order: The court may issue a temporary custody order while the case is pending.
  6. Discovery and Depositions: The parties will engage in discovery, which includes exchanging information and documents related to the custody case.
  7. Mediation and Settlement: The parties may engage in mediation to resolve the custody dispute.
  8. Trial: If the parties are unable to reach a settlement, the case will go to trial.
  9. Final Custody Order: The court will issue a final custody order, which will determine the custody arrangement and visitation schedule.
