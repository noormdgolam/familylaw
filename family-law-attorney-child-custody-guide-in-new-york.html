New York Child Custody Laws and Procedures: A Comprehensive Guide

In the state of New York, child custody laws are governed by the Family Court Act (FCA) and the Domestic Relations Law (DRL). As a family law attorney, understanding the intricacies of child custody laws is crucial to ensuring the best interests of children involved in custody disputes.

Types of Child Custody in New York

In New York, there are two main types of child custody: physical custody and legal custody.

  • Physical Custody: Refers to the physical care and supervision of the child. This type of custody can be awarded to one parent or joint physical custody, where both parents share the child's physical care.
  • Legal Custody: Refers to the right to make decisions regarding the child's education, healthcare, and welfare. This type of custody can also be awarded jointly or solely to one parent.

Additionally, there are two other types of custody: Residential Custody and Supervised Custody. Residential custody refers to the parent with whom the child primarily resides, while supervised custody refers to a situation where the child's interactions with one parent are supervised by the other parent or a third party.

Factors Considered in Determining Child Custody in New York

In New York, the court considers the following factors when determining child custody:

  • Best Interests of the Child: The court's primary consideration is the best interests of the child. This includes the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being.
  • Parental Fitness: The court assesses the fitness of each parent to provide a stable and loving environment for the child.
  • Parent-Child Relationship: The court examines the relationship between the child and each parent, including the quality of the relationship and the frequency of contact.
  • Child's Wishes: Depending on the child's age and maturity, the court may consider the child's wishes regarding custody.
  • Economic Stability: The court considers the economic stability of each parent and how it may impact the child's well-being.
  • Geographic Location: The court considers the geographic location of each parent and how it may impact the child's access to both parents.
  • Criminal History: The court may consider a parent's criminal history and how it may impact the child's safety and well-being.

Common Legal Proceedings in Child Custody Cases in New York

The following are common legal proceedings in child custody cases in New York:

Proceeding Description
Initial Appearance The first court appearance in a child custody case, where the parties are introduced to the court and the case is scheduled for a hearing.
Order to Show Cause A court order requiring the parties to appear in court to show cause why a specific action should or should not be taken in the case.
Hearing A court hearing where evidence is presented and testimony is taken to determine child custody.
Discovery The process of exchanging information and evidence between parties in a child custody case.
Motion Practice The process of filing motions with the court to request specific actions or orders in a child custody case.
Mediation A process where the parties attempt to resolve their differences through the assistance of a neutral third-party mediator.
Trial The final court hearing in a child custody case, where the parties present evidence and testimony to determine custody.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Child Custody Cases in New York

The following is a step-by-step guide for navigating child custody cases in New York:

  1. File a Petition: The first step in a child custody case is to file a petition with the court. This petition must be signed and verified by the party seeking custody.
  2. Serve the Other Party: The party filing the petition must serve the other party with a copy of the petition and a notice of the court date.
  3. Initial Appearance: The parties must appear in court for an initial appearance, where the case is scheduled for a hearing.
  4. Discovery: The parties must exchange information and evidence during the discovery process.
  5. Motion Practice: The parties may file motions with the court to request specific actions or orders.
  6. Mediation: The parties may choose to participate in mediation to attempt to resolve their differences.
  7. Hearing: The parties must appear in court for a hearing, where evidence is presented and testimony is taken.
  8. Trial: If the case does not settle during the hearing, the parties must appear in court for a trial, where the court makes a final determination regarding custody.

Expected Attorney Costs in Child Custody Cases in New York

The cost of hiring an attorney for a child custody case in New York can vary depending on the complexity of the case, the attorney's experience, and the location of the attorney's office.

On average, the cost of hiring an attorney for a child custody case in New York can range from $200 to $500 per hour. In addition to hourly fees, attorneys may also charge a flat fee for specific services, such as filing a petition or representing a client at a hearing.

It is essential to note that the court may order one or both parties to pay attorney's fees and costs in a child custody case.

FAQs about Child Custody in New York

The following are some frequently asked questions about child custody in New York:

  • Q: How long does a child custody case take? A: The length of a child custody case can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule.
  • Q: Can I represent myself in a child custody case? A: Yes, but it is highly recommended that you hire an attorney to represent you in a child custody case.
  • Q: What is the best way to present evidence in a child custody case? A: The best way to present evidence in a child custody case is to use a combination of written and oral testimony.
  • Q: Can I change the custody arrangement after it has been established? A: Yes, but you must file a new petition with the court and follow the procedures for modifying a custody arrangement.
  • Q: How do I enforce a custody order? A: To enforce a custody order, you must file a motion with the court and provide evidence of the other party's non-compliance with the order.
