Introduction to Child Custody in Hawaii

In Hawaii, child custody is governed by the Hawaii Revised Statutes (HRS) Chapter 571, which outlines the laws and procedures for determining child custody. The primary consideration in child custody cases is the best interests of the child, with the court seeking to ensure the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being. Hawaii courts recognize two types of child custody: legal custody and physical custody. Legal custody refers to the right to make decisions regarding the child's upbringing, education, and healthcare, while physical custody refers to the child's living arrangements.

Types of Child Custody in Hawaii

There are several types of child custody arrangements that may be awarded in Hawaii, including:

  • SOLE CUSTODY: One parent has sole legal and physical custody of the child.
  • JOINT CUSTODY: Both parents share legal and physical custody of the child.
  • SHARED CUSTODY: Both parents share physical custody of the child, but one parent may have primary physical custody.
  • ALTERNATING CUSTODY: The child spends alternating periods of time with each parent.

State-Specific Statutes Governing Child Custody in Hawaii

Hawaii Revised Statutes (HRS) Chapter 571 outlines the laws and procedures for determining child custody in Hawaii. Some key statutes include:

  • HRS §571-46: This statute governs the determination of child custody and requires the court to consider the best interests of the child.
  • HRS §571-47: This statute outlines the factors the court must consider when determining child custody, including the child's wishes, the parents' ability to cooperate, and the child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • HRS §571-48: This statute governs the modification of child custody orders and requires the moving party to demonstrate a significant change in circumstances.

Common Legal Proceedings in Child Custody Cases

Child custody cases in Hawaii may involve several legal proceedings, including:

  • COMPLAINT FOR DIVORCE OR SEPARATION: A complaint for divorce or separation may include a request for child custody.
  • PETITION FOR CUSTODY: A petition for custody may be filed as a standalone action or in conjunction with a complaint for divorce or separation.
  • MOTION FOR TEMPORARY CUSTODY: A motion for temporary custody may be filed to establish a temporary custody arrangement pending the outcome of the underlying case.
  • TRIAL: A trial may be necessary to resolve disputes regarding child custody.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Child Custody Cases in Hawaii

The following steps provide a general overview of the child custody process in Hawaii:

  1. CONSULT WITH A FAMILY LAW ATTORNEY: Schedule a consultation with a Family Law attorney to discuss your child custody case and determine the best course of action.
  2. FILE A COMPLAINT OR PETITION: File a complaint for divorce or separation or a petition for custody to initiate the child custody process.
  3. SERVE THE OTHER PARTY: Serve the other party with the complaint or petition and any supporting documents.
  4. NEGOTIATE A CUSTODY AGREEMENT: Attempt to negotiate a custody agreement with the other party.
  5. ATTEND A HEARING: Attend a hearing to establish a temporary custody arrangement or to resolve any disputes regarding child custody.
  6. PREPARE FOR TRIAL: Prepare for trial by gathering evidence, interviewing witnesses, and developing a trial strategy.
  7. ATTEND TRIAL: Attend trial to present your case to the court and advocate for your desired custody arrangement.

Frequently Asked Questions About Child Custody in Hawaii

The following FAQs provide additional information about child custody in Hawaii:

  • Q: What is the primary consideration in child custody cases in Hawaii?
  • A: The primary consideration in child custody cases in Hawaii is the best interests of the child.
  • Q: Can I represent myself in a child custody case in Hawaii?
  • A: While it is possible to represent yourself in a child custody case in Hawaii, it is highly recommended that you hire a Family Law attorney to ensure your rights are protected and your interests are advocated for.
  • Q: How long does a child custody case in Hawaii typically take to resolve?
  • A: The length of time it takes to resolve a child custody case in Hawaii can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. On average, a child custody case can take several months to several years to resolve.
  • Q: Can I appeal a child custody decision in Hawaii?
  • A: Yes, you can appeal a child custody decision in Hawaii. However, appeals are typically only granted in cases where there has been an error of law or an abuse of discretion.
