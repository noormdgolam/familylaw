Georgia's Child Custody Laws: An Overview

The state of Georgia is governed by the Georgia Child Custody Act, which outlines the principles and procedures for determining child custody. According to O.C.G.A. § 19-9-3, the primary consideration in child custody determinations is the best interest of the child. The court shall consider all relevant factors, including:

  • The wishes of the child's parents or other individuals responsible for the child's care
  • The child's wishes, if the child is of sufficient age and maturity
  • The child's relationship with each parent and other individuals
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community
  • The physical and mental health of all individuals involved
  • The stability and continuity of the child's environment
  • The moral fitness of the parents
  • The willingness and ability of each parent to cooperate in the exercise of parental responsibilities
  • The ability of each parent to provide for the child's needs
  • The geographical proximity of the parents' residences

In cases where the parents are not married, the court shall consider the same factors as above, as well as the following:

  • The mother's right to custody of the child, as provided by Georgia law
  • The father's rights, if the child's paternity has been established

Types of Child Custody Arrangements in Georgia

Georgia law recognizes several types of child custody arrangements, including:

Sole Custody

Sole custody is a type of child custody arrangement in which one parent has exclusive physical and legal custody of the child. The non-custodial parent may be granted visitation rights, but the custodial parent has the primary responsibility for making decisions regarding the child's care and well-being.

Joint Custody

Joint custody is a type of child custody arrangement in which both parents share physical and legal custody of the child. Joint custody can be either:

  • Joint physical custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, with each parent having a separate residence
  • Joint legal custody: Both parents share decision-making authority for the child's care and well-being

Split Custody

Split custody is a type of child custody arrangement in which each parent has custody of one or more children, but not all children. For example, one parent may have custody of the older child, while the other parent has custody of the younger child.

Common Legal Proceedings in Georgia Child Custody Cases

The following are common legal proceedings in Georgia child custody cases:

Filing a Petition for Custody

A parent seeking to establish or modify child custody must file a petition with the court. The petition must include the following:

  • A statement of the parent's name, address, and phone number
  • A statement of the child's name, date of birth, and residence
  • A statement of the parent's relationship to the child
  • A statement of the grounds for the petition

Temporary Custody Hearings

Temporary custody hearings are held to determine the temporary custody arrangement for the child until a final custody determination is made.

Custody Evaluations

Custody evaluations are conducted by a court-appointed expert, such as a psychologist or social worker, to assess the child's needs and determine the best custody arrangement.

Custody Trials

Custody trials are held to determine the final custody arrangement for the child. Both parents have the opportunity to present evidence and testimony to support their case.
