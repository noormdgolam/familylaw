Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in South Carolina

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

As a highly experienced Family Law attorney and legal consultant, I am pleased to provide you with this comprehensive guide to child custody in South Carolina. Child custody is a critical aspect of family law, and it is essential to understand the laws and procedures involved in South Carolina. In this article, we will cover state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, step-by-step guidance, and frequently asked questions.

Overview of Child Custody Laws in South Carolina

South Carolina child custody laws are governed by the Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA) and the South Carolina Family Court Code. The primary goal of child custody laws in South Carolina is to determine what is in the best interest of the child.

Types of Custody Orders

There are two main types of custody orders in South Carolina: legal custody and physical custody.

  • Legal Custody: Refers to the right to make major decisions about a child's life, such as education, healthcare, and religion.
  • Physical Custody: Refers to the actual care and supervision of the child.

Custody Arrangements

Custody arrangements can be classified into three main categories:

  • Sole Custody: One parent has both legal and physical custody of the child.
  • Joint Custody: Both parents share legal and physical custody of the child.
  • Shared Custody: Both parents share joint custody, but one parent has primary physical custody.

Factors Considered in Determining Custody

When determining custody, the court considers several factors, including:

  • The love, affection, and relationship between the child and each parent.
  • The ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs.
  • The stability of each parent's home environment.
  • The moral fitness of each parent.
  • The child's preference (if the child is old enough to express a preference).
  • The wishes of each parent regarding custody.

Common Legal Proceedings

There are several common legal proceedings involved in child custody cases in South Carolina:

Initial Custody Proceedings

When a custody dispute arises, one parent may file a petition with the court to establish custody. The court will schedule a hearing to determine custody, and both parents will be required to attend.

Custody Modification Proceedings

If the custody arrangement is not working and a parent wishes to modify the existing custody order, they may file a petition with the court. The court will schedule a hearing to determine whether the modification is in the best interest of the child.

Contempt Proceedings

If one parent fails to comply with a custody order, the other parent may file a petition for contempt with the court. The court will schedule a hearing to determine whether the parent has committed contempt.

Step-by-Step Guidance

To navigate the child custody process in South Carolina, follow these steps:

Step 1: Consult with an Attorney

It is essential to consult with an experienced Family Law attorney to understand your rights and options. Your attorney will guide you through the process and help you prepare for court.

Step 2: File a Petition

If you wish to establish or modify custody, you must file a petition with the court. Your attorney will help you prepare the petition and ensure it is filed correctly.

Step 3: Attend a Hearing

The court will schedule a hearing to determine custody, and both parents will be required to attend. Your attorney will present evidence and arguments to support your position.

Step 4: Receive a Custody Order

After the hearing, the court will issue a custody order. The order will outline the terms of custody, including visitation rights and decision-making authority.

Step 5: Enforce the Custody Order

Once the custody order is issued, both parents are required to comply with its terms. If one parent fails to comply, the other parent may file a petition for contempt with the court.

Expected Attorney Costs

The cost of hiring an attorney for a child custody case in South Carolina can vary depending on several factors, including:

  • The complexity of the case.
  • The attorney's experience and reputation.
  • The location of the attorney's office.

On average, attorneys in South Carolina may charge anywhere from $200 to $500 per hour for child custody cases. The total cost of the case can range from $1,000 to $10,000 or more, depending on the complexity of the case and the number of court appearances.

FAQs

Here are some frequently asked questions about child custody in South Carolina:

Q: What is the best way to determine custody?

A: The court considers several factors, including the love, affection, and relationship between the child and each parent, the ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and educational needs, the stability of each parent's home environment, the moral fitness of each parent, and the child's preference (if the child is old enough to express a preference).

Q: Can I file for custody if I am not married to the other parent?

A: Yes, you can file for custody even if you are not married to the other parent. The court will consider the same factors as in married cases, including the best interest of the child.

Q: How long does a custody case typically take?

A: The length of time a custody case takes can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. Some cases may be resolved in a matter of weeks, while others may take several months or even years to resolve.

Q: Can I change my custody arrangement without going to court?

A: Yes, you can change your custody arrangement without going to court by agreeing with the other parent on a new arrangement. However, if the other parent does not agree, you may need to go to court to modify the existing custody order.

Q: What happens if I lose custody of my child?

A: If you lose custody of your child, you may still be able to see your child and maintain a relationship with them. However, you will not have the same rights and responsibilities as the parent who has primary custody.

Q: Can I get custody of my child if I have a history of substance abuse or domestic violence?

A: It may be more difficult to get custody of your child if you have a history of substance abuse or domestic violence. The court will consider whether it is in the best interest of the child to award custody to a parent with a history of substance abuse or domestic violence.

Q: Can I get custody of my child if I live in a different state?

A: Yes, you can get custody of your child even if you live in a different state. However, you will need to file a petition in the court where the child resides or is found, and the court will consider the laws of that state in making its decision.

Q: What is the Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA)?

A: The UCCJEA is a federal law that governs child custody cases in the United States. It sets out the rules for determining which state has jurisdiction over a child custody case and how to enforce custody orders across state lines.
