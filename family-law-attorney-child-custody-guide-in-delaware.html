Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in Delaware

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

As a Family Law attorney and legal consultant, I have extensive experience in navigating the complex and often emotionally charged realm of child custody cases in Delaware. In this article, we will delve into the intricacies of Delaware's Family Law system, exploring state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance for parents seeking to establish or modify child custody arrangements.

Introduction to Delaware Family Law and Child Custody

Delaware's Family Law system is designed to prioritize the best interests of the child in all custody and visitation matters. Under Delaware law, parents have a fundamental right to the custody of their children, but this right is not absolute. When parents are unable to agree on a custody arrangement, the court will intervene to determine a custody plan that serves the child's best interests. Delaware's child custody laws are primarily codified in Title 13, Chapter 22 of the Delaware Code.

Types of Child Custody in Delaware

Delaware recognizes several types of child custody arrangements, including:

  • Joint Custody: Both parents share decision-making responsibilities and physical custody of the child.
  • Sole Custody: One parent has primary decision-making authority and physical custody of the child.
  • Shared Custody: Both parents have significant periods of physical custody, but one parent may have primary physical custody.
  • Split Custody: Each parent has sole custody of one or more children.

Delaware's Child Custody Statutes

Delaware's child custody statutes are designed to promote the best interests of the child. Under 13 Del. C. § 722, the court shall consider all relevant factors, including:

  • The wishes of the child's parents as to custody.
  • The wishes of the child as to custody.
  • The interaction and interrelationship of the child with the child's parents, grandparents, siblings, and other significant persons.
  • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community.
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved.
  • Past and present compliance by both parents with their parenting obligations, including prompt and regular payment of child support when required, and other obligations to their child.
  • Evidence of domestic violence.
  • Other relevant factors.

Common Legal Proceedings in Delaware Child Custody Cases

In Delaware, child custody cases often involve the following legal proceedings:

  • Complaint for Custody: A parent files a complaint with the court seeking custody of the child.
  • Answer and Counterclaim: The other parent responds to the complaint and may file a counterclaim seeking custody.
  • Temporary Custody Orders: The court may issue temporary custody orders to govern the parties' relationship until a final custody determination is made.
  • Custody Evaluations: The court may appoint a custody evaluator to assess the parties' circumstances and make recommendations regarding custody.
  • Mediation: The parties may participate in mediation to attempt to reach a settlement on custody issues.
  • Trial: If the parties are unable to reach a settlement, the case will proceed to trial, where the court will make a final custody determination.

Expected Attorney Costs in Delaware Child Custody Cases

The cost of hiring a Family Law attorney in Delaware can vary significantly depending on the complexity of the case, the attorney's experience, and the location. On average, Delaware Family Law attorneys charge between $250 and $500 per hour. In a typical child custody case, the total attorney fees can range from $5,000 to $20,000 or more.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Parents Seeking Child Custody in Delaware

To navigate the child custody process in Delaware, parents should follow these steps:

  1. Consult with a Family Law attorney to discuss your options and develop a strategy for your case.
  2. Gather relevant documents, including financial records, medical records, and any other information that may be relevant to your case.
  3. File a complaint for custody with the court, if necessary.
  4. Respond to any counterclaims or other filings made by the other parent.
  5. Participate in temporary custody proceedings, if necessary.
  6. Cooperate with any custody evaluations or other investigations conducted by the court.
  7. Attend mediation, if ordered by the court or agreed upon by the parties.
  8. Prepare for trial, if a settlement cannot be reached.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) About Delaware Child Custody

Below are some common questions and answers about child custody in Delaware:

  • Q: What is the primary consideration in Delaware child custody cases?
  • A: The best interests of the child.
  • Q: Can I represent myself in a Delaware child custody case?
  • A: While it is possible to represent yourself, it is highly recommended that you hire an experienced Family Law attorney to ensure your rights are protected.
  • Q: How long does a typical Delaware child custody case take to resolve?
  • A: The length of time it takes to resolve a child custody case in Delaware can vary significantly, but most cases take several months to a year or more to complete.
  • Q: Can I modify a existing child custody order in Delaware?
  • A: Yes, you can modify a existing child custody order in Delaware, but you will need to demonstrate a significant change in circumstances and show that the modification is in the best interests of the child.
