Arkansas Child Custody Statutes

The state of Arkansas has specific statutes governing child custody, which are outlined in the Arkansas Code Annotated (ACA) § 9-13-101 et seq. According to ACA § 9-13-101, the court's primary consideration in determining child custody is the best interests of the child. The court will consider factors such as the child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs, as well as the parents' ability to provide a stable and loving environment.

Types of Child Custody in Arkansas

There are several types of child custody arrangements that can be awarded in Arkansas, including:

  • Joint custody: Both parents share decision-making responsibilities and physical custody of the child.
  • Sole custody: One parent has primary physical and decision-making responsibility for the child.
  • Primary residence: The child resides with one parent, while the other parent has visitation rights.
  • Split custody: Each parent has primary physical custody of one or more children.

Common Legal Proceedings in Arkansas Child Custody Cases

Child custody cases in Arkansas typically involve the following legal proceedings:

  1. Filing a petition: One parent files a petition with the court, requesting child custody and outlining the proposed custody arrangement.
  2. Temporary orders: The court may issue temporary orders, which can include temporary custody arrangements, child support, and other provisions.
  3. Discovery: Both parties engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and evidence related to the case.
  4. Mediation: The court may require the parties to attend mediation, in an attempt to reach a mutually agreeable custody arrangement.
  5. Trials: If the parties are unable to reach an agreement, the case will proceed to trial, where the court will make a determination based on the evidence presented.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Navigating Arkansas Child Custody Cases

To navigate an Arkansas child custody case, follow these steps:

  1. Consult with a Family Law attorney: Schedule an initial consultation with an experienced Family Law attorney to discuss your case and determine the best course of action.
  2. Gather evidence: Collect relevant evidence, such as financial records, witness statements, and documentation of your involvement in your child's life.
  3. File a petition: Work with your attorney to file a petition with the court, requesting child custody and outlining the proposed custody arrangement.
  4. Attend temporary orders hearing: Attend a hearing to determine temporary orders, which can include temporary custody arrangements and child support.
  5. Engage in discovery: Participate in discovery, exchanging information and evidence with the other party.
  6. Attend mediation: Attend mediation, if required by the court, to attempt to reach a mutually agreeable custody arrangement.
  7. Prepare for trial: If the case proceeds to trial, work with your attorney to prepare evidence and testimony.
  8. Attend trial: Attend the trial, where the court will make a determination based on the evidence presented.

Frequently Asked Questions about Arkansas Child Custody Cases

Here are some frequently asked questions about Arkansas child custody cases:

  • Q: What is the primary consideration in determining child custody in Arkansas?
  • A: The primary consideration is the best interests of the child.
  • Q: Can I modify a child custody order in Arkansas?
  • A: Yes, child custody orders can be modified if there has been a substantial change in circumstances since the original order was issued.
  • Q: Do I need to hire a Family Law attorney for a child custody case in Arkansas?
  • A: While it is not required, hiring an experienced Family Law attorney can significantly improve your chances of achieving a favorable outcome.
  • Q: How long does a child custody case typically take in Arkansas?
  • A: The length of a child custody case can vary significantly, depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. On average, a case can take several months to a year or more to resolve.
  • Q: Can I appeal a child custody decision in Arkansas?
  • A: Yes, child custody decisions can be appealed, but the appeal must be filed within a specific timeframe, typically 30 days.
