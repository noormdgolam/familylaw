Rhode Island Family Law Attorney Child Custody Overview

Child custody disputes in Rhode Island can be emotionally and financially draining for all parties involved. A skilled family law attorney can guide you through the complex process and help you achieve a favorable outcome for your child's well-being.

Understanding Rhode Island Child Custody Laws

The state of Rhode Island follows a best-interest standard for determining child custody, which means the court's primary concern is the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being.

Rhode Island Statutes: Rhode Island General Laws (RIGL) Chapter 15-5, Section 1-4 provides a comprehensive framework for child custody proceedings.

According to RIGL 15-5-3, the court shall consider the following factors when determining child custody:

  • Routine care and supervision of the child
  • Parent-child relationship
  • Ability of each parent to cooperate and provide a stable home environment
  • Ability of each parent to provide for the child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs
  • Ability of each parent to maintain a consistent relationship with the child
  • Child's age, sex, and needs
  • Child's physical and mental health
  • Child's educational needs
  • Child's needs for stability and continuity
  • History of domestic violence between the parents or the parents' history of substance abuse
  • Ability of each parent to provide a safe and stable home environment
  • Ability of each parent to facilitate a positive relationship between the child and the other parent
  • Any other factor relevant to a particular child custody case

The court may also consider the following factors when determining child custody:

  • Parent's ability to communicate and cooperate with the other parent
  • Parent's ability to provide a stable and consistent home environment
  • Parent's ability to provide for the child's emotional and psychological needs
  • Parent's ability to maintain a positive relationship with the child
  • Parent's ability to provide a safe and stable environment for the child
  • Parent's history of substance abuse or domestic violence

Types of Child Custody Arrangements in Rhode Island:

  • Joint custody: Both parents share physical and legal custody of the child
  • Shared custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child
  • Primary custody: One parent has primary physical custody of the child
  • Visitation: One parent has visitation rights, but not primary physical custody

Common Child Custody Proceedings in Rhode Island

Child custody disputes in Rhode Island typically involve the following proceedings:

Filing a Petition: The parent seeking child custody files a petition with the court, which includes a detailed explanation of the child's needs and a proposed custody arrangement.

Temporary Custody Orders: The court may issue a temporary custody order to provide stability and protection for the child until the final custody determination is made.

Custody Hearings: The court holds a custody hearing to determine the best interests of the child and make a final custody determination.

Mediation and Negotiation: Parents may engage in mediation or negotiation to reach a mutually agreeable custody arrangement.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Parents in Rhode Island

Here's a step-by-step guide for parents in Rhode Island seeking child custody:

Step 1: File a Petition: File a petition with the court, which includes a detailed explanation of the child's needs and a proposed custody arrangement.

Step 2: Complete Financial Disclosures: Complete financial disclosures with the court, which includes providing financial information about your income, expenses, and assets.

Step 3: Complete a Custody Evaluation: Complete a custody evaluation with a court-appointed evaluator, which includes a thorough assessment of your ability to provide a stable and safe home environment for the child.

Step 4: Attend a Custody Hearing: Attend a custody hearing with the court, where the judge will make a final custody determination based on the best interests of the child.

Step 5: Negotiate a Custody Agreement: Negotiate a custody agreement with the other parent, which includes a mutually agreeable custody arrangement and visitation schedule.
