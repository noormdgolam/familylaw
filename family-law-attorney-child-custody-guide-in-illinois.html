Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in Illinois

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Understanding Illinois Child Custody Laws

The IMDMA emphasizes the importance of the "best interests of the child" standard in determining child custody. This standard considers various factors, including the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being, as well as the ability of each parent to provide a stable and loving environment. Illinois law also recognizes two types of custody: joint custody and sole custody. Joint custody allows both parents to share decision-making responsibilities, while sole custody grants one parent exclusive decision-making authority.

Illinois Child Custody Statutes

The IMDMA provides specific guidelines for determining child custody in Illinois. According to 750 ILCS 5/601, the court shall consider the following factors when determining child custody:

  • The wishes of the child, if the child is mature enough to express a preference
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved
  • The ability of each parent to cooperate and communicate with each other
  • The level of involvement of each parent in the child's life
  • Any history of domestic violence or abuse
  • The distance between the parents' residences and the potential impact on the child's relationship with each parent

Common Legal Proceedings in Illinois Child Custody Cases

In Illinois, child custody cases typically involve the following legal proceedings:

  1. Filing a petition for dissolution of marriage or a petition for allocation of parental responsibilities
  2. Service of process and response by the opposing party
  3. Discovery and exchange of financial and other relevant information
  4. Mediation or other forms of alternative dispute resolution
  5. Trials or hearings to determine child custody and other issues

Step-by-Step Guidance for Illinois Child Custody Cases

If you are involved in a child custody dispute in Illinois, it is essential to understand the steps involved in the process. Here is a step-by-step guide to help you navigate the system:

  1. Consult with an attorney: Schedule a consultation with a experienced Family Law attorney to discuss your case and determine the best course of action.
  2. Gather relevant documents: Collect financial records, medical records, and other relevant documents that may be used as evidence in your case.
  3. File a petition: File a petition for dissolution of marriage or a petition for allocation of parental responsibilities with the court.
  4. Serve the opposing party: Serve the opposing party with the petition and any other relevant documents.
  5. Respond to the petition: If you are the responding party, file a response to the petition and any other relevant documents.
  6. Engage in discovery: Exchange financial and other relevant information with the opposing party.
  7. Participate in mediation or alternative dispute resolution: Attend mediation or other forms of alternative dispute resolution to attempt to resolve the case without going to trial.
  8. Prepare for trial: If mediation is unsuccessful, prepare for trial by gathering evidence, interviewing witnesses, and developing a trial strategy.
  9. Attend trial: Attend the trial and present your case to the judge.
  10. Appeal the decision: If you are unhappy with the decision, consider appealing the decision to a higher court.
