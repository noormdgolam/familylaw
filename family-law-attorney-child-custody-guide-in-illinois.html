Introduction to Child Custody in Illinois

In Illinois, child custody is governed by the Illinois Marriage and Dissolution of Marriage Act (IMDMA), which outlines the procedures and guidelines for determining child custody. The primary consideration in child custody cases is the best interests of the child, and the court will consider various factors to determine what arrangement is in the child's best interests.

Types of Child Custody in Illinois

There are two main types of child custody in Illinois: joint custody and sole custody. Joint custody, also known as shared custody, is an arrangement where both parents have significant decision-making authority and parenting time. Sole custody, on the other hand, is an arrangement where one parent has primary decision-making authority and parenting time, while the other parent may have visitation rights.

Factors Considered in Child Custody Cases

In determining child custody, the court will consider the following factors:

  • The wishes of the parents
  • The wishes of the child, if the child is mature enough to express a preference
  • The child's relationship with each parent and other significant individuals in the child's life
  • The child's adjustment to their home, school, and community
  • The mental and physical health of each parent
  • The ability of each parent to cooperate and communicate with the other parent
  • Any history of domestic violence or abuse
  • The geographic proximity of the parents' residences

Common Legal Proceedings in Child Custody Cases

Child custody cases in Illinois typically involve the following legal proceedings:

  1. Filing a petition for dissolution of marriage or a petition for allocation of parental responsibilities
  2. Service of process on the other parent
  3. Temporary orders, such as temporary custody or visitation orders
  4. Discovery, including the exchange of financial and other relevant information
  5. Mediation or other forms of alternative dispute resolution
  6. Pre-trial conferences and settlement negotiations
  7. Trial, if a settlement is not reached

Step-by-Step Guidance for Child Custody Cases

If you are involved in a child custody case in Illinois, the following step-by-step guidance may be helpful:

  1. Consult with a Family Law attorney to discuss your options and determine the best course of action
  2. Gather relevant documents, including financial information, medical records, and other evidence that may be relevant to the case
  3. Develop a parenting plan, including a proposed custody arrangement and visitation schedule
  4. File a petition for dissolution of marriage or a petition for allocation of parental responsibilities
  5. Serve the other parent with the petition and any other relevant documents
  6. Participate in temporary orders, discovery, mediation, and other pre-trial proceedings
  7. Prepare for trial, if a settlement is not reached

Modification of Child Custody Orders

If circumstances change after a child custody order has been entered, it may be possible to modify the order. The following grounds for modification may be considered:

  • A significant change in circumstances, such as a change in employment or residence
  • A change in the child's needs or circumstances
  • A failure to comply with the existing custody order

Frequently Asked Questions

The following are some frequently asked questions about child custody in Illinois:

What is the difference between joint custody and sole custody?
Joint custody is an arrangement where both parents have significant decision-making authority and parenting time, while sole custody is an arrangement where one parent has primary decision-making authority and parenting time.
How is child custody determined in Illinois?
Child custody is determined based on the best interests of the child, considering factors such as the wishes of the parents, the child's relationship with each parent, and the child's adjustment to their home, school, and community.
Can I modify a child custody order?
Yes, it may be possible to modify a child custody order if circumstances change after the order has been entered.
How much does it cost to hire a Family Law attorney for a child custody case?
The cost of hiring a Family Law attorney can vary widely, depending on the complexity of the case and the attorney's experience and reputation.
Do I need to hire a Family Law attorney for a child custody case?
While it is possible to represent yourself in a child custody case, hiring a Family Law attorney can provide valuable guidance and representation throughout the process.
