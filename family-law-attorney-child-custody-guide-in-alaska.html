Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in Alaska

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Child custody in Alaska is governed by Alaska Statutes Title 25, Sections 25.24.010-25.24.180, which aim to provide a framework for decision-making regarding the care and well-being of children in divorce, separation, or other family disputes.

Types of Child Custody in Alaska

In Alaska, there are two primary types of child custody: physical custody and legal custody.

Physical Custody

Physical custody refers to the actual day-to-day care and control of a child. In Alaska, physical custody can be either sole or joint. Sole physical custody means that one parent has the primary physical custody of the child, while joint physical custody means that both parents share the physical care and control of the child.

Legal Custody

Legal custody refers to the decision-making authority regarding a child's health, education, welfare, and other important aspects of their life. In Alaska, legal custody can also be either sole or joint.

Factors Considered in Determining Child Custody in Alaska

When determining child custody in Alaska, the court considers several factors, including:

  • Best interests of the child: The court's primary concern is the well-being and best interests of the child. This includes considering the child's age, health, emotional needs, and other relevant factors.
  • Caregiving capacity of each parent: The court evaluates each parent's ability to provide a stable, loving, and nurturing environment for the child.
  • Parent-child relationship: The court considers the nature and quality of the relationship between the child and each parent.
  • Geographic location: The court may consider the distance between the parents' residences and how it affects the child's care and well-being.
  • Work schedule and availability: The court may consider the parents' work schedules and availability to care for the child.
  • Child's wishes (in older children): In older children, the court may consider their wishes regarding custody arrangements.
  • History of domestic violence: The court may consider a history of domestic violence or other forms of abuse in determining custody arrangements.
  • Substance abuse: The court may consider a parent's history of substance abuse in determining custody arrangements.

Alaska Statutes Relevant to Child Custody

The following Alaska statutes are relevant to child custody:

Section Summary
AS 25.24.010 Definitions: "Child custody" and "visitation" are defined.
AS 25.24.020 Best Interests of the Child: The court must consider the best interests of the child in determining custody arrangements.
AS 25.24.030 Sole or Joint Custody: The court may grant sole or joint custody, depending on the circumstances.
AS 25.24.040 Residency Requirements: The court may consider a parent's residency requirements in determining custody arrangements.
AS 25.24.050 Domestic Violence: The court may consider a history of domestic violence in determining custody arrangements.

Common Legal Proceedings in Alaska Child Custody Cases

Common legal proceedings in Alaska child custody cases include:

  • Initial Petition: A parent files a petition for divorce, separation, or custody arrangements.
  • Custody Hearing: The court holds a hearing to determine the best interests of the child and grant custody arrangements.
  • Discovery: Both parties may engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and documents related to the case.
  • Mediation: Both parties may participate in mediation to resolve disputes and reach an agreement on custody arrangements.
  • Contested Hearing: If an agreement cannot be reached, the case may proceed to a contested hearing, where the court will make a final decision on custody arrangements.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Preparing for a Child Custody Case in Alaska

To prepare for a child custody case in Alaska, follow these steps:

  1. Gather Documents: Collect relevant documents, including birth certificates, medical records, and financial statements.
  2. Assess Your Situation: Evaluate your living situation, work schedule, and ability to care for the child.
  3. Consult an Attorney: Meet with an experienced family law attorney to discuss your options and create a strategy.
  4. Prepare for Court: Gather evidence, including witness statements and expert opinions, to support your case.
  5. Attend Court Hearings: Be prepared to present your case and respond to questions from the court.

FAQs About Child Custody in Alaska

Here are some frequently asked questions about child custody in Alaska:

  • Can I change the custody arrangement after it is granted? Yes, you can request a modification to the custody arrangement if there has been a significant change in circumstances.
  • How long does a custody case take to resolve? The length of a custody case can vary depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule.
  • Can I represent myself in a custody case? Yes, you can represent yourself, but it is highly recommended to hire an experienced family law attorney to ensure the best possible outcome.
  • What is the difference between sole and joint custody? Sole custody means one parent has primary physical custody and decision-making authority, while joint custody means both parents share physical custody and decision-making authority.

By understanding the laws and procedures surrounding child custody in Alaska, you can make informed decisions and advocate for the best interests of your child.
