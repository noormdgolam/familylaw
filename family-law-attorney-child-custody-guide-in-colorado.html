Introduction to Child Custody in Colorado

Child custody is a legal term that refers to the rights and responsibilities of parents or guardians towards their minor children. In Colorado, child custody is governed by the Colorado Revised Statutes (C.R.S.) Title 14, Article 10, which outlines the procedures and guidelines for determining child custody. The primary consideration in child custody cases is the best interests of the child, and the court's decision is based on various factors, including the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being.

Types of Child Custody in Colorado

There are several types of child custody in Colorado, including:

  • Joint Custody: Both parents share decision-making responsibility and physical custody of the child.
  • Sole Custody: One parent has primary decision-making responsibility and physical custody of the child.
  • Split Custody: Each parent has primary physical custody of one or more children.
  • Shared Custody: Both parents share physical custody of the child, but one parent may have primary decision-making responsibility.

Colorado Child Custody Statutes

The Colorado Revised Statutes (C.R.S.) Title 14, Article 10, outlines the procedures and guidelines for determining child custody. Some key statutes include:

  • C.R.S. § 14-10-124: Best Interests of the Child - The court shall determine custody based on the best interests of the child, considering factors such as the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being.
  • C.R.S. § 14-10-124(1.5): Parental Responsibility - The court shall consider the ability of each parent to cooperate and make decisions that are in the best interests of the child.
  • C.R.S. § 14-10-124(2): Domestic Violence - The court shall consider any history of domestic violence when determining custody.

Common Legal Proceedings in Child Custody Cases

Child custody cases in Colorado typically involve the following legal proceedings:

  1. Filing a petition: One parent files a petition with the court to establish or modify child custody.
  2. Temporary orders: The court may issue temporary orders to establish a temporary custody arrangement while the case is pending.
  3. Discovery: Both parties exchange information and evidence, such as financial records, witness statements, and expert opinions.
  4. Mediation: The parties may participate in mediation to try to reach a settlement agreement.
  5. Trial: If a settlement agreement cannot be reached, the case will proceed to trial, where the court will make a final determination on child custody.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Child Custody Cases in Colorado

To navigate a child custody case in Colorado, follow these steps:

  1. Consult with a Family Law attorney to discuss your case and determine the best course of action.
  2. Gather all relevant documents, including financial records, witness statements, and expert opinions.
  3. File a petition with the court to establish or modify child custody.
  4. Participate in mediation to try to reach a settlement agreement.
  5. Prepare for trial by gathering evidence, interviewing witnesses, and developing a trial strategy.
  6. Attend trial and present your case to the court.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) about Child Custody in Colorado

Here are some frequently asked questions about child custody in Colorado:

  • Q: What is the best interests of the child standard in Colorado?
  • A: The best interests of the child standard requires the court to consider the child's physical, emotional, and psychological well-being when determining custody.
  • Q: Can I modify a child custody order in Colorado?
  • A: Yes, a child custody order can be modified in Colorado if there has been a significant change in circumstances, such as a change in employment or residence.
  • Q: How long does a child custody case take in Colorado?
  • A: The length of a child custody case in Colorado can vary, but it typically takes several months to a year or more to resolve.
  • Q: Can I represent myself in a child custody case in Colorado?
  • A: While it is possible to represent yourself in a child custody case, it is highly recommended that you hire a Family Law attorney to ensure the best possible outcome for your family.
