Family Law Attorney Child Custody Guide in Colorado

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Family law attorney child custody in Colorado involves a complex set of laws and procedures designed to protect the rights of children and parents in cases of divorce, separation, or other family disputes. As a highly experienced family law attorney, I will provide a comprehensive overview of the child custody laws in Colorado, including state-specific statutes, common legal proceedings, expected attorney costs, and step-by-step guidance.

Understanding Colorado Child Custody Laws

Colorado Revised Statutes (C.R.S.) govern child custody cases in the state. The primary goal of child custody laws is to ensure the best interests of the child are protected. In determining the best interests of the child, the court considers several factors, including:

  • The wishes of the child, if he or she is old enough to express a preference.
  • The child's relationship with each parent and other significant adults in his or her life.
  • The child's adjustment to his or her home, school, and community.
  • The mental and physical health of all individuals involved.
  • The child's needs for a stable and loving environment.
  • The willingness and ability of each parent to cooperate with the other.

Types of Custody in Colorado

In Colorado, there are two primary types of custody: legal custody and physical custody.

Legal Custody

Legal custody refers to the right to make decisions about a child's life, such as education, health care, and extracurricular activities. In Colorado, the court may award one parent sole legal custody or joint legal custody. Joint legal custody means that both parents share decision-making responsibilities.

Physical Custody

Physical custody refers to the time a child spends with each parent. The court may award one parent sole physical custody or joint physical custody. Joint physical custody means that both parents share physical custody of the child, with the child spending a significant amount of time with each parent.

Child Custody Proceedings in Colorado

In Colorado, child custody proceedings typically involve the following steps:

  1. Filing a Petition: The parent seeking custody files a petition with the court, usually accompanied by a summons and a complaint. The petition must include the child's name, date of birth, and the parents' names and addresses.
  2. Serving the Other Parent: The summons is served on the other parent, informing them of the court action and requiring them to respond.
  3. Discovery: Both parents engage in discovery, which involves exchanging information and evidence related to the child custody case. This may include financial information, medical records, and witness statements.
  4. Motions and Hearings: The court may schedule motions and hearings to address specific issues or disputes. These may include motions for temporary custody, visitation, or other relief.
  5. Mediation: In some cases, the court may order mediation to help the parents reach a settlement. Mediation involves a neutral third-party facilitator who helps the parents negotiate a mutually acceptable agreement.
  6. Trial: If the parties cannot reach an agreement, the case may proceed to trial. At trial, each side presents evidence and testimony to support their position.
  7. Judgment: The court renders a judgment, which may include an order for custody, visitation, and support.

Step-by-Step Guidance for Colorado Child Custody Cases

If you are facing a child custody dispute in Colorado, here are some steps you can take to protect your rights:

  1. Seek professional help: Consult with an experienced family law attorney who can guide you through the process and protect your rights.
  2. Gather evidence: Collect relevant documents and information, such as financial records, medical records, and witness statements.
  3. Communicate with the other parent: Try to resolve issues amicably, but be prepared to litigate if necessary.
  4. Attend court hearings: Participate in court hearings and mediations to advocate for your position.
  5. Be prepared for trial: If the case proceeds to trial, be prepared to present evidence and testimony to support your position.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Here are some frequently asked questions about Colorado child custody laws:

  1. Q: How long does a child custody case take in Colorado?

    The length of a child custody case in Colorado can vary widely depending on the complexity of the case and the court's schedule. Some cases may be resolved within a few months, while others may take several years.

  2. Q: Can I represent myself in a child custody case?

    While it is possible to represent yourself in a child custody case, it is not recommended. Child custody laws are complex, and a qualified attorney can help ensure your rights are protected.

  3. Q: Can I move out of state with my child if I have custody?

    In Colorado, you must obtain court permission before relocating your child to another state. The court will consider factors such as the child's best interests, the reason for relocation, and the impact on the other parent.

  4. Q: Can I change my custody arrangement after the divorce is finalized?

    Yes, you can request a modification of your custody arrangement after the divorce is finalized. However, you must demonstrate a significant change in circumstances that warrants a change in custody.
